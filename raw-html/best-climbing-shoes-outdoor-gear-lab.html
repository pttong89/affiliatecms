Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a climbing shoe the best according to outdoor gear lab testing?

The best climbing shoes outdoor gear lab tested excel in five key metrics: comfort for long sessions, edging precision on small holds, smearing capability on smooth surfaces, pulling power on overhanging terrain, and crack climbing performance. Top-rated shoes balance these attributes while maintaining durability and fit consistency across different foot shapes and climbing styles. Laboratory testing includes rubber hardness analysis, sole flexibility measurements, and wear pattern studies, but real-world performance across granite, limestone, and sandstone ultimately determines rankings.

How should beginners choose their first climbing shoes?

Beginners should prioritize comfort and durability over aggressive performance features. Look for shoes with flat profiles, thick rubber soles (4-5mm), and generous toe boxes. The La Sportiva Tarantulace and Finale are excellent starter options that provide good performance without painful break-in periods. Size them snug but not tight, allowing for slight toe curl without pain. Focus on developing proper footwork technique before transitioning to more aggressive, performance-oriented models. Avoid the common mistake of buying overly tight shoes that hinder learning and enjoyment.

What's the difference between aggressive and moderate climbing shoes?

Aggressive climbing shoes feature pronounced downturn and asymmetry, concentrating power on the big toe for steep overhanging routes and precise edging. Moderate shoes have slight downturn with balanced comfort and performance, suitable for varied terrain. Aggressive shoes excel in bouldering and sport climbing where maximum precision and hooking capability are crucial, while moderate shoes work better for trad climbing, multi-pitch routes, and all-day comfort. The choice depends on your climbing style, foot shape, and tolerance for discomfort during extended sessions.

How often should climbing shoes be resoled?

Climbing shoes should be resoled when the rubber wears down to 1-2mm thickness or when holes appear near the toe. High-quality shoes like La Sportiva Katana Lace and TC Pro can be resoled 2-3 times before the upper wears out. Professional resoling costs $40-80 but extends shoe life significantly, making it worthwhile for expensive performance models. Monitor wear patterns regularly and resole before the rand (rubber around the shoe) is compromised. Proper footwork technique reduces unnecessary wear and extends time between resoles.

Which climbing shoes are best for outdoor crack climbing?

The La Sportiva TC Pro dominates crack climbing with its stiff platform, high ankle protection, and narrow toe profile for thin cracks. The Scarpa Generator Mid offers similar crack performance with wider foot accommodation. Both feature durable leather uppers and protective padding around pressure points. Flat profiles and lace closures provide the comfort and adjustability needed for long trad routes. Avoid aggressive shoes for crack climbing as they cause unnecessary pain and reduce jamming efficiency. Consider sizing up slightly for comfort during extended crack climbing sessions.
