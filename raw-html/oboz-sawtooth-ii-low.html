Technical Features & Construction

O FIT Insole Technology

The proprietary O FIT insole is perhaps the most distinctive feature of every Oboz shoe. Molded to match the specific shape and construction of each model, this insole provides exceptional arch support and heel stability that I've rarely experienced in other hiking shoes.

Dual-Density EVA Midsole

The dual-density construction combines softer EVA for cushioning with firmer EVA for support. This creates a balanced platform that absorbs impact while maintaining stability on uneven terrain. The nylon shank adds torsional rigidity without excessive weight.

Leather & Mesh Upper

The combination of durable leather overlays and breathable mesh panels strikes an excellent balance between protection and ventilation. The leather provides durability in high-wear areas, while mesh allows for airflow during extended hikes.

Sawtooth Outsole

The proprietary rubber outsole features a lugged pattern designed for versatile traction. While not as aggressive as some technical hiking boots, it performs admirably on most trail surfaces, though it can struggle with mud retention.

The construction quality of the Oboz Sawtooth II Low immediately impresses upon unboxing. The stitching is consistent and robust, with no loose threads or irregular seams visible even after months of use. The toe bumper, while prone to minor delamination issues in some units (as acknowledged by Oboz's warranty department), provides essential protection during rock scrambles and root encounters.

One technical aspect that deserves special attention is the asymmetrical collar design. This feature, updated in the second generation, provides a more comfortable fit around the ankle while preventing debris from entering the shoe. The padded tongue stays in position well, though I recommend the Oboz Sawtooth II Mid for those seeking additional ankle support on challenging terrain.

The waterproof B-DRY membrane variant adds weather protection without significantly compromising breathability. However, for most three-season hiking, I prefer the non-waterproof version for superior ventilation and faster drying times. This preference becomes particularly apparent during summer hikes in humid conditions where breathability outweighs waterproof protection.

During my second major test – a three-day backpacking trip through the North Cascades – the shoes' comfort consistency became even more apparent. Day after day, mile after mile, my feet remained comfortable and blister-free. Care & Maintenance Tips

Daily Care Routine

Proper maintenance significantly extends the lifespan of the Oboz Sawtooth II Low while maintaining optimal performance. After each hike, I recommend removing excess dirt and debris using a soft brush or cloth. The leather portions benefit from occasional conditioning with a quality leather treatment, though avoid over-conditioning which can soften the leather excessively.

The mesh panels require gentle cleaning with mild soap and water, allowing complete air drying before storage. Never machine wash or dry these shoes, as the heat can damage both the leather and the adhesives bonding the various components together. The removable O FIT insoles can be hand-washed separately and air-dried for hygiene maintenance.

Long-term Storage and Rotation

For hikers who use their Sawtooth II Low shoes regularly, I strongly recommend having two pairs in rotation. This practice allows each pair to fully dry between uses, significantly extending the life of both the materials and the foot-molding characteristics of the O FIT insole. Store shoes in a well-ventilated area away from direct heat sources.

Seasonal storage requires special attention to moisture control. Stuff shoes with newspaper or cedar shoe trees to maintain shape, and store in a breathable bag rather than plastic containers. The natural materials in these shoes need airflow to prevent mold and odor development during extended storage periods.

Resoling and Repair Options

Unfortunately, the construction method of the Oboz Sawtooth II Low doesn't allow for traditional resoling, a common limitation among modern hiking shoes. However, the durable construction typically provides 400-800 miles of hiking depending on terrain and usage patterns. Signs that replacement is needed include significant outsole wear, midsole compression, or upper material degradation.

Minor repairs like eyelet replacement or small leather tears can extend shoe life. Oboz's warranty covers manufacturing defects within the first year, and their customer service team provides helpful guidance on repair versus replacement decisions. For environmental responsibility, consider Oboz's recycling programs when shoes reach end-of-life status.

Maintenance Schedule

  • After each hike: Remove debris, check for damage, air dry completely
  • Weekly (heavy use): Clean mesh panels, brush leather sections
  • Monthly: Condition leather, wash insoles if needed
  • Seasonally: Deep clean, inspect for wear patterns, treat leather
  • Annually: Evaluate overall condition, consider replacement timing

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Oboz Sawtooth II Low shoes good for wide feet?

The standard width Oboz Sawtooth II Low features a medium volume fit that may feel snug for hikers with wide feet. However, Oboz offers wide width options in many sizes that provide additional room through the forefoot while maintaining the heel security the model is known for. The generous toe box design accommodates foot swelling during long hikes better than many competitors. If you have wide feet, I recommend trying the wide version or considering a half-size up in the standard width, though this may compromise heel fit. The key is finding the balance between forefoot comfort and heel security.

How do Oboz Sawtooth II Low compare to Merrell Moab shoes?

The Oboz Sawtooth II Low and Merrell Moab series target similar hiking applications but differ significantly in construction and performance characteristics. The Sawtooth II Low provides superior arch support through the O FIT insole system, more durable construction materials, and better long-term shape retention. However, the Moab shoes typically weigh less and cost less initially. The Merrell shoes break in more quickly but may not maintain support characteristics as long. For hikers prioritizing long-term comfort and durability over weight savings, the Sawtooth II Low represents the better investment despite the higher initial cost.

Do I need the waterproof version for most hiking?

For most three-season hiking conditions, the standard non-waterproof Oboz Sawtooth II Low provides better overall performance than the B-DRY waterproof version. The breathable construction dries faster when wet, provides superior ventilation during warm weather hiking, and costs less initially. The waterproof version makes sense for consistently wet climates, spring hiking with stream crossings, or winter conditions where keeping feet dry is crucial. However, remember that waterproof shoes also trap moisture from internal perspiration, potentially leading to longer wet conditions once moisture enters. Consider your typical hiking conditions and prioritize breathability unless you frequently encounter extended wet conditions.

What is the expected lifespan of these hiking shoes?

The lifespan of Oboz Sawtooth II Low shoes depends heavily on usage patterns, terrain types, and care practices. Based on user reports and my own testing, expect 400-800 miles of hiking from a pair, with most hikers getting 500-600 miles before significant performance degradation. Hikers who primarily use paved trails or smooth dirt paths may exceed 800 miles, while those tackling rocky, technical terrain might see replacement needed around 400 miles. The O FIT insole typically maintains support characteristics throughout the shoe's life, unlike generic foam insoles that compress and lose effectiveness. Proper rotation between pairs, good maintenance practices, and appropriate usage for intended terrain all extend lifespan significantly.

Can these shoes handle technical hiking and scrambling?

The Oboz Sawtooth II Low performs adequately for light technical hiking and Class 2 scrambling but isn't designed for serious technical applications. The low-cut design provides less ankle support than mid or high-cut boots, making them best suited for well-maintained trails and moderate terrain. The outsole provides good traction on dry rock but struggles with wet surfaces and steep, loose terrain. For regular technical hiking or scrambling, consider the mid-cut version for additional ankle support or look at more technical-specific footwear. However, for the vast majority of hiking applications including day hikes, light backpacking, and moderate terrain navigation, these shoes excel and provide the versatility most hikers need.

Are replacement insoles available for long-term use?

Yes, Oboz sells replacement O FIT insoles separately, which is a significant advantage for long-term users of the Sawtooth II Low. The proprietary insoles are specifically molded for each shoe model, ensuring proper fit and support characteristics when replacements are needed. This availability extends the useful life of the shoes significantly since the insole often shows wear before the shoe structure itself needs replacement. The replacement insoles cost approximately $30-40 and maintain the same arch support and comfort characteristics as the originals. This serviceability aspect adds value to the initial investment and supports the environmental benefits of extending shoe lifespan rather than premature replacement due to insole degradation.
