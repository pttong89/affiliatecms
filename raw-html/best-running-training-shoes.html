Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between running shoes and training shoes?

Running shoes are specifically designed for forward motion and the repetitive heel-to-toe movement of running. They feature specialized midsole foams, stack heights, and geometries optimized for running mechanics. Training shoes, conversely, are designed for multi-directional movement including lateral cuts, jumping, and lifting. Based on my testing, dedicated running shoes provide superior comfort and injury prevention for runners compared to cross-training shoes. However, some models like the best training sneakers can bridge both worlds for recreational athletes who mix running with gym workouts.

How often should I replace my running training shoes?

The general rule is 300-500 miles per pair, but this varies significantly based on factors like body weight, running surface, gait efficiency, and shoe construction. Through my testing, premium shoes like the Asics Novablast 5 and Nike Pegasus Plus typically last 400-500 miles with proper rotation. Budget models may show wear closer to 300 miles. I track mileage using a running app and look for signs of worn outsole rubber, compressed midsole foam (shoe feels "dead"), or uneven wear patterns. A good practice is to start breaking in your next pair when your current shoes reach 250-300 miles, allowing for a smooth transition.

Should I buy running shoes a half size larger?

This depends on the specific shoe model and your foot characteristics. During my extensive testing, I found that shoes like the Asics Novablast 5 run true to size, while others like the New Balance Rebel v5 may require a half-size up for some runners. The key is ensuring adequate toe room (about a thumb's width) while maintaining heel security. Your feet naturally swell during runs, especially on longer efforts, so there should be enough space to accommodate this expansion. I recommend trying on shoes later in the day when your feet are naturally more swollen, and always test with your preferred running socks. Many specialty running stores offer gait analysis and professional fitting services that can eliminate guesswork.

Do carbon plate running shoes really make you faster?

Yes, but with important caveats. Independent research shows that carbon-plated shoes can improve running economy by 2-4%, which translates to meaningful time improvements in races. During my marathon testing, the Nike Alphafly 3 helped me achieve a 4-minute personal best. However, the benefits are most pronounced at faster paces (sub-7:00 per mile) and longer distances. For recreational runners or those running slower than 8:00 per mile pace, the benefits may be minimal and may not justify the $250+ cost. Additionally, some runners find carbon shoes feel unstable or unnatural during training. My recommendation is to use carbon shoes primarily for racing and key workouts, while relying on traditional trainers for daily miles. What are the best running shoes for beginners?

Beginner runners should prioritize comfort, injury prevention, and versatility over speed. Based on my coaching experience with new runners, the Asics Novablast 5 is my top recommendation due to its forgiving ride, excellent cushioning, and ability to handle various paces as fitness improves. The Brooks Glycerin Max is another excellent choice for beginners who prefer maximum cushioning. Key features to look for include neutral support (unless you know you need stability), adequate heel cushioning, breathable upper materials, and a price point that allows for proper replacement frequency. Avoid racing shoes or minimalist options until you've built a solid aerobic base and proper running mechanics. Most importantly, beginners should focus on gradual mileage increases (10% rule) and listen to their bodies rather than pushing through pain.
