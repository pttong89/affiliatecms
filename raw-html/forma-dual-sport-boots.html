Waterproof Technology: Drytex vs OutDry Systems Explained

Understanding the waterproof technology in forma dual sport boots is crucial for making an informed purchase decision. Forma uses two primary waterproof systems across their boot lineup: Drytex (their proprietary membrane) and OutDry (a Colombian-developed technology they license). After testing both systems extensively, I can share detailed insights about their real-world performance.

Drytex Technology: My Primary Experience

My main Adventure boots feature Forma's Drytex waterproof membrane, which uses a three-layer construction approach. The membrane sits between the outer leather and inner lining, creating a barrier that prevents water ingress while allowing moisture vapor to escape. During my testing across various climates, I've found the Drytex system performs exceptionally well in moderate to heavy rain conditions.

The breathability of Drytex impressed me during summer rides through Arizona and Nevada. Even in 95°F+ temperatures, my feet never became uncomfortably sweaty, though I did notice some moisture buildup during the longest riding days (8+ hours). For most adventure riding scenarios, the breathability strikes an excellent balance between waterproofing and comfort.

Real-World Test Result: I deliberately rode through 6-inch deep creek crossings in Utah for 30+ crossings over two days. The Drytex membrane never allowed water penetration, and my feet remained completely dry throughout both days of riding.

OutDry Technology: Limited but Promising Experience

OutDry technology bonds the waterproof membrane directly to the boot's outer layer, eliminating the space where water can accumulate and potentially penetrate. I've tested OutDry-equipped Forma boots during shorter rides, and the technology shows promise, particularly for riders in consistently wet climates.

The advantage of OutDry is its immediate water repulsion—water beads and rolls off the boot surface rather than being absorbed into the outer material. This makes the boots dry faster after exposure to rain. However, I found the breathability slightly reduced compared to traditional Drytex construction, which became noticeable during hot weather riding.

For riders seeking maximum waterproof protection, particularly those in the Pacific Northwest or other consistently wet regions, OutDry-equipped boots might be worth the slight breathability trade-off. However, for most adventure riders, the standard Drytex system provides the best balance of protection and comfort.

Maintenance Tips for Long-Term Waterproof Performance

Maintaining the waterproof integrity of your dual sport motorcycle boots requires attention to both the membrane and the outer materials. I apply a leather conditioner every 3-4 months, focusing on the seams and high-flex areas where the waterproof barrier is most vulnerable.

The biggest threat to waterproof performance is abrasion damage to seams and the membrane itself. I've learned to avoid aggressive boot cleaning methods like power washing, which can force water through microscopic membrane damage. Instead, I use a soft brush and mild soap, allowing the boots to air dry completely between uses.

After 18 months of regular use, both my Drytex and OutDry boots maintain excellent waterproof performance. The key is treating them as technical gear rather than simple work boots—proper care extends their lifespan significantly while maintaining the performance that makes them worth the investment.

Sizing Guide and Fit Recommendations

Getting the correct size in forma dual sport boots is crucial for both comfort and safety. After helping several fellow riders with their sizing decisions and experiencing the consequences of poor fit firsthand, I can provide detailed guidance based on real-world experience rather than just manufacturer sizing charts.

My Personal Sizing Experience

I wear size 10.5 US in most athletic shoes and 10 US in work boots. For my Forma Adventure boots, I chose size 44 EU (10.5 US equivalent), and the fit is excellent with medium-weight riding socks. This represents true-to-size fitting, which aligns with most riders' experiences I've encountered.

However, I made an initial mistake by ordering size 43 EU for my first pair, thinking I could save weight and bulk with a smaller size. The result was uncomfortable pressure points during long rides and eventual numbness in my toes after 4+ hours. I exchanged for the larger size, and the difference in comfort was immediately apparent.

Size Up If You Have:

  • Wide feet (D+ width)
  • High instep
  • Preference for thick riding socks
  • Plans for winter riding
  • Previous issues with boot pressure

True-to-Size If You Have:

  • Average width feet (C-D width)
  • Normal instep height
  • Preference for snug fit
  • Primarily warm weather riding
  • Experience with European sizing

Width Considerations

Forma Adventure boots run slightly narrow compared to American work boot brands. If you typically need wide-width footwear, I strongly recommend going up a half-size rather than trying to stretch the leather. The waterproof membrane doesn't stretch, so forcing a narrow boot to accommodate a wide foot will likely compromise the waterproof seal over time.

For riders with particularly wide feet, consider looking at women's dual sport boots from other manufacturers that offer specific width options, or explore Forma's Terra series, which tends to run slightly wider than the Adventure line.

Break-in Period and Fit Evolution

The break-in period for dual sport adventure boots is significant—expect 200-300 miles before they feel truly comfortable. The full-grain leather needs time to soften and conform to your foot shape, while the internal padding compresses to create a custom fit.

Break-in Strategy: Start with short rides (30-60 minutes) for the first week, gradually increasing duration. Wear the boots around the house for short periods to accelerate the break-in process without the stress of riding.

Warning: Don't attempt a long adventure ride in new boots. I made this mistake on a 400-mile day with new boots and developed painful hot spots that took weeks to heal.

Sock Selection and Fit Impact

Sock choice significantly impacts boot fit and comfort. I use merino wool blend riding socks that provide cushioning without excessive bulk. Cotton socks should be avoided—they retain moisture, increase friction, and provide poor insulation when wet.

Sock thickness affects fit more than you might expect. The difference between thin athletic socks and thick hiking socks can change your effective boot size by nearly half a size. I keep both medium and thick riding socks so I can adjust for weather conditions and ensure optimal fit.

Size Comparison with Other Brands

Compared to other premium adventure boot brands, Forma sizing aligns closely with European standards. They run similar to Sidi (perhaps slightly narrower) and about half a size larger than Alpinestars. American work boot wearers typically need to go up half to a full size from their usual work boot size.

If you're transitioning from sport boots to forma dual sport boots, expect a different fit philosophy. Sport boots prioritize precision fit for performance, while adventure boots prioritize comfort and versatility over extended periods.
