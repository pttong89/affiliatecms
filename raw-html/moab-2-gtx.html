Moab 2 GTX Overview & First Impressions

Merrell Moab 2 GTX in popular Beluga colorway

The Moab 2 GTX represents Merrell's flagship waterproof hiking shoe, building upon the legendary reputation of the original Moab series. Standing for "Mother Of All Boots," this model has earned its place as America's #1 selling hiking shoe through a combination of accessible comfort, reliable waterproofing, and proven durability.

Key Specifications

  • Weight: 15.2 oz (431g) per shoe
  • Drop: 13.3mm heel-to-toe
  • Waterproofing: Gore-Tex membrane
  • Outsole: Vibram TC5+ rubber
  • Upper: Suede leather and mesh
  • Price Range: $89-145

Construction & Design Philosophy

The Moab 2 GTX strikes a careful balance between traditional hiking boot protection and modern trail runner agility. The suede leather and mesh upper provides durability while maintaining breathability, though the Gore-Tex liner does restrict airflow compared to non-waterproof versions. After extensive field testing, I've found this construction approach works particularly well for day hikers and weekend warriors who prioritize versatility over specialized performance.

Real-World Waterproof Testing

Over my extensive testing period, I subjected the Moab 2 GTX to numerous waterproof challenges across various seasons and conditions. From creek crossings in Colorado to Pacific Northwest rain hiking, these shoes consistently delivered reliable moisture protection. The Gore-Tex membrane effectively keeps external water out while allowing some interior moisture to escape, though this process is slower than advertised.

Field Test Results:

  • 4-hour rain hike: Feet remained completely dry
  • Stream crossings: No water penetration up to ankle height
  • Wet grass: Excellent protection for extended periods
  • Puddle submersion: Waterproof for depths below the collar
  • Snow conditions: Kept feet warm and dry in slushy terrain

The Gore-Tex construction in the Moab 2 GTX represents a mature technology that has been refined over decades. Unlike cheaper waterproof alternatives, the Gore-Tex membrane maintains its integrity over time and doesn't delaminate easily. However, this comes with the trade-off of reduced breathability, particularly noticeable during high-output activities or warm weather hiking.

Breathability Limitations

The most significant compromise in the Moab 2 GTX design is breathability. During my summer testing in Arizona and Utah, these shoes became uncomfortably warm during extended climbs. The Gore-Tex membrane, while excellent at blocking water, also restricts airflow significantly compared to the non-GTX versions. This limitation makes them less suitable for hot weather hiking or high-intensity activities where temperature regulation is crucial.

Sizing and Fit Considerations

The Moab 2 GTX generally runs true to size, but I recommend careful consideration of your specific foot shape and hiking sock preferences. The toe box is generously sized, accommodating wider feet comfortably, but the midfoot area can feel restrictive for some users. After helping numerous hikers with sizing decisions, I've observed that going up a half size often improves comfort, particularly for those planning to wear thick hiking socks.

Sizing Recommendations:

  • Narrow feet: True to size, consider narrow width if available
  • Average feet: True to size or up 0.5 for thick socks
  • Wide feet: Consider wide width version or up 0.5 size
  • High instep: May need to size up for comfortable fit
  • Long-distance hiking: Size up 0.5 to account for foot swelling

Long-Distance Comfort Performance

During my longest test hike—a 22-mile day in the Sierra Nevada—the Moab 2 GTX performed admirably for the first 15 miles. The cushioning remained effective, and I experienced no hot spots or blisters. However, towards the end of the day, the lack of lateral support became apparent on uneven terrain, and the heel began to slip slightly as my feet swelled. This performance profile makes them ideal for day hikes and weekend adventures but less suitable for multi-day backpacking where precise fit becomes crucial.

Vibram Sole Performance Analysis

The Vibram TC5+ outsole compound used in the Moab 2 GTX strikes an excellent balance between durability and grip. The lug pattern, while not as aggressive as dedicated technical hiking boots, provides reliable traction on most surfaces. I've tested these shoes on wet rock, loose scree, muddy trails, and snow-covered paths, finding consistent performance across diverse conditions.

Traction Test Results:

  • Dry rock: Excellent grip and confidence
  • Wet rock: Good performance with careful foot placement
  • Loose gravel: Adequate traction, some sliding on steep sections
  • Mud: Self-cleaning design works moderately well
  • Snow/ice: Limited traction, consider microspikes
  • Root-covered trails: Good stability and grip

Upper Durability & Material Quality

The suede leather and mesh upper construction of the Moab 2 GTX demonstrates solid durability characteristics. The synthetic leather overlays have resisted tearing and maintain their protective function even after extensive bush-whacking and rocky terrain encounters. The mesh panels show some expected pilling but remain structurally intact without holes or major degradation.

One area of concern is the heel counter, which has begun to show some softening after extended use. This isn't a structural failure, but it does reduce the support provided around the heel area. For hikers who prioritize long-term heel stability, this gradual breakdown may become noticeable after 400+ miles of use. Regular cleaning and proper storage have helped maintain the overall appearance and function of the materials.

Pro Tips from Experience:

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Moab 2 GTX truly waterproof?

Yes, the Moab 2 GTX features Gore-Tex waterproof membrane technology that effectively keeps water out while allowing some breathability. In my extensive field testing across various wet conditions, these shoes maintained waterproof protection for several hours of continuous exposure. However, like all Gore-Tex footwear, prolonged submersion or water above the ankle collar will eventually lead to moisture ingress. The waterproofing performs excellently for typical hiking conditions including rain, puddles, and wet grass.

How does the Moab 2 GTX compare to the newer Moab 3?

The Moab 3 offers several improvements over the Moab 2 GTX, including enhanced comfort with better cushioning, improved stability features, and a slightly updated fit. However, the Moab 2 GTX remains a solid choice with proven durability and often better pricing. The main differences include the Moab 3's upgraded midsole foam, refined upper construction, and better heel lock. For budget-conscious hikers, the Moab 2 GTX still delivers excellent value and performance that has been trail-tested by millions of users worldwide.

What size should I order for the Moab 2 GTX?

The Moab 2 GTX generally runs true to size, but I recommend going up a half size if you plan to wear thick hiking socks or have wider feet. The toe box is reasonably spacious, but the midfoot can feel snug initially. After testing multiple sizes, I found that sizing up slightly provides better comfort for long hikes and allows for natural foot swelling. Always try them on with your preferred hiking socks, and consider that the shoes require minimal break-in time compared to traditional hiking boots.

How long do Moab 2 GTX shoes typically last?

Based on my personal experience and community feedback, the Moab 2 GTX typically lasts 400-600 miles of hiking, depending on terrain and usage patterns. The Vibram outsole shows excellent wear resistance, while the upper materials hold up well to abrasion. In my testing over 18 months covering approximately 500 miles, the shoes showed moderate sole wear but remained structurally sound. Factors affecting longevity include hiking frequency, terrain roughness, proper care, and individual gait patterns. For occasional hikers, expect 2-3 years of reliable use.

Are Moab 2 GTX shoes good for wide feet?

The Moab 2 GTX accommodates wider feet reasonably well, with a generous toe box that allows natural toe movement. However, the midfoot area can feel restrictive for very wide feet. Merrell offers wide-width versions specifically designed for broader feet. From my experience helping fellow hikers with fit issues, I'd recommend trying both regular and wide versions if you typically wear wide-width shoes. The lacing system provides good adjustability, but extremely wide feet may find better options in brands like Keen or Altra that specialize in wider fits.
