Setting Up Your Ridge Outdoor Gear Hammock: A Step-by-Step Guide

Proper setup is crucial for both comfort and safety when using any Ridge Outdoor Gear hammock. Through trial and error across dozens of camping trips, I've developed a systematic approach that ensures consistent results regardless of campsite conditions. The following guide represents lessons learned from both successes and mistakes in various national park environments.

Step 1: Site Selection and Assessment

Begin by identifying two healthy trees spaced 12-15 feet apart. I prefer trees at least 6 inches in diameter to ensure adequate strength. Avoid dead, diseased, or leaning trees, as these pose safety risks. During my early camping days at Yellowstone, I learned this lesson the hard way when a seemingly sturdy pine showed signs of bark beetle damage only after setup.

Check for overhead hazards including dead branches, known as "widow makers" among experienced campers. Wind can dislodge these branches, creating dangerous situations. Ground conditions matter too â€“ while hammocks don't require level terrain, avoid areas with excessive slopes that might cause discomfort during entry and exit.

Step 2: Tree Protection and Strap Installation

Wrap your tree straps around each tree at approximately 7-8 feet high. This height allows for proper sag while keeping you off the ground when lying in the hammock. The straps should sit snugly against the bark without over-tightening, which can damage the tree's cambium layer. I always use 1-2 inch wide straps to distribute load effectively and minimize bark damage.

Leave No Trace: Always remove straps completely when breaking camp. Leaving straps can girdle growing trees and violate national park regulations. Consider portable hammock stands for areas with sensitive vegetation or limited tree availability.

Step 3: Hammock Attachment and Adjustment

Connect your Ridge Outdoor Gear hammock to the suspension system, aiming for approximately 30 degrees of angle from horizontal. This "banana curve" provides optimal comfort and reduces stress on both the hammock and anchor points. Too tight, and you'll feel like you're sleeping in a stretched drum. Too loose, and you'll sink uncomfortably low.

The structural ridgeline on Ridge hammocks helps maintain proper sag automatically, but fine-tuning remains important. I test the setup by carefully sitting in the center, then adjusting as needed. The bottom of the hammock should be 12-18 inches off the ground when occupied â€“ high enough to avoid ground contact but low enough for safe entry and exit.

Step 4: Bug Net Deployment and Final Checks

Deploy the mosquito net and check all zipper operations before settling in for the night. The 360-degree zipper on the Pinnacle 360 should operate smoothly â€“ if you encounter resistance, check for fabric bunching or debris. I keep a small piece of soap for zipper maintenance during extended trips.

Perform a final safety check by gently bouncing in the hammock to test all attachment points. This technique has prevented several potential failures over the years. Store your gear in the hammock's foot end or use a separate hiking hammock gear organization system to keep essentials within reach during the night.
