Trail Overview and Route Options

Two Routes to Paradise

The Stewart Falls Trail offers two distinct routes to reach this magnificent 200-foot two-tiered waterfall. The most popular and accessible route begins at the Aspen Grove Trailhead, covering 3.5-3.8 miles roundtrip with a moderate 652-foot elevation gain. This well-maintained trail takes you through diverse mountain terrain, offering spectacular views of Mount Timpanogos and the surrounding Wasatch peaks.

The alternative route starts from Sundance Resort, accessible via chairlift (seasonal) or hiking the Stewart Falls Lower Trail, which is 3.2 miles roundtrip with an 830-foot elevation gain. This route provides a different perspective and is exclusively for Sundance guests and resort visitors. Both trails converge at the falls, creating opportunities for experienced hikers to make a loop adventure.

What sets Stewart Falls apart from other waterfall hikes like Laurel Falls is its dramatic two-tier cascade that flows year-round, fed by snowmelt and mountain springs. The falls are particularly impressive during spring and early summer when snowmelt creates a thunderous display of natural power. Unlike some trails where you view waterfalls from a distance, Stewart Falls allows you to get remarkably close to the base, where the cooling mist provides relief after your mountain ascent.

Quick Trail Facts

  • • Distance: 3.5-3.8 miles roundtrip (Aspen Grove)
  • • Elevation Gain: 652 feet
  • • Difficulty: Moderate
  • • Time: 2-3 hours
  • • Dog Friendly: Yes, on leash

Getting There and Parking

Reaching Stewart Falls requires some planning, especially regarding fees and parking. From Salt Lake City, drive south on I-15 to US-189 through Provo Canyon. The journey offers stunning canyon views that serve as a preview of the natural beauty awaiting you. After approximately 7 miles up Provo Canyon, turn left onto Alpine Loop Road toward Sundance Resort.

Here's where preparation becomes crucial: you'll encounter a fee station approximately 5 miles up the Alpine Loop Road. The current fee is $10 for a 3-day pass (cash only), though your America the Beautiful National Parks Pass will grant you access. I learned this the hard way during my first visit when I arrived with only a credit card!

Parking Strategy

The Aspen Grove parking lot fills quickly, especially on weekends. Arrive before 8 AM during peak season (May-October) to secure parking. I've witnessed cars lined along the road for nearly a mile when the lot is full. The parking lot accommodates about 100 vehicles, but demand often exceeds capacity by 9 AM on Saturdays.

The trailhead facilities include pit toilets and trail information boards, but no water sources. The Dark Hollow Falls Trail offers similar amenities, but Stewart Falls provides better mountain vistas throughout the hike. GPS coordinates for the Aspen Grove Trailhead are 40.4043°N, -111.605°W, though cell service can be spotty in the mountains.

Pro tip from a local ranger: if the main lot is full, don't park along the road as you'll likely receive a citation. Instead, consider visiting midweek or starting your hike very early. The golden hour lighting on the falls makes an early start worthwhile for photographers and provides a more peaceful experience before the crowds arrive.

What to Expect on the Trail

The Stewart Falls Trail journey unfolds in distinct phases, each offering unique rewards. The first mile begins relatively flat through mixed coniferous forest, where towering pines provide welcome shade and the mountain air fills your lungs with that distinctive alpine freshness. You'll quickly understand why this trail attracts families with young children - the initial section feels more like a nature walk than a challenging hike.

Trail Highlights

  • Spectacular Mount Timpanogos views
  • Diverse forest ecosystems
  • Multiple viewpoints of the falls
  • Wildlife viewing opportunities

Trail Challenges

  • Rocky terrain near the falls
  • Steep descent to waterfall base
  • Potentially slippery rocks
  • Crowded conditions on weekends

Around mile 1.5, the trail character changes dramatically. You'll encounter several fallen trees that require scrambling over - nothing dangerous, but enough to add adventure to your journey. These natural obstacles actually enhance the wilderness feeling, unlike more developed trails such as Grotto Falls Trail in Tennessee.

The final approach to Stewart Falls requires careful attention. A steep, rocky descent leads to the main viewing area, where most hikers stop for photos. However, adventurous visitors can continue to the waterfall's base, though this requires crossing streams and navigating wet, potentially slippery rocks. The reward is experiencing the falls' full power and the refreshing mist that provides natural air conditioning.

Wildlife encounters add magic to the experience. During my visits, I've spotted various bird species, chipmunks, and even caught glimpses of deer in the early morning hours. The trail's diverse elevations create multiple micro-environments, from dense forest floors carpeted with ferns to rocky outcroppings offering panoramic vistas of the Wasatch Range.

Weather preparedness cannot be overstated for mountain hiking. I've experienced everything from sudden afternoon thunderstorms to unexpected temperature drops during seemingly perfect conditions. Layering is crucial - start with moisture-wicking base layers, add insulating mid-layers, and bring a waterproof shell regardless of the forecast. Mountain weather systems develop rapidly and can catch unprepared hikers off-guard.

Trail etiquette enhances everyone's experience and safety. Yield to uphill hikers, keep noise levels respectful, and practice Leave No Trace principles rigorously. The Stewart Falls area shows signs of heavy use, and every visitor's commitment to conservation ensures future generations can enjoy this natural wonder. Pack out all trash, stay on designated trails, and respect wildlife viewing distances.

Emergency preparedness goes beyond gear - inform someone of your hiking plans, including expected return time. Cell service is limited along much of the trail, though some carriers have coverage near the trailhead. Download offline maps to your smartphone as backup navigation, and consider carrying a GPS device or satellite communicator for remote area hiking. These precautions might seem excessive for a popular day hike, but mountain conditions can change situations quickly.

Fall hiking at Stewart Falls offers perhaps the most rewarding experience for serious outdoor enthusiasts. September through early October provides perfect weather conditions, fewer crowds, and stunning autumn colors throughout the surrounding forest. The aspen groves create a golden canopy that rivals Colorado's famous fall displays, while cooler temperatures make hiking more comfortable and photography more enjoyable.

Winter hiking requires serious preparation and experience with snow conditions. While the falls remain accessible and create stunning ice formations, the trail becomes challenging with snow depth varying significantly year to year. Microspikes or snowshoes become essential, and daylight hours limit hiking windows severely. Only experienced winter hikers should attempt Stewart Falls during snow season, though the reward is experiencing this natural wonder in pristine, uncrowded conditions that feel truly wild.

Wildlife activity varies dramatically by season, with spring and fall offering the best opportunities for animal sightings. Summer's heavy human traffic pushes most wildlife to early morning and late evening hours, while winter provides excellent tracking opportunities in fresh snow. Bird watching peaks during migration periods, and I've documented over 20 species during various seasonal visits, from mountain bluebirds to occasional golden eagles soaring above the canyon walls.

Frequently Asked Questions

How difficult is the Stewart Falls Trail for beginners?

Stewart Falls Trail is considered moderate and very suitable for beginners with basic fitness levels. The 3.5-mile roundtrip distance and 652-foot elevation gain make it challenging enough to feel accomplished without being overwhelming. Most beginners complete the hike in 2-3 hours, though I recommend allowing extra time for rest breaks and enjoying the scenery. The trail is well-marked and maintained, reducing navigation concerns for first-time hikers. Start early to avoid crowds and bring plenty of water - dehydration is the most common issue I see affecting beginner hikers on this trail.

What's the best time of year to hike Stewart Falls?

The optimal hiking season runs from May through October, with late spring and early fall offering the best overall experience. June provides peak waterfall flow and warm weather, while September offers fewer crowds and stunning fall colors. Summer months (July-August) feature perfect trail conditions but heavy crowds that require early morning starts. I personally prefer late May when wildflowers bloom and the falls run at maximum volume, or early October when autumn transforms the surrounding aspen groves into golden cathedrals. Winter hiking is possible but requires snow experience and appropriate gear including microspikes or snowshoes.

Can you swim in the pools at Stewart Falls?

While there are pools at the base of Stewart Falls, swimming isn't recommended due to extremely cold water temperatures and safety concerns. The water remains shockingly cold year-round, fed by mountain snowmelt and underground springs. Even during summer, water temperatures rarely exceed 50°F (10°C). Additionally, the rocky bottom and swift currents near the falls create hazardous conditions. Many visitors enjoy wading in the shallower areas and standing in the mist for cooling relief after the hike. I've seen people briefly immerse themselves for photos, but most find the water too cold for extended contact. Always exercise extreme caution around the waterfall's powerful flow and slippery rocks.

Are dogs allowed on the Stewart Falls Trail?

Yes, dogs are welcome on Stewart Falls Trail and must remain on leash at all times. The trail is quite dog-friendly, though owners should prepare for rocky terrain and potentially muddy conditions that might be challenging for smaller breeds. Bring extra water for your pet, as dogs can quickly become dehydrated in mountain conditions. Unfortunately, I regularly encounter dog waste along the trail - please pack waste bags and carry out all pet waste to preserve the trail for everyone. The rocky descent to the falls can be difficult for some dogs, and the stream crossings require careful supervision. Many local dog owners consider this their favorite hiking destination due to the moderate distance and interesting terrain that keeps dogs engaged throughout the journey.

How much does it cost to hike Stewart Falls?

Accessing Stewart Falls requires paying a fee at the Alpine Loop toll booth: $10 for a 3-day pass (cash only) or free with an America the Beautiful National Parks Pass. This fee covers access to the entire Alpine Loop area, not just Stewart Falls. There are no additional trail fees or permits required for day hiking. If you're staying at Sundance Resort, you can access the alternate trail route without paying the Alpine Loop fee, though resort guests typically pay significantly more for lodging. I strongly recommend purchasing the annual National Parks Pass if you plan multiple visits to Utah's outdoor destinations, as it quickly pays for itself and provides access to numerous other fee areas throughout the region.

Is there cell phone service on the Stewart Falls Trail?

Cell phone coverage is limited and unreliable throughout most of Stewart Falls Trail. Some carriers have spotty coverage near the Aspen Grove trailhead, but service becomes minimal once you enter the forest and completely absent near the falls themselves. This communication limitation is actually part of the trail's appeal for digital detox, but creates safety considerations for emergency situations. I always inform someone of my hiking plans and expected return time before departing. Download offline maps to your smartphone before starting the hike, and consider carrying a whistle for emergency signaling. Some hikers bring satellite communicators for peace of mind, though this level of preparation isn't necessary for most day hikers on this popular trail.
