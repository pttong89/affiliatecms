Running Nutrition: Fueling for Performance

The Golden Rule of Running Nutrition

For runs longer than 60-90 minutes, aim for 30-60g of carbohydrates per hour. Start fueling early (within the first 45 minutes) and fuel often (every 15-20 minutes).

Remember: Never try anything new on race day. Train your gut just like you train your legs.

Electrolyte Strategy

Light Sweaters:

Heavy Sweaters:

Frequently Asked Questions

What running shoes should I buy as a beginner?

Start with a neutral daily trainer from a reputable brand like Brooks Ghost 16, HOKA Clifton 9, or Nike Pegasus 41. Visit a specialty running store for proper fitting - your running shoe size is typically 0.5-1 full size larger than your street shoe. Focus on comfort over speed features, and budget $120-160 for quality that will last 300-500 miles. Avoid minimalist shoes, racing flats, or highly cushioned max shoes until you develop proper running form and understand your specific needs.

Do I really need a GPS running watch?

A GPS watch isn't essential for casual running, but becomes valuable for structured training or goal-oriented running. Benefits include accurate pace and distance tracking, heart rate monitoring for training zones, and recovery metrics to prevent overtraining. For beginners, start with a basic model like the Garmin Forerunner 55 ($200) rather than expensive multisport watches. You can also start with smartphone apps like Strava or Garmin Connect, though watches provide better accuracy and don't drain your phone battery. The key metrics that matter most: pace, distance, heart rate, and weekly mileage trends.

How much should I spend on running equipment to start?

A quality beginner setup costs $300-500 total. Prioritize: running shoes ($120-160), moisture-wicking clothing ($80-120), and good socks ($30-50). Optional but recommended: basic GPS watch ($150-200), reflective gear for safety ($20-40), and water bottle or belt ($25-50). Avoid expensive gear until you're running consistently 3-4x per week for several months. Many beginners spend on premium equipment thinking it will motivate consistency, but comfort and injury prevention from proper basics matter more than advanced features. Upgrade individual pieces as you identify specific needs through experience.

What's different about summer vs winter running gear?

Summer priorities: lightweight, moisture-wicking fabrics, sun protection, and increased hydration capacity. Key items include technical shirts with UPF protection, minimal shorts with phone pockets, electrolyte supplements, and handheld water bottles or hydration belts. Winter requires layering systems: moisture-wicking base layer, insulating mid-layer, and wind/water-resistant outer shell. Add traction devices for icy conditions, reflective gear for shorter daylight, and waterproof gloves. The biggest mistake is overdressing in winter - you should feel slightly chilly for the first 10 minutes. Both seasons benefit from quality running socks and properly fitted shoes, though winter may require slightly larger sizes to accommodate thicker socks.

How often should I replace my running equipment?

Running shoes: Every 300-500 miles depending on your weight, running surface, and shoe construction. Track mileage with apps or write the purchase date inside the shoe. Replace when tread wears significantly or when you feel more impact/soreness after runs. Apparel: High-quality technical fabrics last 2-3 years with proper care, while budget options may need annual replacement. Socks with lifetime warranties (like Darn Tough) can last 3-5 years. GPS watches: 4-6 years typical lifespan, though battery replacement can extend this. Replace gear that no longer performs its function - moisture-wicking clothing that stays wet, socks with holes, or watches with poor GPS accuracy. Proper care extends equipment life significantly.

Is expensive running gear worth the cost?

Quality gear pays off in comfort, durability, and injury prevention, but expensive doesn't always mean better. Invest more in items that directly impact safety and performance: shoes, socks, and weather-appropriate clothing. Mid-tier options often provide 90% of premium performance at 60% of the cost. Expensive gear becomes worthwhile when you're running 25+ miles per week consistently, training for specific goals, or have identified particular needs through experience. Carbon-plate racing shoes, premium GPS watches, and recovery equipment justify their cost for serious runners but offer minimal benefit to casual runners. Start with proven mid-range options, then upgrade specific pieces as you identify limitations in your current setup.
