Overview & First Impressions

The Salomon X Ultra 4 represents a significant evolution in the lightweight hiking shoe category, building upon the success of its predecessors while introducing new technologies and design elements. When I first unboxed my pair in early 2023, I was immediately struck by the shoe's more robust appearance compared to the sleeker X Ultra 3 model.

The X Ultra 4 features Salomon's Advanced Chassis technology, Contagrip MA outsole, and Gore-Tex waterproofing in a package that weighs just 14 ounces per shoe. This combination promises the stability of a hiking boot with the agility of a trail runner—a compelling proposition for modern hikers seeking versatile footwear.

Quick Specs

  • Weight: 14 oz (397g)
  • Drop: 11mm
  • Waterproof: Gore-Tex
  • Price: $155-180

Design Evolution from Previous Models

Having extensively used the X Ultra 3 for over two years, the transition to the X Ultra 4 felt like stepping into a completely different category of shoe. The most noticeable change is the higher ankle collar, which provides additional stability but required an adjustment period for long-distance hiking. The new design philosophy clearly prioritizes protection and support over the pure minimalist approach of earlier models.

Quality Control Concerns

Based on extensive user feedback and my own experience with multiple pairs, there are legitimate concerns about manufacturing consistency in the X Ultra 4 line. Issues range from toe box creasing problems to premature sole separation.

Performance Analysis

Traction and Grip: Contagrip Technology Deep Dive

The Contagrip MA outsole is arguably the X Ultra 4's strongest feature. During my testing across various terrains, from wet limestone in Kentucky caves to loose scree in Colorado's fourteeners, the traction consistently impressed. The chevron-shaped lugs provide excellent bite on both ascent and descent, while the compound rubber offers surprising durability for such a soft material.

Traction Strengths

  • Exceptional wet rock performance
  • Superior mud shedding capabilities
  • Confident grip on loose surfaces
  • Multi-directional lug pattern

Areas for Improvement

  • Faster wear on concrete/pavement
  • Softer compound than Vibram alternatives
  • Limited performance on smooth rock slabs
  • Noise on hard surfaces

Comfort and Fit Analysis

The fit characteristics of the X Ultra 4 represent both an evolution and a departure from Salomon's traditional approach. The toe box feels more spacious than the X Ultra 3, accommodating a wider range of foot shapes, but the overall last runs narrower than competing models from brands like Altra or Topo Athletic. For my foot shape (size 10.5 with normal width), the regular width provided adequate room without requiring the wide version.

The advanced cushioning system in the X Ultra 4 provides surprisingly good comfort for a lightweight hiking shoe. The OrthoLite insole offers decent arch support, though hikers with specific orthotic needs may want to consider custom replacements.

Waterproofing and Breathability

Gore-Tex remains the gold standard for waterproof hiking footwear, and the X Ultra 4's implementation is generally effective. During creek crossings and wet weather hiking, my feet stayed dry in most conditions. However, the membrane's breathability limitations become apparent during summer hiking or high-intensity activities.

Seasonal Performance Notes

Spring/Fall: Excellent performance in variable conditions with good temperature regulation.

Summer: Can feel warm during high-exertion activities; consider non-GTX version for hot climates.

Winter: Adequate for light snow conditions but lacks insulation for serious cold weather hiking.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do Salomon X Ultra 4 shoes typically last?

Based on extensive user feedback and my personal testing, the X Ultra 4's lifespan varies significantly depending on usage patterns and manufacturing quality. Conservative hikers can expect 300-500 miles, while aggressive trail use may reduce this to 200-300 miles. The outsole durability is generally good, but upper material separation represents the primary failure mode. Quality control inconsistencies mean some pairs fail prematurely while others exceed expectations. For maximum value, purchase from retailers with generous return policies and inspect your pair carefully during the first 50 miles of use.

Are the X Ultra 4 shoes true to size?

The X Ultra 4 generally runs true to size in length but fits narrower than many competing models. Most users find their normal shoe size appropriate, though those with wider feet should strongly consider the wide version or size up a half-size. The toe box offers more room than previous X Ultra models but less than brands like Altra or Topo Athletic. I recommend trying them on in-store or ordering from retailers with easy returns. The break-in period is minimal due to synthetic construction, so initial fit largely determines long-term comfort. For extended hiking applications, ensure adequate toe room for foot swelling.

What's the difference between X Ultra 4 and X Ultra 4 Mid versions?

The primary differences lie in ankle support, protection level, and weight. The Mid version adds approximately 1.5 ounces per pair while providing enhanced ankle stability and debris protection. Based on my testing of both versions, the Mid offers superior performance on technical terrain and loose surfaces but sacrifices some of the low-cut version's agility. The Mid's higher collar requires a brief adaptation period but provides confidence on challenging descents. For day hiking on established trails, the low-cut version suffices. For backpacking, technical terrain, or unstable ankles, the Mid version justifies its weight penalty. Both versions share identical outsole and waterproofing technologies.

How does the X Ultra 4 perform in winter conditions?

The X Ultra 4's winter performance is adequate for light snow and dry cold but limited for serious winter hiking. The Gore-Tex membrane provides windproof protection, while the Contagrip outsole maintains reasonable traction on packed snow and ice. However, the shoe lacks insulation, making it unsuitable for prolonged cold exposure below 20°F. The synthetic upper offers poor thermal properties compared to leather alternatives. For winter hiking, consider adding insulated insoles, wool socks, and gaiters. In icy conditions, microspikes or similar traction aids become necessary. For serious winter hiking, dedicated insulated hiking boots provide superior protection and comfort.

Can the X Ultra 4 be used for trail running?

While the X Ultra 4's lightweight design and responsive feel make it tempting for trail running, several factors limit its suitability for serious running applications. The 11mm heel-to-toe drop feels high compared to dedicated trail runners, and the Gore-Tex membrane restricts breathability during high-intensity activities. The advanced chassis provides excellent lateral stability but can feel restrictive during dynamic movements. For occasional jogging on gentle trails, the X Ultra 4 performs adequately. However, dedicated trail runners offer superior breathability, lower drops, and running-specific fit characteristics. The X Ultra 4 excels as a fast hiking shoe that can handle brief running sections rather than a primary trail running shoe.

Should I choose Gore-Tex or non-Gore-Tex version?

The Gore-Tex versus non-Gore-Tex decision depends primarily on your typical hiking conditions and personal preferences. The Gore-Tex version excels in wet weather, creek crossings, and variable conditions but sacrifices breathability and drying speed. During hot weather or high-intensity activities, the membrane becomes a liability rather than an asset. The non-Gore-Tex version offers superior breathability and faster drying but provides no water resistance. For Pacific Northwest conditions, spring hiking, or creek crossings, Gore-Tex proves invaluable. For desert hiking, summer conditions, or fast-drying preferences, the non-Gore-Tex version performs better. Consider your most common hiking conditions and whether you prefer staying dry or drying fast when making this decision.
