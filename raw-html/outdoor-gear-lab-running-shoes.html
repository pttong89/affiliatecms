Complete Buying Guide Based on Outdoor Gear Lab Methodology

Fit and Sizing Fundamentals

OGL's testing reveals significant sizing variations between brands. ASICS tends to run true to size, while Hoka often runs narrow. Based on their measurements and my experience:

  • Always try both feet - Most people have slightly different sized feet
  • Shop in the afternoon - Feet naturally swell throughout the day
  • Wear your running socks - Thickness affects fit significantly
  • Check toe clearance - Thumb width between longest toe and shoe front
  • Consider width options - Many brands offer wide and narrow versions

How should I choose between road and trail running shoes according to Outdoor Gear Lab?

Outdoor Gear Lab recommends choosing based on your primary running surfaces and terrain. Road running shoes like the ASICS Novablast 5 or Hoka Mach 6 are ideal for pavement, gravel paths, dirt roads, and treadmills, featuring more plush cushioning, breathable uppers, and lighter weight construction. Trail running shoes become necessary when running on uneven, rocky, or steep terrain, offering aggressive tread for traction, rock plates for protection, and lower stack heights for stability. If 80% or more of your running occurs on roads and maintained paths, stick with road shoes. For mixed terrain, consider having both types or choose a versatile trail shoe that can handle occasional road miles. The key is matching the shoe's design to your most common running environment while considering factors like weather conditions and seasonal terrain changes.

What are the key testing metrics that Outdoor Gear Lab uses to evaluate running shoes?

Outdoor Gear Lab evaluates running shoes across six primary metrics: cushioning, upper comfort, stability, weight, traction, and overall performance. Their testing process involves logging dozens of miles per shoe across various terrain types and workout intensities. In their laboratory, they perform precise measurements including stack height analysis, heel-to-toe drop verification, rocker angle assessment, and weight comparisons. They also conduct durability testing and comfort evaluation during different running phases. Each metric receives detailed scoring, with cushioning and comfort typically weighted most heavily for daily trainers, while weight and responsiveness become more critical for racing shoes. This comprehensive evaluation system ensures their recommendations account for both measurable specifications and subjective performance characteristics that matter to real-world runners.

When should I replace my running shoes based on Outdoor Gear Lab recommendations?

Outdoor Gear Lab recommends replacing running shoes every 300-500 miles, though this varies based on running style, body weight, and surface types. Key indicators include developing new hotspots or blisters that weren't present before, feeling more post-run aches and soreness, visible wear in tread patterns, compressed midsole foam that doesn't bounce back, and uneven wear patterns indicating biomechanical changes. They emphasize that comfort and performance degradation often occur before visible wear becomes obvious. Heavier runners or those who primarily run on concrete may need replacement closer to 300 miles, while lighter runners on softer surfaces might extend to 500 miles safely. Their testing shows that maintaining shoe rotation with 2-3 pairs can extend overall lifespan and provide better training variety, as different shoes stress your feet and legs in slightly different ways.
