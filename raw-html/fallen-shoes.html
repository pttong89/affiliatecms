The Rise and Resurrection of Fallen Footwear

The story of fallen shoes begins with one of skateboarding's most influential figures: Jamie Thomas, nicknamed "The Chief." After establishing himself as a powerhouse in the skateboarding world through his work with brands like Toy Machine and Zero Skateboards, Thomas set his sights on creating footwear that would revolutionize how skaters approached their craft.

In 2003, Thomas initially wanted to name his new venture "Armee," but the name didn't resonate with the intended audience. Instead, Fallen Footwear burst onto the scene with a name that perfectly embodied the skateboarding ethos of rising after every fall. The brand's mission was clear from day one: create shoes by skateboarders, for skateboarders, with no compromises on quality or performance.

During my early skateboarding years in the mid-2000s, I witnessed firsthand how famous footwear brands dominated the market, but Fallen carved out its unique niche. The brand's association with DC Shoes from 2003-2005 provided crucial distribution channels, but Thomas's vision extended far beyond corporate partnerships. When DC was acquired by Quiksilver in 2005, the amicable separation allowed Fallen to maintain its authentic skateboarding roots through Black Box Distribution.

The Golden Era (2005-2016)

At its peak, Fallen offered 17 different models in over 50 colorways. Popular models like The Clipper, The Patriot, The Rival, Trooper, and Forte became staples in skate shops worldwide. The brand's video parts, particularly the legendary "Ride the Sky" (2008), showcased not just incredible skating but how fallen shoes performed under the most demanding conditions.

The tragic closure announcement came on April 11, 2016, when Thomas posted on social media that spring 2016 would be Fallen's final season. As someone who had grown to appreciate the brand's commitment to skateboarding authenticity, this news hit the community hard. Thomas cited the increasing difficulty for core skate shoe brands to survive in an evolving marketplace while maintaining their mission of supporting local skate shops.

However, like its namesake suggests, Fallen rose again. On January 1, 2018, Town Connection, led by Ronnie Mazzei (Fallen's original distributor in Argentina), acquired the brand for a global relaunch. The resurrection brought back legendary team riders including Chris Cole, Tommy Sandoval, and Billy Marks, breathing new life into classic models while introducing innovative technologies.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Fallen shoes good for skateboarding?

Yes, fallen shoes are excellent for skateboarding. What happened to Fallen Footwear?

Fallen Footwear temporarily closed in 2016 when founder Jamie Thomas announced the brand's discontinuation due to increasing difficulties for core skate shoe brands in the evolving marketplace. However, the brand was acquired by Town Connection in 2018 and successfully relaunched with original team riders Chris Cole, Tommy Sandoval, and Billy Marks returning to the roster. The resurrection maintained Fallen's authentic skateboarding identity while incorporating modern technologies and construction methods. This phoenix-like return has been celebrated by the skateboarding community as a victory for authentic skate brands over mainstream commercialization.

What's the difference between Fallen Patriot and Bomber shoes?

The Fallen Patriot features classic stitching nose design available in both cupsole with FLX-Flexology technology and vulcanized versions, while the Bomber is a vulcanized-only model with one-piece toe construction. Patriots offer more impact protection making them ideal for street skating and stair sessions, while Bombers provide better board feel and flexibility perfect for technical flip tricks. The Patriot includes TrueFit heel technology for superior foot lock, whereas the Bomber emphasizes Hell Drag Reinforcement for durability in high-wear areas. Based on my testing, Patriots excel for heavy impact skating while Bombers shine for precision technical work.

How long do Fallen shoes last?

Based on my testing and user feedback, fallen shoes typically last 3-6 months of regular skateboarding. The Patriot model with cupsole construction tends to last longer than vulcanized models due to its enhanced impact protection. Durability depends significantly on skating frequency, trick types, and maintenance practices like using Shoe Goo on high-wear areas. During my 90-day intensive testing period, the Patriot showed minimal wear despite daily sessions, suggesting exceptional longevity for serious skaters. Pro skaters often get 4-6 months from a single pair, which is outstanding considering their intense skating schedules and demanding trick repertoires.

Where can I buy authentic Fallen shoes?

Authentic fallen shoes are available through their official website fallenfootwear.us, authorized skate shops like CCS and local skateboard retailers, and select online platforms including Amazon with proper authentication. Always purchase from authorized dealers to ensure authenticity and quality, as counterfeit skateboarding shoes can compromise safety and performance. Prices typically range from $65-95 for most models depending on construction type and colorway. I recommend supporting local skate shops when possible, as they often provide expert fitting advice and contribute directly to the skateboarding community. For online purchases, verify seller authorization and customer reviews before buying.
