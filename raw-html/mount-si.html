Trail Overview and Stats

Trail Statistics

Distance: 8.0 miles round-trip
Elevation Gain: 3,200 feet
Highest Point: 3,980 feet
Difficulty: Moderate to Strenuous
Estimated Time: 4-6 hours

Mount Si has earned its reputation as Washington's training ground for serious mountaineers. Located just 45 minutes from Seattle, this iconic peak offers the perfect blend of accessibility and challenge that keeps hikers coming back year after year.

The trail follows a well-maintained path of relentless switchbacks through lush Pacific Northwest forest, with the reward of stunning views across the Snoqualmie River Valley. What makes Mount Si particularly special is its consistent grade â€“ unlike many peaks with flat approaches and steep finishes, Si maintains a steady climb that builds endurance without overwhelming beginners.

Getting There and Parking

Driving Directions

From Seattle, take I-90 East to Exit 32 (436th Avenue SE/North Bend). Turn left (north) onto 436th Avenue SE and drive 0.5 miles. Turn left (west) onto North Bend Way and continue for 0.25 miles, then turn right (north) onto Mount Si Road. Follow Mount Si Road for 2.5 miles to reach the large trailhead parking area.

Parking Reality Check

Here's what no one tells you about Mount Si parking: it's a nightmare on weekends. The lot holds about 200 cars, but by 8 AM on any decent weather day, you'll be circling like a vulture. I've arrived at 7 AM on Saturdays and still struggled to find a spot. My advice? Arrive by 6:30 AM or consider weekday hikes.

The good news is that King County Metro's Trailhead Direct bus service runs on weekends during hiking season, connecting Seattle to Mount Si. It's environmentally friendly and eliminates parking stress â€“ though you'll need to plan your hike around the bus schedule.

Parking Requirements

A Washington State Discover Pass is required ($10 daily or $35 annual). Rangers regularly patrol and ticket, so don't risk it. You can purchase passes at the trailhead, but I recommend getting your annual pass in advance.

What to Expect on the Trail

The First Two Miles: Forest Immersion

The trail begins immediately with switchbacks â€“ no gentle warm-up here. You'll climb steadily through dense Pacific Northwest forest, with towering Douglas firs and western hemlocks creating a cathedral-like canopy. The trail is well-maintained but can be muddy during Washington's wet months (October through May).

Snag Flat: Ancient Forest Marvel

Around mile 1.5, you'll reach Snag Flat, home to some of the most impressive old-growth trees on the trail. These giants survived both logging and fires, with some specimens over 300 years old. The wooden boardwalk here provides excellent photo opportunities and interpretive signs explain the area's logging history.

Don't rush through Snag Flat like most hikers do. I always take 10 minutes here to appreciate these ancient sentinels. It's also an excellent spot to refuel and check your hydration â€“ you're about 40% done with the elevation gain at this point.

The Final Push: Views and Wildlife

Beyond mile 3, the trail steepens noticeably. You'll catch your first glimpses of the Snoqualmie Valley through breaks in the canopy. Wildlife becomes more common here â€“ I've encountered black bears twice (both times they moved off quickly) and regularly see deer, chipmunks, and the infamous gray jays that beg for snacks at the summit.

Summit Views and the Haystack Challenge

The official trail ends at a large meadow with spectacular 270-degree views. Mount Rainier dominates the southern horizon, while the Olympic Mountains stretch across the west. Seattle's skyline is visible on clear days, making you appreciate how quickly you've gained elevation from sea level.

The infamous Haystack â€“ Mount Si's true summit â€“ looms above the meadow. This is where many hikers make a crucial decision. The scramble to the Haystack is technical Class 3-4 terrain with serious exposure. Unless you have rock climbing experience and proper gear, stick to the meadow views. I've seen too many rescue helicopters on this mountain to recommend the scramble casually.

Safety Warning

The Haystack scramble has resulted in multiple fatalities and numerous rescues. Only attempt it if you have technical rock climbing skills, proper gear, and experience with exposed terrain. The meadow views are spectacular without the additional risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

How difficult is Mount Si for beginner hikers?

Mount Si is considered moderately difficult for beginners due to its 3,200 feet of elevation gain over 8 miles. While the trail is well-maintained with consistent switchbacks, the sustained uphill climb can be challenging. I recommend beginners start with shorter hikes first, but with proper preparation and pacing, many first-time hikers successfully complete Mount Si. Consider starting with easier trails like Mount Major before attempting this peak.

What time should I start hiking Mount Si?

I always recommend starting before 7 AM, especially on weekends and during summer months. The parking lot fills up quickly by 8-9 AM, and you'll avoid crowds on the trail. Early starts also mean cooler temperatures and better chances of clear summit views before afternoon clouds roll in. Unlike some peaks where timing is flexible, Mount Si's popularity makes early starts essential for a quality experience.

Can I hike Mount Si in winter?

Yes, but winter hiking on Mount Si requires proper preparation. From November through March, expect snow and icy conditions. Microspikes or snowshoes are essential, along with layers for temperature regulation. I've hiked it in winter several times, and while beautiful, the trail becomes much more challenging and potentially dangerous without proper gear. The experience is similar to winter conditions on Mount Ellinor â€“ spectacular but demanding.

Should I attempt the Haystack scramble?

The Haystack scramble should only be attempted by experienced hikers with rock climbing skills. It's a technical Class 3-4 scramble with serious exposure. Many injuries occur here annually. If you don't have scrambling experience or aren't comfortable with steep, exposed terrain, enjoy the views from the meadow instead. The meadow offers 90% of the views with none of the risk â€“ it's a much safer choice for most hikers.

What should I pack for a Mount Si day hike?

Essential items include 2-3 liters of water, energy-rich snacks, layers for changing weather, a first aid kit, headlamp, and proper hiking boots. In my experience, trekking poles are invaluable for the descent. Always check weather conditions and pack rain gear, even on sunny days, as mountain weather changes quickly. Your pack should be similar to what you'd bring for other challenging day hikes like Mount Cammerer or Mount Osceola.
