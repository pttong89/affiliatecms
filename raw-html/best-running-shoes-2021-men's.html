Buying Guide

Expert review of the best running shoes 2021 men's selections

Key Factors to Consider

Running Style & Distance

Your weekly mileage and typical distances should guide your choice. Daily trainers like the ASICS Novablast 2 excel for 30+ miles per week, while racing shoes like the Vaporfly Next% 2 are reserved for special occasions.

Foot Type & Gait

Understanding whether you're a heel striker, midfoot striker, or forefoot striker helps determine the optimal heel-to-toe drop. Most runners do well with 8-12mm drops, but minimalist enthusiasts might prefer 4-6mm options.

Cushioning Preferences

Firm cushioning provides better ground feel and responsiveness, while soft cushioning offers maximum comfort for longer distances. Your body weight and injury history should influence this choice.

Terrain & Environment

Road shoes like our featured models excel on pavement and tracks. Consider weather conditions, typical surfaces, and whether you need additional grip for wet conditions.

Technology Explained

Carbon Fiber Plates

Featured in racing shoes like the Vaporfly Next% 2 and Metaspeed Sky, these provide propulsion and energy return. Best for tempo runs and races, but not necessary for daily training.

Advanced Foam Technologies

Nike's ZoomX, New Balance's FuelCell, and ASICS' FlyteFoam Blast represent the pinnacle of midsole innovation. These lightweight foams provide exceptional energy return while maintaining durability.

Upper Construction

Engineered mesh uppers like those on the Rebel V2 offer better breathability and fit compared to traditional materials. Look for seamless construction to reduce hot spots.

Pro Tip

I recommend having at least two pairs of running shoes in rotation. This allows the foam to fully decompress between runs and can extend the life of both pairs. Consider pairing a daily trainer like the ASICS Novablast 2 with a lighter option like the New Balance Rebel V2 for variety. How do I choose the right running shoe for my needs?

Choose running shoes based on your running style, foot type, and intended use. Consider factors like heel-to-toe drop (8-12mm for most runners), cushioning level (firm for speed, soft for comfort), your weekly mileage, and whether you need stability features. Get properly fitted at a specialty running store and consider having multiple pairs for different types of runs. Your gait analysis and foot strike pattern should also influence your choice. I recommend visiting a specialty store for gait analysis, especially if you're transitioning from casual to serious running. Personal experience matters most – what works for others may not work for you.

What made 2021 special for running shoes?

2021 was a breakthrough year for running shoe technology, featuring widespread adoption of carbon fiber plates, nitrogen-infused foams, and advanced midsole designs. Brands like ASICS made significant comebacks with their Metaspeed line, while New Balance revolutionized the tempo category with FuelCell technology. The year also saw improved sustainability efforts and better upper materials across most major brands. Additionally, the return of in-person racing after pandemic restrictions drove innovation in racing-specific footwear. Companies invested heavily in R&D during 2020, and 2021 was when those innovations reached consumers.

Are expensive running shoes worth the investment?

Premium running shoes often justify their cost through advanced materials, better durability, and performance benefits. However, excellent options exist across all price ranges. Budget-friendly shoes like the New Balance FuelCell Propel V3 ($99) offer great value, while high-end models like the ASICS Metaspeed Sky ($250) provide cutting-edge technology for serious runners. Consider your running goals, weekly mileage, and budget when deciding. If you're running 30+ miles per week or training for competitive races, premium shoes often provide measurable benefits. For casual runners, mid-range options usually offer the best value proposition.

How often should I replace my running shoes?

Most running shoes should be replaced every 300-500 miles, depending on your running style, body weight, and the shoe's construction. Signs it's time for replacement include worn outsole tread, compressed midsole foam, uneven wear patterns, or increased soreness after runs. Track your mileage and consider rotating between multiple pairs to extend overall shoe life and reduce injury risk. Racing shoes typically have shorter lifespans (200-300 miles) due to softer foams and lighter construction. Keep a running log or use apps to track mileage per pair – this data helps predict when replacement is needed.
