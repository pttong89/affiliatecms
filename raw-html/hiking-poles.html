Types of Hiking Poles

Telescoping vs. Folding Poles

The fundamental choice in hiking poles comes down to telescoping (collapsible) versus folding designs. Telescoping poles, like traditional REI hiking poles, extend and contract like a spyglass, typically offering the widest adjustment range and often greater durability. They're ideal for varying terrain where you need frequent length adjustments.

Folding poles break down into multiple sections connected by internal cables, similar to tent poles. They pack incredibly small – often under 16 inches – making them perfect for air travel or ultralight backpacking. However, they typically have limited or no length adjustment and can be more vulnerable to cable failure in extreme conditions.

Materials: Aluminum vs. Carbon Fiber

Aluminum hiking poles remain the workhorses of the hiking world. They're durable, affordable, and bend rather than break under extreme stress. I've seen aluminum poles straightened out after being bent in rock falls, though they never quite perform the same. They're heavier but can handle serious abuse, making them ideal for aggressive hiking styles or rocky terrain.

Carbon fiber poles offer superior vibration dampening and significant weight savings – typically 2-4 ounces per pair compared to aluminum. They feel less fatiguing on long hikes and transmit less shock to your hands and arms. The trade-off is higher cost and the potential for catastrophic failure under side loads. The best hiking poles balance these materials strategically, sometimes using aluminum for locking mechanisms while keeping carbon shafts.

Complete Buying Guide

Key Features to Consider

The locking mechanism is arguably the most critical component of any hiking pole. FlickLock systems (lever-style) offer quick adjustments and visual confirmation of security, making them ideal for terrain requiring frequent length changes. Twist locks are lighter and have fewer moving parts but can be difficult to operate with wet or cold hands. I've experienced twist lock failure on winter hikes where condensation froze the mechanism.

Grip material significantly impacts comfort on long hikes. Cork grips conform to your hands over time, absorb moisture, and provide excellent grip even when wet. Foam grips are lighter and often more affordable but can become slippery and deteriorate faster with heavy use. Rubber grips offer durability but can become uncomfortable during extended use and don't breathe well.

Sizing and Adjustability

Proper sizing is crucial for maximizing the benefits of hiking poles. When standing upright holding the pole with the tip on the ground, your elbow should form a 90-degree angle. Most adjustable poles range from 24-54 inches, accommodating users from 4'10" to 6'4". However, you'll want to adjust length based on terrain – shorter for uphills (reducing strain on your shoulders), longer for downhills (providing better stability and reach).

Weight considerations extend beyond the poles themselves. Factor in accessories like baskets, rubber tips, and carrying cases. A 2-ounce difference per pole translates to 4 ounces total – significant for ultralight backpackers. However, don't sacrifice durability for weight savings if you hike aggressively or on technical terrain.

Consider your hiking style and destinations when choosing between telescoping and folding designs. Day hikers and those who frequently fly to hiking destinations often prefer folding poles for their packability. Backpackers and those hiking varied terrain typically benefit from the adjustability and durability of telescoping poles. Quality options exist in both categories, from budget-friendly hiking poles to professional-grade models.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need hiking poles for day hikes?

While not absolutely essential for every hike, hiking poles provide significant benefits even on day hikes. They reduce joint stress by up to 25%, improve stability on uneven terrain, and help maintain better posture while carrying a pack. For steep descents, stream crossings, or loose terrain, they're invaluable safety tools. Many hikers find once they start using poles, they never want to hike without them again. The investment in comfort and injury prevention makes them worthwhile for most hikers, especially those with knee issues or carrying heavier loads.

What's the difference between hiking poles and trekking poles?

The terms are often used interchangeably, but hiking poles typically refer to simpler, more basic models suitable for day hikes and casual use. Trekking poles usually feature more advanced materials like carbon fiber, better locking mechanisms, and additional features for serious backpacking. Both serve the same fundamental purpose of providing stability and reducing fatigue during outdoor adventures. The distinction is mostly marketing-driven, though trekking poles often command higher prices for premium features and materials.

How do I determine the correct length for my hiking poles?

The ideal hiking pole length creates a 90-degree angle at your elbow when you're standing upright holding the pole with the tip on the ground. For most people, this translates to roughly 0.68 times your height in inches. However, adjustable poles are recommended as you'll want shorter poles for uphill climbs and longer poles for downhill descents. Most quality poles adjust from 24-54 inches to accommodate different users and terrain. Consider your typical hiking terrain when choosing – technical trails benefit more from frequent adjustments than gentle, consistent grades.

Are carbon fiber poles worth the extra cost?

Carbon fiber poles offer several advantages: they're lighter (typically 2-4 ounces less per pair), absorb vibrations better, and feel less fatiguing over long distances. However, they're more expensive and can be more prone to catastrophic failure if subjected to side impacts. For serious hikers covering long distances or those prioritizing weight savings, carbon fiber is worth the investment. Casual hikers may find aluminum poles perfectly adequate. Consider your hiking frequency, distance, and budget when making this decision – the comfort benefits become more pronounced on longer adventures.

Can I use hiking poles for tent setup?

Many ultralight tents and tarp-tents are designed to use trekking poles as structural supports, eliminating the need for separate tent poles. This saves significant weight in your pack. However, ensure your poles extend to at least 48-50 inches and have reliable locking mechanisms. Some poles aren't designed for the lateral stress of tent setup, so check manufacturer recommendations. This dual-use functionality makes trekking poles even more valuable for backpackers, though it requires careful consideration of pole strength and your shelter's specific requirements.
