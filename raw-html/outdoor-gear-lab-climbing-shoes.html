Complete Analysis of Outdoor Gear Lab's Testing Method

Understanding how Outdoor Gear Lab climbing shoes are evaluated is crucial for interpreting their recommendations. Their methodology centers around five key performance metrics, each weighted at 20% of the overall score: comfort, smearing, edging, pulling, and crack climbing performance.

Testing Metrics Breakdown

  • Comfort (20%): Long-term wearability and break-in period
  • Smearing (20%): Performance on friction-dependent moves
  • Edging (20%): Precision on small holds and micro-edges
  • Pulling (20%): Heel/toe hooking and steep terrain performance
  • Cracks (20%): Jamming comfort and protection

Expert Buying Guide: Choosing Your Perfect Climbing Shoes

After years of following Outdoor Gear Lab climbing shoes recommendations and testing dozens of models personally, I've developed a systematic approach to shoe selection that goes beyond simply choosing the highest-rated option. Understanding how OGL's categories translate to your specific climbing goals is essential for making the right choice.

Beginner Selection Strategy

For climbers new to the sport, prioritize comfort and durability over performance ratings. The La Sportiva Tarantulace consistently earns OGL's beginner recommendation for good reason.

  • Size similar to street shoes initially
  • Focus on flat or minimally downturned models
  • Prioritize leather uppers for stretch and comfort
  • Choose lace-up closures for adjustability

Advanced Selection Criteria

Experienced climbers can leverage OGL's detailed performance metrics to match shoes to specific projects and climbing styles.

  • Match aggression level to primary climbing style
  • Consider specialized shoes for different disciplines
  • Evaluate long-term durability vs. performance trade-offs
  • Size for intended use (comfort vs. performance fit)

Seasonal and Regional Considerations

One aspect often overlooked in Outdoor Gear Lab reviews is how shoes perform across different seasons and regional climbing styles. During winter climbing in Red Rocks, I discovered that shoes sized perfectly for summer conditions became uncomfortably tight in cold weather. Similarly, the rubber compounds that excel on southeastern sandstone may not perform as well on alpine granite.

Pro Tips from the Field

Multiple Shoe Strategy: Serious climbers often maintain 2-3 pairs for different applications. I keep comfortable trainers for gym sessions, performance shoes for outdoor projects, and specialized crack shoes for trad climbing.

Break-in Process: Contrary to popular belief, modern climbing shoes shouldn't require a painful break-in period. If shoes cause significant discomfort initially, they're likely too small or the wrong shape for your feet.
