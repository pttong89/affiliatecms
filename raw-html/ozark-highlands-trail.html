What is the Ozark Highlands Trail?

The Ozark Highlands Trail represents one of America's most challenging and rewarding long-distance hiking experiences. Stretching 254 miles across northwestern Arkansas, this wilderness pathway traverses some of the most remote and pristine terrain between the Rockies and Appalachians. Unlike more commercialized trails, the Ozark Highlands Trail offers genuine solitude where you might hike for days without encountering another human being.

What sets this trail apart is its commitment to wilderness preservation. The entire route avoids towns and roads, creating an unbroken wilderness corridor through the Ozark Mountains. Featured in National Geographic Magazine and Backpacker Magazine, the trail has gained recognition among serious hikers seeking authentic backcountry experiences. The Ozark Highlands Trail Association, founded in 1981, continues building and maintaining this remarkable pathway.

Currently, 254 miles are officially designated, though only 237 miles consist of built trail. The remaining 17 miles through the Lower Buffalo Wilderness require challenging bushwhacking and navigation skills. Future plans call for extending the trail to 320 miles, eventually connecting with Missouri's Ozark Trail to create over 700 miles of continuous hiking opportunities.

The trail's unique character comes from its seasonal accessibility. While other long-distance trails become impassable during winter, the Ozark Highlands Trail offers excellent winter hiking conditions. This makes it perfect for hikers seeking adventure during traditional off-seasons. The trail showcases Arkansas's natural diversity, from clear mountain streams to towering limestone bluffs, ancient forests to cascading waterfalls.

Trail Sections and Navigation

Understanding the Ozark Highlands Trail sections is crucial for planning your adventure. The trail divides into four distinct segments, each offering unique challenges and rewards. Having hiked all sections, I can attest that each requires different preparation and mindset.

Boston Mountains Section (164 Miles)

The Boston Mountains section forms the trail's backbone, running from Lake Fort Smith State Park to the Buffalo River at Woolum. Completed in 1989, this segment originally constituted the entire Ozark Highlands Trail. The terrain here is consistently challenging, featuring steep climbs, rocky descents, and numerous stream crossings. During my hike, I averaged 12 miles per day through this section due to the rugged topography.

Key features include White Rock Mountain's spectacular views, the challenging Hurricane Creek crossings, and the beautiful Marinoni Scenic Area. Water sources are abundant but require filtration. The section passes through several wilderness areas, ensuring pristine conditions but also meaning limited escape routes if weather turns severe. Trail blazing is generally good, with white rectangular markers and mile posts every mile.

Buffalo River Section (43 Miles)

The Buffalo River section co-exists with the Buffalo River Trail, offering a completely different hiking experience. Here, the Ozark Highlands Trail alternates between riverside walking and high bluff traverses. This section provides some of the trail's most spectacular scenery, with sweeping river views and historical homestead remnants.

River crossings can be challenging during high water periods. I encountered knee-deep water at several fords during my March hike. The National Park Service prohibits dogs on this section, and camping regulations are more restrictive than on Forest Service lands. The section offers excellent wildlife viewing, including elk, deer, and numerous bird species.

Lower Buffalo Wilderness Gap (17 Miles)

The most challenging aspect of the Ozark Highlands Trail isn't the miles you hike – it's the miles you bushwhack. Between AR-14 and Spring Creek lies a 17-mile wilderness gap with no constructed trail. This section requires advanced navigation skills, physical fitness, and mental toughness. I spent two days fighting through waist-deep brambles and multiple river crossings.

The Ozark Highlands Trail Association publishes a detailed route guide essential for this section. GPS coordinates and compass navigation are mandatory. Despite the challenges, this wilderness offers unparalleled solitude and pristine beauty. Plan for slow progress and difficult camping conditions.

Sylamore Section (32 Miles)

The newest section of the Ozark Highlands Trail runs from Spring Creek to Matney Knob, featuring recently restored trail with excellent condition. This section combines forest roads, technical rock gardens, and beautiful creek crossings. The terrain is challenging but well-maintained, offering a welcome change after the wilderness gap.

Water sources are reliable, and camping opportunities are good. The section passes through diverse ecosystems, from dense hardwood forests to open meadows. Trail marking is excellent, and the recent maintenance makes navigation straightforward. This section connects to various Ozark Mountain adventures and shorter hiking opportunities.

My Experience Hiking the OHT

My first encounter with the Ozark Highlands Trail came after completing several other long-distance trails. Nothing quite prepared me for the raw wilderness experience this trail delivers. Starting my thru-hike in November, I expected challenging conditions but discovered a trail that pushes you beyond typical backpacking comfort zones.

The first few days felt deceptively easy around Lake Fort Smith. The trail meanders gently through mixed forests with frequent water sources and established campsites. By day three, reality hit as the Ozark Highlands Trail began its relentless climbing through the Boston Mountains. My daily mileage dropped from a planned 15 miles to 10-12 miles as the terrain demanded respect.

Hurricane Creek proved the trail's most memorable challenge. During my crossing, recent rains had swollen the creek to nearly river proportions. The normally rock-hop crossing became a careful wade through swift, knee-deep water. This experience taught me why experienced hikers emphasize checking weather conditions before attempting the Ozark Highlands Trail.

The Lower Buffalo Wilderness gap tested every backcountry skill I possessed. Armed with the OHTA route guide and GPS waypoints, I still found navigation challenging through the dense vegetation. Progress slowed to one mile per hour through some sections. However, the pristine wilderness and complete solitude provided rewards few trails can match. Standing on remote bluffs overlooking the Buffalo River, I understood why this trail attracts serious wilderness enthusiasts.

Weather proved unpredictable throughout my journey. November brought everything from 70-degree sunny days to freezing rain and even light snow. The trail's exposure to weather systems means conditions change rapidly. Quality Ozark camping gear becomes essential, not optional. My three-season tent handled the conditions, but a four-season shelter would have provided more confidence during the worst weather.

Lessons Learned from 254 Miles

Completing the Ozark Highlands Trail taught me valuable lessons applicable to any serious backpacking adventure. First, water management differs significantly from western trails. Instead of scarce water requiring careful rationing, Arkansas streams provide abundant sources requiring consistent filtration and treatment. I went through water filter cartridges faster than anticipated.

Second, the trail demands flexibility in daily planning. Unlike trails with established camping every few miles, the Ozark Highlands Trail sometimes forces unexpected stops when suitable campsites appear. My most memorable night occurred when approaching darkness and lack of flat ground forced an uncomfortable slope camping experience. Since then, I always scout campsites earlier in the day.

Third, solitude comes with responsibility. Cell phone coverage is practically nonexistent for long stretches. During my 18-day journey, I encountered fewer than a dozen other hikers. This isolation, while peaceful, means self-reliance becomes critical. Emergency preparedness isn't just recommended – it's mandatory for safe Ozark Highlands Trail completion.

Resupply Strategy and Logistics

Resupply planning represents the most complex aspect of Ozark Highlands Trail preparation. Unlike trails with frequent town access, this wilderness route forces careful logistical planning months in advance. Poor resupply planning can turn an adventure into a survival situation, while good planning ensures steady progress and adequate nutrition throughout your journey.

Post Office Resupply Points

Three small post offices provide the primary resupply opportunities along the Ozark Highlands Trail. Oark (mile 61), Ozone (mile 84.7), and Pelsor (mile 123) each require multi-mile road walks from the trail. These tiny rural post offices operate limited hours – typically closing by noon on Saturdays and remaining closed Sundays and Mondays. Call ahead to confirm hours and notify them of your expected arrival.

Mail drops require careful timing. Send packages to arrive 2-3 days before your estimated pickup date to account for slow rural mail delivery. Address packages to "General Delivery" with your name and estimated pickup date. Include your cell phone number if you have one, though service is unreliable. Pack resupply boxes in sturdy containers as small post offices may lack secure storage.

Food Planning and Carrying Capacity

The Ozark Highlands Trail demands carrying 7-10 days of food between resupply points. This significantly increases pack weight and requires careful food selection. Focus on calorie-dense, lightweight options. During my thru-hike, I averaged 3,500 calories per day but still lost 15 pounds due to the trail's physical demands.

Plan for 125 calories per ounce of food weight minimum. Avoid canned goods and heavy items. Dehydrated meals, nuts, dried fruits, energy bars, and pasta work well. Consider nutrition variety – eating the same foods for weeks becomes mentally challenging. Include some fresh items you can pick up during town stops to break the monotony of processed foods.

Alternative Resupply Strategies

Creative hikers develop alternative resupply methods for the Ozark Highlands Trail. Cache drops work well if you can drive to accessible points before starting your hike. Popular cache locations include trailheads and road crossings, though always respect private property and local regulations.

Support person meetings provide another option. Highway 7, Highway 16, and US Highway 65 crossings allow car access for friends or family to bring supplies. This strategy requires flexible timing and reliable communication. Consider combining cache drops and support meetings for maximum flexibility during your Ozark Highlands Trail adventure.

Town Stops and Services

Marshall, Arkansas (mile 178) provides the first real town services along the Ozark Highlands Trail. This small town offers a grocery store, restaurants, Dollar General, and basic services. However, accommodation options are limited – call ahead to confirm availability. The hitch from Highway 65 to Marshall is usually successful but may require patience.

Mountain View, Arkansas, accessible from several trail points, provides more services including outfitters that understand hiker needs. Some hikers arrange mail drops here through local businesses. Research and confirm any business arrangements before starting your hike. Small towns can change quickly, and services hikers relied on may no longer exist.

Best Time to Hike and Weather

Timing your Ozark Highlands Trail adventure correctly can mean the difference between an epic wilderness experience and a miserable struggle. Arkansas weather patterns create distinct seasons, each offering unique advantages and challenges. Having hiked sections during different times of year, I can provide insights into what to expect throughout the calendar.

Peak Season: Late Fall to Early Spring

The optimal window for the Ozark Highlands Trail runs from late October through early April. During this period, several factors align to create ideal hiking conditions. Temperatures moderate to comfortable ranges, typically 40-70°F during daytime with nighttime lows in the 20-40°F range. More importantly, the oppressive humidity and biting insects that make summer hiking unbearable largely disappear.

November through February represents the sweet spot for experienced hikers. My November thru-hike featured perfect conditions – crisp mornings, comfortable afternoons, and minimal bug pressure. However, this period coincides with hunting season, making blaze orange clothing essential for safety. The reduced vegetation also improves trail visibility and reduces bushwhacking difficulty in the Lower Buffalo Wilderness.

Winter hiking on the Ozark Highlands Trail offers unique rewards. Snow occasionally dusts the landscape, creating stunning photography opportunities. Ice formations around waterfalls provide magical scenes. However, winter demands serious preparation for potential temperature drops into single digits and occasional ice storms that can make travel dangerous.

Spring Hiking Considerations

Spring (March-May) presents both opportunities and challenges for Ozark Highlands Trail hiking. Early spring offers moderate temperatures and spectacular wildflower displays. Stream flows run high from spring rains, creating impressive waterfalls but potentially dangerous creek crossings. The famous Hurricane Creek crossing can become impassable during spring flood events.

Late spring brings increasing challenges as temperatures rise and vegetation explodes. By May, the trail corridor becomes overgrown in many sections, particularly the Lower Buffalo Wilderness route. Tick populations surge during warm spring weather, creating serious health concerns. Chiggers also become problematic, causing intensely itchy welts that can persist for weeks.

Why Summer Is Problematic

Summer hiking on the Ozark Highlands Trail is strongly discouraged for multiple reasons. Arkansas summers feature oppressive heat and humidity combinations that make strenuous hiking dangerous. Temperatures regularly exceed 90°F with humidity levels creating heat indices over 100°F. The dense forest canopy provides limited cooling, and water sources may become stagnant or dry up entirely.

Insect pressure becomes intolerable during summer months. Ticks reach peak activity, carrying diseases like Rocky Mountain spotted fever and ehrlichiosis. Chiggers infest grassy areas, making camping miserable. Mosquitoes swarm near water sources, while biting flies make daytime hiking unpleasant. The combination of heat, humidity, and insects creates conditions that even experienced hikers find unbearable.

Vegetation growth during summer creates additional challenges. Trail corridors become tunnel-like passages through dense undergrowth. Poison ivy flourishes, creating unavoidable contact during bushwhacking sections. Spider webs span the trail continuously, requiring constant face protection. Overall, summer conditions make the Ozark Highlands Trail more of an endurance test than an enjoyable wilderness experience.

Weather Patterns and Preparation

Arkansas weather systems move quickly and can change dramatically within hours. During my Ozark Highlands Trail thru-hike, I experienced everything from 70°F sunny skies to freezing rain and snow within a single week. Weather forecasting becomes challenging due to limited cell service and the trail's remote location.

Precipitation patterns vary significantly by season but remain unpredictable year-round. Winter brings occasional ice storms that can make hiking treacherous. Spring features frequent thunderstorms with potential for severe weather including tornadoes. Fall generally provides the most stable conditions but can still produce significant storms. Quality rain gear and flexible scheduling are essential regardless of hiking season.

For optimal Ozark outdoor adventures, plan your Ozark Highlands Trail attempt between November and March. This timing maximizes your chances of comfortable weather while minimizing insect pressure and vegetation challenges. However, prepare for variable conditions and have contingency plans for severe weather events that could strand you on remote trail sections.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to hike the entire Ozark Highlands Trail?

The complete Ozark Highlands Trail typically takes 2-3 weeks for most hikers to complete. The 254-mile trail requires an average of 15-21 days depending on your hiking pace, weather conditions, and resupply strategy. I completed my thru-hike in 18 days, averaging about 14 miles per day with rest days built in for resupply and weather delays. Experienced hikers might complete it faster, while those wanting to savor the experience might take longer. The challenging terrain, stream crossings, and bushwhacking sections naturally slow progress compared to more developed trails.

What is the best time of year to hike the Ozark Highlands Trail?

The optimal time for hiking the Ozark Highlands Trail is late fall through early spring, specifically from late October to early April. During my November hike, I experienced perfect weather with daytime temperatures in the 50s-60s and minimal bugs. Winter hiking is possible but requires preparation for temperatures that can drop into the teens. Avoid late spring and summer due to oppressive heat, humidity, ticks, and overgrown vegetation that makes trail navigation difficult. The peak season coincides with hunting season, so wearing blaze orange is strongly recommended for safety.

Where can I resupply along the Ozark Highlands Trail?

Resupply options are limited on the Ozark Highlands Trail, making it one of the more challenging aspects. The main resupply points are post offices at Oark (mile 61), Ozone (mile 84.7), and Pelsor (mile 123) - all requiring multi-mile road walks. The first feasible hitch to a town isn't until mile 178 at US Highway 65, where you can reach Marshall, Arkansas. Plan for long carries between resupply points, and consider mail drops well in advance as these small post offices have limited hours. Cache drops and support person meetings at highway crossings provide alternative strategies.

Is the Lower Buffalo Wilderness section really that difficult?

Yes, the Lower Buffalo Wilderness section is genuinely challenging and not for inexperienced hikers. This 17-mile gap between AR-14 and Spring Creek requires bushwhacking through dense vegetation with no maintained trail. During my traverse, I encountered waist-deep brambles, multiple river crossings, and complete navigation reliance on GPS and compass. The area is beautiful but remote with limited camping and no trail markers. I strongly recommend purchasing the OHTA route guide and having solid wilderness navigation skills before attempting this section. Progress slows to 1-2 miles per hour in difficult areas.
