Design and Construction Analysis

Close-up of the Moab 2 GTX's mixed material construction

The Merrell Moab 2 GTX represents a thoughtful evolution of the original design, incorporating lessons learned from thousands of hikers worldwide. The upper construction combines full-grain and nubuck leather with highly breathable mesh panels, creating a shoe that balances durability with weight considerations. At 15.2 oz (431g) for a men's size 9, it's not the lightest option available, but this weight serves a purpose in providing structural integrity.

What immediately struck me about the build quality was the attention to high-wear areas. Merrell has reinforced the toe cap with a substantial rubber rand that extends well up the forefoot, protecting against rock strikes that would damage lesser shoes. The heel counter feels substantial without being rigid, providing stability while allowing natural foot movement. Compared to the newer Merrell Moab 3 GTX, the Moab 2 feels more broken-in straight out of the box.

The midsole construction deserves particular attention. Merrell uses their Air Cushion technology in the heel, which I initially dismissed as marketing speak until I cut open a worn pair (yes, I sacrifice gear for science). There's actually a small air pocket that provides meaningful shock absorption, though it's more subtle than the explosive cushioning found in running shoes. The EVA midsole surrounding this air pocket offers a firm yet forgiving platform that excels on moderate terrain.

The outsole deserves special mention, as it's where the Moab 2 GTX truly shines. Merrell partnered with Vibram to develop the TC5+ rubber compound, which strikes an excellent balance between durability and grip. The lug pattern, while not as aggressive as some dedicated technical hikers, provides reliable traction on most surfaces. After 500+ miles, my outsoles show wear but remain fully functional—a testament to the compound's durability.

One area where the design falls short is the lacing system. While the traditional eyelet and hook design is bomber and field-repairable, it doesn't provide the precise fit adjustment that modern quick-lace systems offer. For hikers with narrow heels or high-volume feet, this can lead to the fit issues that plague many online reviews. Understanding this limitation upfront helps set appropriate expectations and guides sizing decisions.

Waterproof Performance Testing

Real-world waterproof testing of the Merrell Moab 2 GTX

The Gore-Tex membrane in the Merrell Moab 2 GTX performs exactly as advertised—with some important caveats that real-world testing revealed. During my extensive field testing, including deliberate stream crossings and extended periods in wet conditions, the waterproofing proved reliable up to the collar height. However, breathability remains the eternal compromise with any waterproof membrane, and the Moab 2 GTX is no exception.

My most rigorous test came during a four-day Olympic Peninsula backpacking trip where rainfall totaled over six inches. The shoes kept my feet completely dry during stream crossings and while traversing saturated moss-covered logs. However, on day three, I noticed moisture buildup from internal condensation—a reminder that Gore-Tex blocks water in both directions. This taught me the importance of proper sock selection and the need to air out the shoes whenever possible.

Waterproof Testing Results

Stream Crossing Test: Stood in 4-inch deep water for 10 minutes—completely dry feet. The seam sealing held perfectly, and no water penetrated through the upper materials.

Puddle Stomping Test: Deliberately walked through standing water up to mid-sole level for extended periods during multiple hikes. Zero water ingress through the Gore-Tex membrane.

Extended Rain Test: Three consecutive days of hiking in moderate to heavy rain. Feet remained dry from external moisture, but breathability limitations became apparent during high-exertion periods.

The key to maximizing the waterproof performance lies in understanding the system's limitations. The Gore-Tex membrane extends only to the collar height, so puddles deeper than about 3 inches will overtop the shoes. Additionally, the fabric's DWR (Durable Water Repellent) treatment gradually wears off with use, though this can be refreshed with aftermarket treatments. I found that washing the shoes with specialized technical cleaner and reapplying DWR treatment every 100 miles maintained optimal performance.

Comparing the Moab 2 GTX's waterproofing to competitors like the Merrell Moab GTX Men's predecessor, the improvement in seam sealing and membrane integration is noticeable. The newer construction eliminates many of the weak points that allowed water infiltration in the original design. However, for hikers prioritizing breathability, the non-waterproof Moab 2 Ventilator remains the better choice for dry conditions.

Comfort and Fit Guide

Proper fit is crucial for Moab 2 GTX performance

The comfort equation for the Merrell Moab 2 GTX starts with proper sizing, but it doesn't end there. After testing multiple sizes across different seasons and with various sock combinations, I've developed a comprehensive understanding of how these shoes fit different foot types. The most important lesson: the Moab 2 GTX runs true to size length-wise but can feel narrow for some foot shapes, particularly in the midfoot area.

Out of the box, the Moab 2 GTX requires virtually no break-in period—a significant advantage over traditional leather hiking boots. The synthetic and mesh construction flexes naturally from day one, though the midsole does soften slightly after the first 50 miles. This immediate comfort made them my go-to choice for spur-of-the-moment hiking adventures where I couldn't afford hot spots or blisters.

The toe box offers generous space for most foot shapes, with enough room for natural toe splay during descents. However, the midfoot can feel constrictive for hikers with high arches or wide feet. I addressed this by upgrading to the wide width version, which opened up the midfoot without compromising heel fit. For hikers considering the Merrell Moab 2 GTX boots version, the mid-height collar provides additional ankle support but can create pressure points for some ankles.

Sizing Recommendations by Foot Type

Narrow/Normal Feet

Order true to size. The standard width provides appropriate snugness without pressure points. Consider half-size up for thicker socks or all-day comfort.

Wide Feet

Strongly recommend wide width option. Standard width often creates midfoot pressure. Stay true to size length-wise when ordering wide.

High Arches

May experience midfoot pressure in standard width. Wide width or aftermarket insoles often resolve fit issues. Consider professional fitting.

The heel fit deserves special attention, as it's the most commonly criticized aspect in online reviews. The heel counter provides adequate structure, but the overall heel cup can feel loose for some foot shapes, leading to lift during the stride. I solved this with a simple lacing technique: skip the bottom eyelet and create a heel lock by running the laces backwards through the top hooks before tying. This modification dramatically improved heel security without affecting overall comfort.

Personal Fitting Experience: During my initial fitting at a local outdoor retailer, I tried on sizes 10, 10.5, and 11 in both standard and wide widths. The size 10 wide provided the best overall fit for my slightly wide feet with normal arches. I recommend trying on shoes later in the day when feet are naturally swollen to simulate hiking conditions.

Sock selection significantly impacts comfort in the Moab 2 GTX. I found that medium-thickness merino wool hiking socks provided the best balance of cushioning and moisture management. Thick cushioned socks can make the shoes feel tight, while thin liner socks don't provide adequate protection on longer hikes. The internal seaming is well-finished, so hot spots are rarely an issue with proper sock choice.

The insole system deserves mention for its versatility. The removable EVA insole provides moderate arch support and cushioning, but it can be easily swapped for custom orthotics or aftermarket alternatives. For hikers with specific foot issues, this adaptability makes the Moab 2 GTX a viable platform for customization. The internal volume accommodates most insole modifications without compromising fit.

Performance on Different Terrains

Over 18 months of testing, I've pushed the Merrell Moab 2 GTX across virtually every terrain type found in North American hiking. From the loose scree of Colorado's 14ers to the root-laden trails of Pacific Northwest temperate rainforests, these shoes have proven their versatility while revealing their limitations. Understanding how they perform on different surfaces helps match expectations with reality.

🏔️ Rocky Terrain

Performance: Excellent

The Vibram TC5+ outsole excels on dry rock, providing reliable grip on granite slabs and limestone. The protective toe cap has saved me from countless rock strikes. However, wet rock requires caution as the lug pattern lacks aggressive edges.

🌲 Forest Trails

Performance: Outstanding

This is where the Moab 2 GTX truly shines. The mixed terrain capabilities handle roots, rocks, and soft soil with confidence. Debris shedding is good, and the waterproofing excels in creek crossings common on forest trails.

🏜️ Desert Hiking

Performance: Good with Caveats

The Gore-Tex membrane becomes a liability in extreme heat, creating a sauna effect. The outsole compound softens in high temperatures but maintains grip. Fine sand infiltration through the collar can be an issue on sandy trails.

❄️ Snow and Ice

Performance: Limited

The lug pattern provides some traction on packed snow, but these shoes weren't designed for winter conditions. Ice requires microspikes or similar traction aids. The Gore-Tex does provide some insulation and waterproofing in slushy conditions.

🌧️ Muddy Conditions

Performance: Fair

The lug spacing doesn't shed mud as effectively as specialized designs. Traction degrades significantly in sticky clay or deep mud. However, the waterproofing keeps feet dry, and cleanup is straightforward.

🥾 Technical Terrain

Performance: Moderate

The flexible midsole and heel lockdown issues limit performance on technical terrain requiring precise foot placement. Better suited for Class 1-2 hiking than scrambling. Consider the Moab 2 GTX mens mid-height for additional ankle support.

During my most challenging test—a traverse of the Teton Crest Trail in Wyoming—the shoes performed admirably on the mixed terrain but revealed their limitations on steep, loose scree fields. The lack of aggressive lugs and firm platform made me work harder to maintain traction compared to more technical hiking shoes. However, for the majority of day hikers tackling well-maintained trails, these limitations rarely become issues.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Merrell Moab 2 GTX compare to the newer Moab 3 GTX?

The Moab 3 GTX addresses some fit issues found in the Moab 2, particularly around heel lockdown and midfoot support. It features updated materials and construction but comes at a $20-30 price premium. The Moab 2 GTX remains an excellent value choice, especially when found on sale. Both share similar waterproofing and comfort characteristics, making the choice largely dependent on budget and whether you experience the fit issues common to the Moab 2. For most hikers, the performance difference doesn't justify the extra cost unless fit problems necessitate the upgrade.

Should I size up in the Merrell Moab 2 GTX for hiking?

The Moab 2 GTX runs true to size for length, but the width can feel narrow for some foot shapes. I recommend trying your normal size first, then considering a half-size up only if you plan to wear thick socks or hike long distances where foot swelling becomes a factor. For wide feet, the wide-width option in your normal size typically provides the best fit. Avoid sizing up more than a half size, as this can compromise heel fit and lead to blisters. Remember that proper hiking sock selection impacts fit significantly—medium-thickness merino wool socks provide the best balance of cushioning and moisture management.

How long do Merrell Moab 2 GTX shoes typically last?

Based on my testing and extensive user feedback, the Moab 2 GTX typically provides 400-600 miles of hiking before requiring replacement, though this varies significantly based on terrain, hiking style, and body weight. The Vibram outsole compound is quite durable, often outlasting the midsole cushioning and upper materials. Heavier hikers or those covering predominantly rocky terrain may see shorter lifespans, while lighter hikers on softer trails often exceed 600 miles. The Gore-Tex membrane typically maintains waterproofing throughout the shoe's life if properly maintained. Regular cleaning and DWR treatment refreshing can extend overall lifespan and performance.

Are the Merrell Moab 2 GTX shoes suitable for backpacking?

The Moab 2 GTX works well for lightweight backpacking (packs under 25-30 pounds) but isn't ideal for traditional heavy backpacking. The flexible midsole and lower-cut design provide less support than dedicated backpacking boots when carrying heavier loads over multiple days. For overnight trips with ultralight gear, they perform admirably and offer superior comfort compared to heavier alternatives. However, the heel lockdown issues that some users experience can become more problematic under load. If you're primarily a backpacker rather than day hiker, consider the mid-height version or more supportive alternatives like the Salomon Quest series.

How waterproof is the Gore-Tex membrane in real-world conditions?

The Gore-Tex membrane in the Moab 2 GTX performs reliably in typical hiking conditions, keeping feet dry during stream crossings, puddle encounters, and moderate rainfall. However, it's not magic—water can still enter through the collar opening if you step in deep water or during prolonged exposure to driving rain. The DWR treatment on the outer fabric helps water bead and roll off initially, but this wears off with use and needs periodic refreshing. Internal condensation can build up during high-exertion activities, especially in warm weather, leading to damp feet from sweat rather than external water. For most day hiking scenarios involving occasional wet conditions, the waterproofing provides valuable peace of mind and protection.

Can I use custom orthotics with the Merrell Moab 2 GTX?

Yes, the Moab 2 GTX accommodates custom orthotics quite well thanks to its removable insole system and generous internal volume. The factory EVA insole removes easily, and most standard orthotic thicknesses fit without compromising the upper fit. However, very thick orthotics might require sizing up a half size to maintain proper toe clearance. The shoe's flexible construction adapts well to different foot shapes and support needs, making it popular among hikers with plantar fasciitis, flat feet, or other conditions requiring custom support. I recommend bringing your orthotics when trying on the shoes to ensure proper fit, as thickness and volume vary significantly between different orthotic types and manufacturers.
