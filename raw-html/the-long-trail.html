What is the Long Trail?

The Long Trail represents more than just a hiking path—it's a living piece of American outdoor history. Conceived by James P. Taylor in 1909 and built by the Green Mountain Club between 1910 and 1930, the long trail became the prototype that inspired the famous Appalachian Trail. Running 273 miles along Vermont's spine, this footpath traverses the Green Mountains from the Massachusetts border near North Adams to Journey's End at the Canadian frontier.

What makes the long trail unique isn't just its age—it's the character carved by countless volunteers who've maintained this wilderness corridor for over a century. Unlike its more famous cousin, the AT, the Long Trail maintains a distinctly Vermont personality: rugged, unpretentious, and beautifully challenging. The northern 150 miles, where the trail splits from the Appalachian Trail, offer some of the most technical and rewarding hiking in New England.

The trail passes through six wilderness areas within the Green Mountain National Forest, including the mystical Glastenbury Wilderness where several hikers mysteriously disappeared in the 1940s. From the rounded summits of southern Vermont to the dramatic peaks of Mount Mansfield and Camel's Hump, the long trail showcases Vermont's diverse mountain ecology. The path connects over 70 backcountry shelters and campsites, creating a network that supports both day hikers exploring local sections and determined thru-hikers tackling the entire route.

For those considering long-distance hiking, the Long Trail offers an ideal introduction to multi-week backpacking without requiring the time commitment of trails like the Pacific Crest Trail or Continental Divide Trail. The combination of readily available resupply points, maintained shelters, and Vermont's hiker-friendly communities creates a supportive environment for both novice and experienced long-distance hikers.

Planning Your Long Trail Adventure

Successfully hiking the long trail requires careful planning that balances ambition with realistic expectations. Most thru-hikers complete the trail in 20-30 days, though I've met speed hikers who finished in 16 days and others who savored every mile over six weeks. The key lies in matching your timeline to your experience level, fitness, and hiking goals.

Choosing Your Direction and Season

I strongly recommend hiking southbound (SOBO) starting in late September or early October. This direction follows the fall foliage from north to south while avoiding the peak summer crowds on the southern sections shared with the Appalachian Trail. Northbound (NOBO) hikers face steeper climbs in the challenging northern sections when they're most fatigued, though some prefer ending at the remote Canadian border for the symbolic achievement.

Timing is critical for the long trail success. Late spring brings "mud season" with trail closures designed to prevent erosion damage. Summer offers the longest daylight hours but also brings black flies, higher temperatures, and crowded shelters. My preferred window runs from late August through early October, when stable weather combines with spectacular foliage and fewer insects.

Permits and Logistics

Unlike many western trails, the long trail requires no permits for thru-hiking or section hiking. The Green Mountain Club maintains the shelters free of charge, though donations support their vital work. However, group size restrictions limit overnight parties to 10 people or fewer, and some areas have specific camping regulations to protect fragile ecosystems.

Transportation logistics deserve careful attention. The northern terminus near Jay, Vermont, requires shuttle service from Burlington airport or coordinating with trail angels. The southern terminus offers easier access via public transportation to North Adams, Massachusetts. I recommend booking shuttle services well in advance, especially during peak foliage season when demand exceeds supply.

Pro Tip: Consider purchasing the Long Trail Guide from the Green Mountain Club for detailed shelter information and trail updates that digital apps might miss.

Trail Logistics and Navigation

Navigation on the long trail follows a straightforward system that hasn't changed much since the 1930s. White blazes painted on trees mark the main trail, while double blazes indicate important turns or trail junctions. Side trails typically use blue blazes, creating a logical system that serves hikers well even in challenging weather conditions.

Shelter System and Camping

The Long Trail's shelter system represents one of its greatest strengths. Over 70 lean-tos, lodges, and tent sites dot the trail at roughly 5-mile intervals, though some sections require longer carries between accommodations. These three-sided structures provide protection from Vermont's unpredictable weather while fostering the trail community that makes the long trail special.

Popular shelters like Taft Lodge on Mount Mansfield or Little Rock Pond can fill quickly during peak season, especially on weekends. I learned to arrive at shelters by mid-afternoon to secure space, though tent sites near most shelters provide backup options. The "lodge" and "camp" designations typically indicate four-walled structures with doors, offering extra protection during storms.

Resupply Strategy

Resupply planning for the long trail benefits from Vermont's well-spaced towns and hiker-friendly businesses. Key resupply points include Manchester Center, Killington, Waterbury (for Ben & Jerry's factory tour), and Stowe. Most sections allow 3-5 day food carries, preventing excessive pack weight that could slow progress on the trail's challenging terrain.

The Inn at Long Trail near Killington offers a legendary stop for thru-hikers, serving their famous Long Trail Ale alongside hearty meals. For hikers needing quality gear during their journey, having reliable long-lasting walking boots becomes essential when facing Vermont's notoriously muddy and rocky terrain.

Essential Gear for Long Trail Success

Gear selection for the long trail requires balancing weight against Vermont's challenging conditions. The trail's reputation for mud, rocks, roots, and unpredictable weather demands equipment that prioritizes durability and weather protection over ultralight specifications.

Footwear Foundation

Your choice of footwear can make or break a Long Trail experience. After testing various options, I strongly recommend sturdy hiking boots over trail runners for the long trail. The combination of sharp rocks, persistent mud, and frequent stream crossings demands footwear that provides ankle support and waterproof protection.

Consider investing in quality long boots or specialized long walking boots for women and mens long walking boots depending on your specific needs. The Merrell Moab 2 Mid Waterproof consistently receives praise from Long Trail veterans for its durability and comfort on technical terrain.

Weather Protection Systems

Vermont weather changes rapidly, especially in the mountains. I carry a proven rain protection system including a durable rain jacket and rain pants—not just a lightweight shell that might work for day hiking. The Columbia Watertight II Rain Jacket offers excellent protection without breaking the budget.

For base layers and hiking clothes, prioritize moisture-wicking materials that dry quickly. Vermont's humidity can keep cotton clothing damp for days, leading to comfort issues and potential hypothermia risks during cool autumn nights.

Sleep System Considerations

Most Long Trail hikers rely on the shelter system, but carrying reliable backup shelter remains essential. I recommend a lightweight three-season tent or tarp that handles Vermont's frequent precipitation. The shelter floors consist of rough wooden planks, making a sleeping pad crucial for comfort and insulation.

Temperature ratings for sleeping bags should account for autumn mountain conditions, with nighttime temperatures potentially dropping into the 20s Fahrenheit by October. The comprehensive outdoor gear guide can help you select appropriate sleeping systems for various conditions.

Essential Gear Checklist: Waterproof boots, rain jacket and pants, three-season sleeping bag, reliable tent/tarp, trekking poles for the technical sections, water treatment system, and navigation tools including paper maps as backup.

My Experience Hiking the Long Trail

My first encounter with the long trail came during a crisp September morning at Journey's End, the northern terminus tucked away in Vermont's remote Northeast Kingdom. Standing at that modest sign marking the beginning of a 273-mile journey, I felt the mixture of excitement and apprehension that defines the start of any great adventure.

The Northern Challenge

The first week tested everything I thought I knew about hiking. The long trail's northern sections present relentless challenges: steep ascents followed immediately by technical descents, stream crossings without bridges, and the infamous "Vermont mud" that clings to everything. Jay Peak's alpine summit offered my first real glimpse of what lay ahead—endless ridges disappearing into the southern horizon.

By day four, I understood why veteran hikers speak of the Long Trail with both reverence and respect. Unlike the more groomed sections of the Appalachian Trail, this path maintains its wild character. I spent an entire afternoon navigating around blowdowns that blocked the trail, following faded blazes while scrambling over massive fallen trees.

Mount Mansfield: The Heart of Vermont

Reaching Mount Mansfield marked a turning point in my Long Trail journey. Vermont's highest peak demanded respect, with exposed ridges and unpredictable weather that reminded me why proper long outdoor boots and weather gear remain essential. The alpine zone above treeline revealed a completely different ecosystem—delicate plants clinging to life in harsh conditions.

Taft Lodge, perched near Mansfield's summit, provided an unforgettable night. Built in 1920 and rebuilt in 1996, this historic shelter buzzes with stories from generations of hikers. I shared the lodge with section hikers from Massachusetts, thru-hikers bound for the Canadian border, and local day hikers who knew every trail junction by heart.

Community and Solitude

What surprised me most about the long trail was the balance between solitude and community. Unlike the busy Appalachian Trail corridor, the northern Long Trail sections often provided complete solitude for hours at a time. Yet the shelter system created natural gathering points where hikers shared meals, weather reports, and trail wisdom.

I remember a particularly memorable evening at Camel's Hump Hut, where three generations of a Vermont family were section-hiking together. The grandfather had first hiked portions of the long trail in the 1960s, while his granddaughter was completing her first backpacking trip. Their stories illustrated how this trail weaves itself into Vermont's cultural fabric across decades.

"The Long Trail taught me that hiking isn't just about covering miles—it's about embracing the challenges and celebrating the small victories. Every muddy step, every spectacular vista, every shared moment in a shelter contributes to an experience that changes you."

Challenges and Rewards

The long trail presents unique challenges that distinguish it from other long-distance hiking routes. Understanding these difficulties—and the corresponding rewards—helps hikers prepare mentally and physically for this demanding adventure.

The Infamous Vermont Conditions

"Mud, rocks, and roots"—this trilogy defines much of the Long Trail hiking experience. Vermont's notorious mud isn't just a seasonal inconvenience; it's a year-round reality that demands specific strategies. I learned to embrace rather than fight the mud, finding that resistance only increases energy expenditure and frustration levels.

The technical nature of the long trail requires constant attention to foot placement. Rocky scrambles, root networks that create natural obstacles, and stream crossings without bridges slow hiking pace significantly compared to more groomed trails. What might be a 15-mile day on the Appalachian Trail becomes a challenging 10-mile day on the Long Trail's northern sections.

Weather adds another layer of complexity. Vermont's mountain weather changes rapidly, with clear morning skies giving way to afternoon thunderstorms or sudden temperature drops. I experienced a 40-degree temperature swing within six hours while crossing an exposed ridge, highlighting why versatile clothing systems and quality rain gear become essential investments rather than optional extras.

Physical and Mental Demands

The Long Trail's elevation profile reveals the truth about its difficulty—constant ups and downs with few flat sections for recovery. The trail gains and loses approximately 68,000 feet of elevation over its 273-mile length, creating what hikers call PUDs (Pointless Ups and Downs) that test endurance and mental resilience.

For hikers accustomed to western mountain terrain, where long climbs lead to extended ridgeline travel, the Long Trail's relentless undulation requires mental adjustment. Success depends on accepting slower progress and focusing on consistent forward movement rather than daily mileage goals.

Rewards Beyond Measure

The challenges of the long trail pale compared to its rewards. Vermont's autumn transformation creates some of the most spectacular hiking in North America, with brilliant maples, birches, and oaks painting entire mountainsides in scarlet, gold, and orange. Timing a thru-hike with peak foliage provides daily visual rewards that make every difficult climb worthwhile.

Wildlife encounters on the Long Trail surpass those of most eastern trails. I observed moose, black bears, and countless bird species while hiking through diverse ecosystems from lowland forests to alpine zones. The trail's relative remoteness, especially in northern sections, creates opportunities for authentic wilderness experiences rarely found in the crowded Northeast.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to hike the Long Trail?

Most thru-hikers complete the long trail in 20-30 days, averaging 9-14 miles per day. The fastest known time is under 5 days, achieved by elite ultrarunners, but most hikers prefer 3-4 weeks to fully enjoy the experience and account for weather delays. Section hikers often complete the trail over several years, tackling 20-50 mile segments during weekend trips or vacation periods.

What is the best time to hike the Long Trail?

Late summer through early fall (August-October) offers the best conditions for hiking the long trail. This period features fewer bugs, more stable weather patterns, and spectacular autumn foliage. Spring brings muddy conditions and seasonal trail closures to prevent erosion, while winter presents serious challenges including deep snow, ice, and extreme cold that require advanced winter camping skills and specialized equipment.

Do I need permits to hike the Long Trail?

No permits are required for hiking the long trail, whether for day hiking, section hiking, or thru-hiking. The Green Mountain Club maintains shelters and facilities through donations and volunteer work rather than permit fees. However, group size restrictions limit overnight parties to 10 people or fewer, and some areas within the Green Mountain National Forest have specific camping regulations to protect sensitive ecosystems.

How difficult is the Long Trail compared to other long-distance trails?

The Long Trail presents unique challenges that differ from western long-distance trails. While shorter than trails like the Pacific Crest Trail or Continental Divide Trail, the long trail's technical terrain, persistent mud, and constant elevation changes create slower hiking pace and higher energy demands per mile. The excellent shelter system and shorter overall distance make it ideal for first-time thru-hikers who want to experience long-distance hiking without committing to a 6-month journey.

What gear is essential for Long Trail success?

Essential gear for the long trail includes waterproof hiking boots with ankle support, comprehensive rain gear (jacket and pants), three-season sleeping bag, reliable shelter, trekking poles for technical sections, and water treatment system. The Ultimate Hiker's Gear Guide provides detailed recommendations for selecting equipment that balances weight with Vermont's demanding conditions. Quality walking boots become particularly important given the trail's notorious mud and rocky terrain.
