Nike Zegama 2: Maximum Cushion for Ultra Distances

The Zegama 2 addresses the biggest criticism of its predecessor by introducing a Vibram outsole, finally giving Nike trail shoes the grip they desperately needed. This is Nike's answer to ultra-cushioned trail shoes like the Hoka Speedgoat, and after running a 50K trail race in them, I can attest to their effectiveness for long-distance efforts.

Named after the famous Zegama-Aizkorri marathon in Spain, this shoe is built for serious mountain running. The ZoomX foam midsole provides exceptional energy return, while the updated Vibram Litebase outsole delivers confident traction on loose and wet surfaces. During my testing in the Santa Cruz Mountains, I noticed significantly better grip compared to previous Nike trail models, especially on steep descents.

Performance Highlights:

  • 37mm heel stack height for maximum cushioning
  • Vibram Litebase outsole with 4mm lugs
  • ZoomX foam for superior energy return
  • Best for: Long trail runs, ultras, recovery runs
  • Weight: 11.2 oz (men's size 10)

The trade-off for all this cushioning is weight – the Zegama 2 is noticeably heavier than the Pegasus Trail 5. However, for runs over 15 miles, the extra cushioning becomes a significant advantage. I've completed several 20+ mile trail runs in these shoes with minimal foot fatigue, something I couldn't say about the original Zegama.

Sizing and Fit Considerations

Nike trail shoes generally fit consistent with other Nike running models, but several factors influence the ideal size selection. Through fitting dozens of runners at local trail running events, I've observed these patterns:

Most runners find their normal Nike size appropriate for the Pegasus Trail 5, with the shoe offering adequate toe room for longer runs. However, the Zegama 2 runs slightly narrow, and I recommend trying on or considering a half-size up if you have wider feet or prefer extra room for long runs.

Sizing Tips from My Testing:

  • Order your normal Nike size for Pegasus Trail 5

For runners coming from other trail shoe brands, the transition to Nike often requires a brief adjustment period. The midsole firmness differs from heavily cushioned brands like Hoka, while the heel-to-toe drop may feel different from zero-drop or minimal shoes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Nike trail shoes good for hiking?

Yes, Nike trail shoes are excellent for day hiking and light backpacking. Models like the Pegasus Trail 5 offer great versatility for both trail running and hiking, with adequate grip and cushioning for most trail conditions. However, for technical mountaineering or heavy backpacking, dedicated hiking boots might be more appropriate. I've personally used them on numerous day hikes throughout California's national parks, and they perform admirably on everything from smooth fire roads to rocky scrambles. The lighter weight compared to traditional hiking boots reduces fatigue during long days on the trail, while still providing adequate protection and traction for most hiking scenarios.

How do Nike trail shoes fit compared to regular Nike running shoes?

Nike trail shoes generally fit similar to regular Nike running shoes, but many models have a slightly wider toe box to accommodate foot swelling during long runs. The Pegasus Trail series fits true to size, while the Zegama tends to run slightly narrow. I recommend trying them on or ordering a half size up if you're between sizes, especially for the waterproof GORE-TEX versions. The key difference I've noticed is in the heel construction – trail models often have more substantial heel counters for stability on uneven terrain, which can initially feel different if you're used to lightweight road shoes. Most runners adapt quickly to this difference, and it provides valuable support during technical trail sections.

What's the difference between Nike Pegasus Trail 5 and regular Pegasus?

The main differences are the outsole and upper construction. The Trail 5 features a more aggressive tread pattern with better grip for off-road terrain, a more durable upper with protective overlays, and slightly firmer cushioning for stability on uneven surfaces. The regular Pegasus is optimized for road running with smoother tread and lighter weight. During my testing, I found the Trail 5 adds about 0.8 ounces compared to the road version, but this weight increase brings significant benefits in terms of durability and traction. The midsole geometry is also slightly different, with the trail version offering a more stable platform for lateral movements common in trail running. Both shoes share Nike's React foam technology, ensuring similar comfort levels despite their different intended uses.

How long do Nike trail shoes last?

Based on my experience, Nike trail shoes typically last 300-500 miles depending on the model and terrain. The Zegama 2 and Wildhorse series tend to be the most durable, often exceeding 400 miles. The Pegasus Trail series usually provides 300-350 miles of reliable performance. Rocky, technical terrain will reduce lifespan compared to smoother dirt trails. I track my mileage carefully and have found that Nike trail shoes compare favorably to other premium brands in terms of durability. The outsole rubber compounds seem particularly resistant to wear, while the midsole foam maintains its cushioning properties well beyond what I expected from earlier Nike models. Proper rotation between multiple pairs and appropriate use for intended terrain significantly extends the lifespan of any trail shoe.

Are Nike trail shoes waterproof?

Nike offers GORE-TEX versions of several trail models including the Pegasus Trail 5 GTX and Juniper Trail 2 GTX. These provide excellent waterproof protection but reduce breathability. The regular versions are water-resistant but not fully waterproof. For consistently wet conditions, I recommend the GORE-TEX models, but for most trail running, the standard versions offer better breathability. During my testing in various weather conditions, the GORE-TEX versions kept my feet completely dry during stream crossings and puddle running, but they did get noticeably warmer during high-intensity efforts. The standard versions handle light rain and wet grass well, but will eventually let water through during prolonged exposure to standing water or heavy downpours. Consider your typical running conditions and climate when choosing between waterproof and standard versions.
