The Rise and Fall of Vasque: A 60-Year Legacy

The story of Vasque shoes begins in 1964 when Italian cobbler Domenico Brustia founded the company with a vision to bring European craftsmanship to American hiking boots. I remember the first time I heard about Vasque from an old-timer at Yellowstone's backcountry office in 1998. "Those Italian Sundowners will last you a lifetime," he told me, and he wasn't wrong about the early models.

The Golden Years: Italian Craftsmanship

During the 1970s and 1980s, Vasque shoes were synonymous with quality. The original Italian-made boots featured full-grain leather uppers, Vibram soles, and could be resoled multiple times. I've spoken with countless hikers who still wear their vintage Vasque boots from this era, testament to the exceptional build quality.

The Sundowner model, in particular, became legendary among serious backpackers. These heavy-duty leather boots required significant break-in time but rewarded hikers with decades of reliable service. Park rangers throughout the Rocky Mountains swore by them, and for good reason - they could handle everything from scree fields to creek crossings without complaint.

The Decline: Production Moves to China

Everything changed when Red Wing Shoe Company acquired Vasque and moved production to China in the early 2000s. While this reduced costs, it also marked the beginning of quality issues that would plague the brand until its recent discontinuation. The difference was immediately noticeable to longtime Vasque users - synthetic materials replaced leather, construction became less robust, and durability plummeted.

In October 2024, Red Wing announced the heartbreaking news that Vasque would cease operations entirely. After 60 years of serving the outdoor community, changing market conditions and declining sales forced this difficult decision. For those of us who grew up with Vasque hiking boots on our feet, this felt like losing an old friend.

Vasque Sundowner: The Legendary Workhorse

No discussion of Vasque shoes would be complete without the Sundowner. These full-leather, heavy-duty boots defined what serious backpacking footwear should be. While they weighed nearly three pounds per pair, they offered unmatched durability and support for multi-day adventures with heavy packs.

I still encounter hikers on remote trails wearing decades-old Sundowners, and they inevitably share stories of thousands of miles logged without major issues. The original Italian-made versions are now collector's items, selling for premium prices on eBay when they appear.

As hiking trends shifted toward lighter footwear in the 2010s, I transitioned to the Breeze series. Seasonal Considerations and Lessons Learned

Through years of testing, I learned that Vasque shoes performed best in specific conditions. The heavier leather models excelled in cooler weather and technical terrain but became uncomfortably hot during summer desert hiking. The lighter Breeze models offered better breathability but sacrificed some durability on abrasive surfaces.

One hard-learned lesson came during a winter attempt on Mount Whitney, where my older Vasque boots' leather absorbed moisture and froze solid overnight. This experience taught me the importance of proper boot care and the limitations of traditional materials in extreme conditions.

The decision came after years of declining sales and changing market conditions that made the brand financially unviable. While existing inventory may still be available through various retailers, no new production is planned. This marks the end of a significant era in outdoor footwear history.

What happened to Vasque shoes?

Vasque was discontinued due to changing market conditions and consumer preferences. The brand struggled with declining sales and quality issues after moving production from Italy to China in the early 2000s. Red Wing Shoe Company, which owned Vasque, determined that there was no viable path forward for the brand. The outdoor footwear market had shifted toward lighter trail runners and more technical boots, leaving traditional brands like Vasque struggling to maintain relevance. Additionally, increased competition from newer brands offering similar features at lower price points contributed to the decision.
