Frequently Asked Questions

Are the Salomon Quest 4 GTX worth the price?

Based on my testing and analysis of user feedback, the Quest 4 GTX justifies its $230 price point for serious hikers. The combination of superior ankle support, reliable waterproofing, and exceptional traction makes them excellent value for technical hiking and backpacking. However, casual day hikers might find better value in lighter, less expensive options. The boots excel in challenging conditions where their features truly matter â€“ multi-day trips, heavy pack loads, and technical terrain.

How do they compare to the previous Quest 4D models?

The Quest 4 GTX represents an evolution from the beloved Quest 4D series, with several key improvements. The new ADV-C 4D chassis provides better lateral stability, while the updated Contagrip TA outsole offers superior grip. Weight has been reduced compared to the 4D 3 GTX, making them more versatile. However, some long-time users report concerns about durability compared to earlier generations. The fit has also evolved, with a slightly wider toe box that accommodates more foot shapes. For detailed comparisons, our Quest 4D 3 GTX comparison provides additional insights.

What's the break-in period like?

One of the Quest 4 GTX's standout features is the minimal break-in period. Thanks to the synthetic materials in the forefoot and the boot's modern construction, most hikers can wear them immediately for moderate distances. I personally hiked 10 miles on my first day without blisters or hot spots. The SensiFit technology and Ortholite sockliner contribute to immediate comfort. However, I still recommend starting with shorter hikes to allow your feet to adapt to the boot's support system. The high ankle collar may feel restrictive initially if you're used to low-cut shoes, but this adjusts quickly as you appreciate the stability it provides.

How waterproof are they really?

The Gore-Tex membrane in the Quest 4 GTX provides excellent waterproofing in real-world conditions. During my testing, they remained completely dry through stream crossings, puddle walking, and wet grass. The 5.5-inch flood height means you can step into deeper water than most hiking boots allow. However, waterproofing performance depends on proper care â€“ the DWR coating on the exterior needs periodic reapplication. In extremely wet conditions lasting multiple days, some moisture may eventually penetrate through the upper materials, though the Gore-Tex membrane itself remains effective. For comparison with other waterproof options, check our Salomon GTX series overview.

Are they good for winter hiking?

The Quest 4 GTX performs well in winter conditions, though they're not specifically designed as winter boots. The waterproofing keeps feet dry in snow, and the aggressive tread provides good traction on packed snow and ice. However, for serious winter hiking or mountaineering, you might want to consider the Quest series' dedicated winter models or add microspikes for icy conditions. The boot's breathability, while excellent for three-season use, means they're not the warmest option for extreme cold. I've used them comfortably down to about 20Â°F with quality wool socks. For more winter-specific options, explore our 4D GTX winter models.

How do they fit compared to other hiking boots?

The Quest 4 GTX fits true to size for most hikers, with a medium-width profile that accommodates average feet well. The toe box offers generous room, which I appreciate for long descents when feet swell. However, Salomon doesn't offer width variations, which can be problematic for very wide or narrow feet. The heel cup is secure without being overly tight, and the ankle collar provides snug support without restriction. I recommend trying them on in-store if possible, as individual foot shape greatly affects fit. Compared to brands like Merrell or Keen, the Quest 4 feels more precision-fit rather than accommodating.
