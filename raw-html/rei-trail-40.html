What Makes the REI Trail 40 Special

The REI Trail 40 isn't just another hiking backpack—it's a carefully engineered piece of outdoor equipment that bridges the gap between day hiking and multi-day adventures. At $149, this pack delivers features typically found in backpacks costing twice as much, making it an exceptional choice for budget-conscious outdoor enthusiasts.

What sets the REI Trail 40 apart is its unique U-shaped panel opening system, which provides full access to the main compartment without the frustration of digging through layers of gear. Unlike traditional top-loading packs, this design allows you to pack and unpack efficiently, whether you're organizing gear at home or searching for items on the trail.

The pack features an internal steel perimeter frame that distributes weight effectively across your torso, enabling comfortable carries of up to 35 pounds for extended periods. This structural support, combined with the well-padded shoulder straps and hip belt, creates a carrying experience that rivals much more expensive packs.

Perhaps most importantly, the Trail 40 comes in three different sizes for both men and women, including dedicated plus-size options. This inclusive sizing approach ensures that hikers of all body types can find a proper fit, which is crucial for comfort and safety on the trail. The attention to fit extends to the shoulder strap width, hip belt proportions, and torso length adjustments.

Detailed Features and Performance Analysis

Comfort and Fit System

The REI Trail 40's comfort system is where it truly shines for the price point. The internal steel frame provides excellent load transfer, while the generous padding on both the backpanel and shoulder straps prevents hot spots during long carries. During my testing on multi-day trips through Olympic National Park, the pack remained comfortable even when loaded to its 35-pound capacity.

The hip belt deserves special mention—it's substantially padded and features dual zippered pockets large enough for smartphones, snacks, and other quick-access items. Unlike many budget packs that include minimalist hip belts, the Trail 40's belt actually carries a significant portion of the load, reducing shoulder strain considerably.

Storage and Organization

Organization is where the Trail 40 excels beyond its price point. The pack offers six exterior pockets plus the main compartment, providing ample space for systematic gear organization. The front stretch pocket can accommodate a rain jacket or extra layer, while the dual side pockets easily hold 32-ounce water bottles.

Inside the main compartment, you'll find two mesh pockets and a dedicated hydration sleeve. The hydration system routing is well-designed, with secure clip points along the shoulder straps. However, it's worth noting that while the pack can accommodate a hydration bladder, the sleeve isn't padded enough for laptop protection during travel use.

Durability and Weather Resistance

Constructed from 210-denier ripstop nylon with a DWR coating, the Trail 40 strikes a good balance between weight and durability. The fabric resists light precipitation effectively, though it's not fully waterproof. REI includes a rain cover in a dedicated bottom pocket—a thoughtful touch that many competing packs charge extra for.

After two years of regular use across various conditions, from desert heat in Joshua Tree to wet conditions in the Cascades, my test pack shows minimal wear. The YKK zippers operate smoothly, and the mesh pockets have maintained their elasticity without tears.

Pro Tip

For maximum weather protection, line the pack with a compactor bag rather than relying solely on the rain cover. This provides better water protection and can double as a pack liner for organization.

Real-World Testing and My Experience

Three-Day Olympic Peninsula Adventure

My most comprehensive test of the REI Trail 40 came during a three-day solo hiking trip through Olympic National Park's Hoh River trail system. Loading the pack with a full three-season kit including tent, sleeping bag, cook system, and provisions, the total weight reached 32 pounds—well within the pack's comfort zone.

The U-shaped zipper system proved invaluable for camp organization. Instead of unpacking everything to reach items at the bottom, I could access any part of the main compartment quickly. This feature became especially useful during a sudden afternoon thunderstorm when I needed to quickly grab rain gear without soaking the rest of my equipment.

On steep terrain, the pack's load lifters helped bring the weight closer to my center of gravity, reducing the pull-back sensation common with top-heavy loads. The chest strap, which includes an integrated safety whistle, kept the shoulder straps properly positioned during challenging scrambles.

Urban Travel Performance

Beyond hiking, I've extensively used the Trail 40 as a travel backpack on domestic flights. Its dimensions (20.5" x 12" x 9.5" for the medium size) meet most airline carry-on requirements, though it's worth checking specific airline policies for international travel.

The pack's low-profile design moves well through crowded airports and train stations. The multiple external pockets keep travel documents, electronics, and snacks easily accessible without opening the main compartment. For extended travel, the REI Trail 25 might be more appropriate for minimalist packers.

Seasonal Considerations

Spring hiking in the Pacific Northwest challenged the pack's water resistance capabilities. While the DWR coating handles light drizzle effectively, prolonged exposure to heavy rain does result in some moisture intrusion through the zippers. The included rain cover helps, but proper waterproofing requires internal organization with dry bags.

Summer desert hiking revealed the pack's ventilation limitations. The foam-heavy back panel doesn't breathe as well as suspended mesh designs, leading to noticeable back sweat during hot conditions. However, the trade-off in load stability is generally worth it for most users.

Winter use with snowshoes and additional layers pushes the 40-liter capacity to its limits. For serious winter hiking, consider the larger capacity options in REI's lineup or supplement with external attachment points for bulky gear.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the REI Trail 40 suitable for international carry-on requirements?

The REI Trail 40's dimensions generally meet most international carry-on requirements, but policies vary significantly between airlines and regions. The pack measures 20.5" x 12" x 9.5" in medium size, which typically complies with US domestic carriers and many European airlines. However, budget carriers often have stricter size limitations. Always check your specific airline's requirements before traveling, and consider that when fully packed, the soft-sided construction may expand slightly beyond stated dimensions.

How does the Trail 40 perform for multi-day backpacking trips?

The Trail 40 excels for 2-3 day backpacking trips with proper gear selection. Its 40-liter capacity accommodates a three-season setup including shelter, sleeping system, cooking gear, and provisions when using modern lightweight equipment. The internal steel frame and padded hip belt provide excellent load support for weights up to 35 pounds. However, for extended trips over four days or winter conditions requiring bulky gear, you'll likely need the larger capacity found in traditional backpacking packs like 50-65 liter models.

What's the difference between men's and women's versions of the Trail 40?

The primary differences lie in fit specifications rather than features. Women's versions have narrower shoulder strap spacing, shorter torso lengths, and hip belts shaped to accommodate wider hip angles. The men's version offers larger size ranges including XXL-4XL options. Both versions include identical pocket configurations, materials, and organizational features. The weight difference is minimal, with women's sizes typically being 2-3 ounces lighter due to smaller overall dimensions. Choose based on your body measurements rather than gender for the best fit.

Can the Trail 40 accommodate a full-size laptop for digital nomad use?

While the Trail 40's hydration sleeve can technically fit most 15" laptops, it lacks dedicated padding for electronics protection. The sleeve is designed primarily for hydration bladders and doesn't provide adequate cushioning for safe laptop transport. For digital nomad use, consider adding a separate padded laptop sleeve or case within the main compartment. The pack's organization system works well for travel, but it's optimized more for outdoor gear than electronic equipment. Purpose-built travel backpacks might better serve pure digital nomad needs.

How does the Trail 40 compare to previous generations of the same pack?

The current Trail 40 represents significant improvements over earlier versions. Key enhancements include a redesigned top pocket that's larger and easier to access, improved hip belt padding, and better durability in the rain cover storage system. The hydration system has been updated with more secure attachment points, and the shoulder straps feature improved contouring. The capacity remains consistent at 40 liters, but the updated design maximizes usable space more effectively. Users upgrading from older versions consistently report improved comfort and functionality with the current generation.

What maintenance is required to keep the Trail 40 in optimal condition?

Regular maintenance extends the Trail 40's lifespan significantly. After each trip, empty all pockets and compartments, brush off dirt and debris, and spot clean with mild soap and water as needed. The DWR coating benefits from periodic reapplication using products like Nikwax or similar treatments, especially after multiple washings. Store the pack uncompressed in a dry location to maintain shape and prevent mildew. Inspect zippers regularly and lubricate with zipper wax if they become stiff. Check webbing and stitching for wear, particularly at high-stress points like hip belt attachment areas and load lifter connections.
