Construction and Key Features

The Oboz Sawtooth II Mid represents a significant evolution in hiking boot design, incorporating over a decade of user feedback and technological advancement. Having tested these boots extensively across various terrains, I can confidently say they've addressed many of the shortcomings found in their predecessors while maintaining the reliability that made Oboz a trusted name among outdoor enthusiasts.

The upper construction features premium nubuck leather paired with strategically placed mesh panels for optimal breathability. What immediately caught my attention during my first inspection was the asymmetrical collar design – a thoughtful engineering choice that provides enhanced ankle support while preventing the uncomfortable pressure points I've experienced with other mid-height boots. The collar sits perfectly around the ankle bone, offering protection without restricting natural movement.

At the heart of these boots lies Oboz's proprietary O FIT Insole, which has been specifically molded to match the boot's internal shape. During my initial fitting, I was impressed by the immediate arch support and cushioning – no break-in period required for basic comfort. The insole features dual-density EVA construction with targeted support zones that cradle the foot's natural contours.

Key Technical Specifications

  • Weight: 2 lbs 2.6 oz (per pair, size 10)
  • Upper Material: Nubuck leather with textile mesh panels
  • Waterproofing: B-DRY waterproof/breathable membrane
  • Midsole: Dual-density EVA with nylon shank
  • Outsole: Oboz Trail Tread rubber compound
  • Height: Mid-cut (over-the-ankle support)

The B-DRY waterproof membrane deserves special attention. Unlike traditional Gore-Tex alternatives, B-DRY uses a proprietary construction that Oboz claims offers superior breathability while maintaining complete waterproof protection. In my testing, this proved largely accurate – the boots managed moisture effectively during high-output activities while keeping my feet dry during stream crossings and unexpected downpours.

Comfort and Fit Analysis

Comfort in hiking boots extends far beyond initial impressions – it's about how they perform during mile 15 when your feet are swollen, on steep descents when your toes are pressing forward, and during multi-day adventures when every pressure point becomes magnified. The Oboz Sawtooth II Mid has consistently impressed me across all these scenarios.

Sizing and Fit Characteristics

Based on my experience and feedback from fellow hikers, these boots run true to size with a medium width profile. I typically wear a size 10.5 in most hiking boots, and the same size in the Sawtooth II Mid provided perfect accommodation with hiking socks. The toe box offers adequate room for natural toe splay without feeling cavernous, while the heel cup provides secure lockdown that prevents slippage during steep ascents.

One notable aspect is the graduated lacing system. The lower eyelets allow for precise adjustment of forefoot pressure, while the upper hooks enable quick customization of ankle support. During my testing across various terrains, I found myself frequently adjusting the lacing – loose for flat terrain hiking and snug for technical scrambles.

Long-Distance Comfort Performance

The true test of any hiking boot comes during extended use. On a particularly challenging 18-mile day hike in the Sawtooth Range (fitting, given the boot's name), these boots maintained their comfort throughout the journey. The arch support remained consistent, preventing the foot fatigue that often plagues me during long days. The cushioning in the forefoot area, while initially feeling modest, proved adequate for rocky terrain and maintained its responsiveness even after hours of use.

Temperature regulation proved excellent during my testing. The combination of mesh panels and breathable lining prevented the swampy conditions I've experienced with less ventilated boots. Even during summer hiking in temperatures exceeding 85°F, my feet remained reasonably comfortable, though I wouldn't recommend these for extreme heat conditions where low-cut trail runners might be more appropriate.

Real-World Performance Testing

Laboratory tests and manufacturer specifications tell only part of the story. Real-world performance emerges through countless miles of varied terrain, weather conditions, and the gradual revelation of both strengths and limitations. My comprehensive testing of the Oboz Sawtooth II Mid spanned eight months across four distinct seasons and multiple ecosystems.

Traction and Stability Analysis

The Trail Tread outsole compound delivers exceptional performance across most surfaces. On granite slabs in Yosemite, the rubber compound provided confident grip even on slightly damp surfaces. The lug pattern, while not as aggressive as some dedicated scrambling boots, offers versatile performance from loose scree to hardpacked dirt trails. I was particularly impressed during a challenging descent in the North Cascades where wet roots and muddy conditions tested every aspect of the boot's traction capabilities.

However, these boots do show limitations on extremely technical terrain. During a climb involving sustained rock scrambling, I noticed the relatively shallow lug depth struggled with mud shedding compared to boots specifically designed for such conditions. For typical hiking and light scrambling, though, the traction is more than adequate.

Waterproof Performance Testing

The B-DRY waterproof membrane faced rigorous testing throughout my evaluation period. Stream crossings in early spring runoff, hiking through persistent rain in the Pacific Northwest, and even accidental submersion during a river crossing all provided opportunities to assess the waterproof capabilities. In every scenario, the boots maintained complete water protection while allowing noticeable breathability.

One particularly noteworthy test occurred during a three-day backpacking trip in Olympic National Park, where continuous rain and muddy conditions provided the ultimate waterproof challenge. After 72 hours of wet conditions, my feet remained completely dry, and the boots showed no signs of water penetration. The breathability prevented the clammy conditions that often accompany waterproof footwear during extended use.

Performance Limitations to Consider

  • Limited performance in deep snow without additional traction devices
  • Heavier than trail runners for ultralight hiking applications
  • Break-in period required for optimal ankle collar comfort
  • Not ideal for extreme heat conditions (90°F+)

My Personal Experience

As someone who has guided over 200 hiking trips across North America's most challenging terrain, I've developed particular preferences and standards for hiking footwear. The Oboz Sawtooth II Mid entered my gear rotation skeptically – I'd been burned by overly marketed boots before. However, after eight months of rigorous use, these boots have earned a permanent place in my gear closet.

What impressed me most was the boots' consistency – they performed equally well whether navigating loose talus at 11,000 feet or crossing marshy areas near treeline.

Seasonal Performance Observations

Spring Conditions: During peak snowmelt season in the Cascades, these boots handled variable conditions exceptionally well. The waterproofing proved crucial when postholing through late-season snow, while the breathability prevented overheating during steep ascents in warming temperatures. I paired them with lightweight gaiters for additional protection against snow entry.

Summer Performance: High-country hiking in Colorado and Utah revealed both strengths and limitations. The boots excel on rocky terrain and provide excellent ankle protection on loose scree. However, during extreme heat waves (temperatures exceeding 95°F), I found them too warm for comfortable all-day wear. For most summer conditions, though, they performed admirably.

Fall Adventures: Autumn hiking presented ideal conditions for these boots. The waterproofing handled wet leaves and intermittent rain, while the insulation proved perfect for cool mornings that warmed throughout the day. Several ranger friends I spoke with during this season specifically praised these boots for their versatility during shoulder season conditions.

Winter Limitations: While not designed as winter boots, I tested them during early winter conditions with microspikes attached. They provided adequate insulation for temperatures down to about 20°F with appropriate socks, but I wouldn't recommend them for serious winter mountaineering or extended cold-weather exposure.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do Oboz Sawtooth II Mid boots fit compared to other hiking boots?

The Oboz Sawtooth II Mid runs true to size with a medium width profile. They fit similarly to Merrell Moab boots but with a more secure heel cup. The toe box provides adequate room without being overly spacious. I recommend trying them on with your preferred hiking socks, and if you have wide feet, consider sizing up half a size or looking for wide width options. The asymmetrical collar design accommodates ankle bones better than many competitors, reducing pressure points during extended wear.

Are these boots suitable for winter hiking and snow conditions?

The Oboz Sawtooth II Mid can handle light winter conditions and shoulder season hiking, but they're not designed as dedicated winter boots. They provide adequate insulation down to approximately 20°F when paired with appropriate wool socks. The waterproofing helps with slushy conditions, and the tread pattern works well with microspikes for traction. However, for serious winter mountaineering, deep snow, or extended cold weather exposure below 15°F, I'd recommend dedicated insulated winter boots. They excel in three-season conditions and early winter day hikes.

How long do Oboz Sawtooth II Mid boots typically last?

Based on my testing and user feedback, these boots typically last 500-800 miles depending on terrain and hiking style. The nubuck leather upper and reinforced construction contribute to exceptional durability. I've seen pairs with over 600 miles that still had significant tread depth and intact waterproofing. The O FIT Insole may compress after 300-400 miles but remains supportive. Oboz offers a one-year warranty against manufacturing defects, and their customer service is responsive to legitimate durability concerns. Proper care including cleaning and occasional waterproofing treatment can extend their lifespan significantly.

Can I use these boots for backpacking with a heavy pack?

Yes, the Oboz Sawtooth II Mid performs excellently for backpacking applications. The nylon shank provides adequate support for loads up to 40-50 pounds, while the dual-density EVA midsole offers good cushioning for extended wear. The ankle support is particularly beneficial when carrying a pack over uneven terrain. I've successfully used them on multi-day trips with 35-pound packs without issues. The durable construction handles the additional stress well. However, for very heavy loads (50+ pounds) or extended backpacking trips, you might consider more robust mountaineering boots, though these work well for typical backpacking scenarios.

What's the difference between the waterproof and non-waterproof versions?

The waterproof version features Oboz's B-DRY membrane, adding complete water protection while maintaining breathability. It costs about $15-20 more than the non-waterproof version. The non-waterproof model offers superior breathability and is lighter but provides no water protection beyond the leather upper's natural water resistance. Choose waterproof for varied conditions, stream crossings, and wet climates. The non-waterproof version is better for desert hiking, hot conditions, and situations where maximum breathability is prioritized. Both share the same construction, comfort, and durability characteristics. In my experience, the waterproof version's versatility justifies the modest price increase for most hikers.

How do I properly care for and maintain these boots?

Proper care extends the life and performance of your Oboz Sawtooth II Mid boots significantly. After each use, remove dirt and debris with a soft brush, paying attention to the tread pattern and seams. For deeper cleaning, use lukewarm water and mild soap, avoiding harsh detergents. Allow them to air dry naturally away from direct heat sources. Apply leather conditioner every 3-4 months or after 50-75 miles of use to maintain the nubuck leather's water resistance and flexibility. For the waterproof version, reapply DWR treatment annually or when water stops beading on the surface. Store them in a cool, dry place with cedar shoe trees to maintain shape. Replace laces when they show wear to prevent failure on the trail.
