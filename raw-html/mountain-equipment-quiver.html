Key Features

  • Helmet-compatible Super Alpine Hood
  • 2-way underarm pit zippers

  • Articulated sleeve design
  • Laminated adjustable cuffs
  • YKK AquaGuard zippers throughout

The heart of the Mountain Equipment Quiver lies in its Gore-Tex Pro construction, utilizing the most robust version of Gore's waterproof-breathable technology. This 3-layer design bonds the membrane directly to both face and lining fabrics, creating a streamlined package that eliminates the bulk and weight of traditional shell-liner-membrane constructions.

Having tested various Mountain Equipment jackets over the years, I can attest that the Quiver's 30-denier nylon face fabric strikes an optimal balance between durability and weight. While some ultralight shells use 20D fabrics, the Quiver's 30D construction provides crucial abrasion resistance against ski edges, crampon contact, and rock abrasion common in alpine environments.

Pro Tip from Experience: The laminated cuff design deserves special mention—unlike traditional Velcro adjustments that ice up and lose functionality, these laminated cuffs remain adjustable even when frozen, a crucial detail I appreciated during a dawn start on Mont Blanc's Cosmiques Ridge.

The pocket configuration reflects the jacket's ski mountaineering focus: two large hand pockets positioned for harness compatibility, plus an internal mesh pocket for essentials. Notably absent are ski pass pockets and media ports—deliberate omissions that reduce weight and complexity while maintaining the jacket's serious alpine character. Performance in Varied Conditions

During a memorable tour in the Bernese Oberland, the Quiver demonstrated its weather protection credentials through a six-hour approach in driving snow and wind. The helmet-compatible hood remained stable without peripheral vision obstruction, while the jacket's weather protection never wavered. However, the fabric's appearance changed noticeably after contact with my ski edges during a technical descent—superficial scratches that didn't affect performance but highlighted the material's visibility of wear.

The removable powder skirt proved invaluable during deep powder days, though I discovered the importance of proper integration with Mountain Equipment pants for optimal function. Without compatible pants, the skirt's effectiveness diminished significantly, making this more of a system piece than standalone garment.

Lesson Learned: The Quiver's packability became a game-changer during long tours where weather uncertainty required carrying shells "just in case." After three seasons of use, the Quiver has become my go-to shell for serious ski mountaineering objectives, though I've learned to size up and accept that its durability, while adequate, requires more care than burlier alternatives.
