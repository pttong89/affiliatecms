Frequently Asked Questions

Are Shoe the Bear boots worth the investment for long-term wear?

How do I determine the correct size when ordering online?

Accurate sizing requires careful attention to the brand's specific measurements rather than relying on general conversion charts. Start by measuring both feet in centimeters, as European sizing is based on foot length. Most models run true to size, but the York series tends to fit narrowly while the Stanley model runs small. If you're between sizes, customer feedback suggests sizing up, especially for the narrower models. Contact customer service with your measurements for personalized recommendations. Remember that returns to Denmark can be costly, so investing time in proper sizing research prevents expensive mistakes and ensures a satisfactory purchase experience.

What's the best way to care for Shoe the Bear suede and leather boots?

Proper care significantly extends the life of your shoe the bear boots and maintains their premium appearance. For suede models, apply a water-repellent spray before first wear and reapply monthly during heavy use seasons. Use a suede brush to restore texture and remove light scuffs. Leather versions benefit from regular conditioning with high-quality leather cream and periodic polishing. Store all boots with cedar shoe trees to maintain shape and absorb moisture. Rotate between pairs to allow complete drying between wears. Professional cleaning every 6-12 months helps address deeper stains and provides expert conditioning. Avoid wearing the same pair daily, as this prevents proper ventilation and accelerates wear patterns.

Can Shoe the Bear boots be resoled when the outsole wears down?

The resoling potential depends on the specific construction method used for each model. Most shoe the bear Chelsea boots use cemented construction rather than traditional Goodyear welting, which limits resoling options compared to premium dress boots. However, skilled cobblers can often replace worn outsoles, though the cost may approach 50-70% of a new pair's price. Before the sole wears through completely, consider adding rubber sole protectors or heel taps to extend the original sole's life. Some customers report success with local cobblers for basic repairs like heel replacement. For boots in the $100-150 range, replacement might be more economical than extensive resoling, but individual assessment by a qualified cobbler provides the best guidance for your specific situation.
