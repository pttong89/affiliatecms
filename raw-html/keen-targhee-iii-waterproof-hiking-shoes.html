Overview & First Impressions

The KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes represent the evolution of one of hiking's most recognizable silhouettes. Released in 2017 as an update to the beloved Targhee II, these shoes promised improved fit, better breathability, and enhanced agility. However, my three-year journey with multiple pairs has revealed both impressive strengths and concerning inconsistencies that every potential buyer should understand.

What immediately strikes you about the KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes is their robust construction. The nubuck leather upper feels substantial without being overly stiff, while the signature KEEN toe protection extends well beyond what you'll find on most competitors. The KEEN.DRY waterproof membrane promises breathability alongside weather protection, though real-world performance varies significantly with conditions.

Performance Analysis

Waterproof Performance

The KEEN.DRY membrane in the KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes delivers solid weather protection, though with important caveats. During my tests crossing streams in Mount Rainier National Park and hiking through morning dew in the Smokies, these shoes kept my feet completely dry for the first 18 months of use. The fully gusseted tongue creates an effective barrier against debris and moisture entry.

However, breathability becomes a significant issue during summer hiking. Unlike other KEEN Targhee III waterproof models, the low-cut version offers limited ventilation. Rangers at Yosemite specifically warned me about this during my July visit, noting that many hikers develop hot spots from excessive moisture retention in warm conditions.

Traction & Stability

The KEEN.ALL-TERRAIN outsole provides exceptional grip on most surfaces. With 4.5mm lug depth, these shoes handle loose gravel, muddy sections, and moderate rock scrambling with confidence. The triangular lug pattern in the center transitions to more aggressive perimeter lugs, creating versatility across terrain types.

Where they struggle is on wet rock surfaces. During a challenging descent in Glacier National Park's slick limestone sections, I experienced several near-slips that wouldn't have occurred with Vibram Megagrip soles. The rubber compound, while durable, lacks the sticky quality needed for technical terrain.

Comfort & Support

Comfort in the KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes depends heavily on proper fit and break-in patience. The CMEVA midsole provides moderate cushioning—enough for day hikes but lacking for long-distance backpacking. At 17.1 ounces per shoe, they're significantly heavier than modern alternatives, which becomes noticeable during extended wear.

The wide toe box accommodates most foot shapes comfortably, but the midfoot and heel run narrower than expected. This creates an unusual fit profile that works well for some hikers but causes pressure points for others. The KEEN Targhee III wide version addresses this issue more effectively.

Sizing & Fit Issues

Critical Sizing Alert

The most significant issue plaguing KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes is inconsistent sizing between production runs. Multiple users on Reddit and hiking forums report newer models running 6-8mm narrower than older versions, even in identical sizes. This isn't just perception—it's a documented manufacturing change that KEEN hasn't officially acknowledged.

During my testing, I compared shoes made in Thailand (2021) with identical models manufactured in India (2023). The difference is immediately noticeable, with the Indian-made version feeling significantly tighter in the midfoot despite identical size labels.

Fit Recommendations

Based on extensive testing and feedback from fellow hikers, here's my sizing guidance for the KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes:

Wide Feet

Break-in Period Reality

Unlike many hiking shoes that are comfortable immediately, the KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes require a substantial break-in period. The nubuck leather needs 40-60 miles of varied terrain to fully soften and conform to your foot shape. During this period, expect some initial stiffness, particularly around the ankle collar and heel cup.

I recommend starting with short day hikes and gradually increasing distance. The transformation is remarkable—shoes that feel rigid initially become notably more comfortable after proper break-in. However, if sizing is incorrect, no amount of break-in will resolve fundamental fit issues.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are KEEN Targhee III waterproof hiking shoes true to size?

KEEN Targhee III sizing has been inconsistent, with many users reporting newer models run narrower than older versions. Based on my testing and community feedback, I recommend trying them on in-store or ordering from retailers with generous return policies. The wide version is essential for broader feet, and manufacturing location (Thailand vs India) may affect fit consistency. If you're between sizes, consider sizing up for newer production runs, especially if you plan to wear thick hiking socks.

How long do KEEN Targhee III hiking shoes last?

In my experience testing multiple pairs, KEEN Targhee III shoes typically last 400-600 miles of moderate trail use. The nubuck leather upper holds up exceptionally well, often showing minimal wear even after extensive use. However, the outsole shows wear more quickly on rocky terrain and aggressive hiking styles. Proper care, including regular cleaning and conditioning of the leather, plus rotation with other hiking shoes, can extend their lifespan significantly. I've seen well-maintained pairs exceed 700 miles.

Are KEEN Targhee III good for winter hiking?

Yes, KEEN Targhee III performs excellently in winter conditions and is actually where these shoes shine brightest. The KEEN.DRY membrane keeps feet dry in snow and slush, while the midsole remains flexible in temperatures down to 20°F. The aggressive tread pattern provides solid traction on packed snow and icy trails. During my winter testing in Yellowstone and Rocky Mountain National Park, they handled sub-freezing conditions admirably. However, for extreme icy conditions, I still recommend carrying microspikes for additional safety.

Do KEEN Targhee III run wide or narrow?

KEEN Targhee III has an unusual fit profile - the toe box runs wide and accommodating, but the midfoot and heel run narrower than expected. This creates a fit that works well for some foot shapes but causes pressure points for others. Recent production runs appear to be running even narrower overall, which has frustrated many long-time KEEN customers. If you have wide feet, definitely consider the wide version, and if you have high-volume feet, the mid-height models may provide better accommodation.

What's the difference between Targhee III and Targhee IV?

The main differences between Targhee III and IV include improved breathability in the IV through enhanced mesh panels, a updated midsole compound for better comfort, and refined outsole pattern. However, many users report that the IV runs even narrower than the III, and some durability concerns have emerged with the newer model. The III remains popular among hikers who prioritize the proven track record and slightly roomier fit. If you're deciding between them, consider that the III is often available at better prices now, while the IV addresses some breathability issues but may have tighter sizing.

Are KEEN Targhee III worth the money?

KEEN Targhee III offers good value for hikers who prioritize waterproof protection, toe safety, and durability over lightweight performance. At current pricing (often under $130), they compete well with premium alternatives. However, the sizing inconsistency issues and heavy weight may not justify the cost for everyone. They're particularly worthwhile for winter hiking, wet climate conditions, and rugged terrain where toe protection is crucial. If you can find them on sale and the sizing works for your feet, they represent solid value. For casual hiking or warm weather use, lighter alternatives might offer better cost-per-mile value.
