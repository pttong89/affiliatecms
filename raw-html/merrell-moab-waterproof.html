Complete Review: Testing the Merrell Moab Waterproof Series

Waterproof Performance: Real-World Testing

The M Select DRY membrane in Merrell Moab waterproof boots has proven reliable across various wet conditions. During a week-long backpacking trip in the Cascade Range, I deliberately tested the waterproof capabilities through stream crossings, puddle walking, and hiking in persistent drizzle.

Waterproof Performance Results:

  • Excellent: Stream crossings up to 4 inches deep, puddle walking, light to moderate rain
  • Good: Heavy rain for 2-3 hours, walking through wet vegetation
  • Limitations: Extended submersion beyond 6 inches, prolonged heavy downpours (4+ hours)

The breathability aspect deserves special attention. While waterproof membranes inherently reduce airflow, the Merrell Moab waterproof manages moisture fairly well during moderate exertion. I've noticed minimal sweat accumulation during spring and fall hikes, though summer conditions in humid climates can challenge any waterproof boot.

Durability and Construction Quality

After extensive use, the pigskin suede leather and mesh construction has held up remarkably well. The reinforced toe cap has protected against rock strikes, and the overall build quality reflects Merrell's reputation for creating long-lasting footwear.

One area where I've seen improvement in recent models is sole attachment. Early versions of Merrell Moab waterproof boots occasionally experienced sole separation, but my newer pairs show no signs of this issue after hundreds of miles.

Traction and Grip Analysis

The Vibram TC5+ outsole provides reliable traction across most terrain types. During testing on granite slabs in Yosemite, wet rocks in the Pacific Northwest, and loose scree in the Rockies, the grip performance has been consistently good, though not exceptional on the most technical surfaces.

Traction Performance by Terrain:

  • Dry rock and dirt trails: Excellent grip and confidence
  • Wet surfaces: Good performance with careful foot placement
  • Mud and loose dirt: Adequate, though cleat depth could be deeper
  • Snow and ice: Limited performance, microspikes recommended

Merrell Moab 2 vs Moab 3 Waterproof: What's Changed?

Having worn both generations extensively, I can provide insights into the meaningful differences between the Merrell Moab 2 waterproof hiking shoes and the newer Moab 3 series.

Key Improvements in Moab 3

The most noticeable upgrade is the enhanced Vibram TC5+ outsole with deeper 5mm lugs compared to the Moab 2's shallower tread pattern. This translates to better grip on loose terrain and improved durability. The rubber compound also feels more pliable, providing better surface contact.

Construction-wise, the Moab 3 features improved materials including recycled content in the webbing and laces, though this doesn't significantly impact performance. The nylon arch shank receives better integration into the midsole, providing more stable support during long hikes with heavy packs.

Model Variations: Low vs Mid Cut

Both series offer low-cut and mid-cut options. The Merrell Moab 3 waterproof hiking shoes provide excellent flexibility and lighter weight, while the mid versions offer ankle support for heavier loads and more challenging terrain.

During my testing, I found the mid-cut versions particularly beneficial when carrying multi-day backpacking loads exceeding 30 pounds, though the low-cut models excel for day hiking and approach situations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Merrell Moab waterproof boots actually waterproof?

Yes, Merrell Moab waterproof boots feature the M Select DRY waterproof membrane that effectively keeps water out in most hiking conditions. Based on my extensive testing, they handle stream crossings up to 4-6 inches deep, puddle walking, and moderate to heavy rain effectively. However, like all waterproof boots, prolonged submersion or extreme conditions can eventually lead to water intrusion. The waterproof protection works best when combined with proper gaiters and appropriate socks for extended wet-weather hiking.

How do Merrell Moab waterproof boots fit compared to other hiking boots?

Merrell Moab waterproof boots generally run true to size with a roomier toe box compared to brands like Salomon or La Sportiva. The fit accommodates wider feet very well, which is why they're popular among hikers who struggle with narrow European-style boots. If you have narrow feet, you might find the heel cup slightly loose, but proper lacing and thicker socks usually address this. I always recommend trying them on with your hiking socks if possible, as the generous width can feel different from your street shoe size.

What's the difference between Merrell Moab 2 and Moab 3 waterproof versions?

The main improvements in Moab 3 include an upgraded Vibram TC5+ outsole with deeper 5mm lugs for better traction, enhanced nylon arch shank integration for improved support, and slightly refined materials including recycled components. The waterproof membrane remains essentially the same M Select DRY technology. From my experience testing both, the Moab 3 offers noticeably better grip on loose terrain and feels more supportive during long hikes with heavier packs, though the differences aren't dramatic enough to necessitate upgrading if you already own Moab 2 boots.

How long do Merrell Moab waterproof boots typically last?

Based on my experience and community feedback, you can expect 400-800 miles from a pair of Merrell Moab waterproof boots, depending on terrain, care, and hiking style. Recent models show improved durability, particularly regarding sole attachment issues that affected some earlier versions. Proper care, including cleaning after muddy hikes and allowing thorough drying, significantly extends lifespan. I've found that the upper materials typically outlast the outsole, which is normal for hiking boots. For casual weekend hikers, expect 2-3 years of use, while frequent hikers might need replacement annually.

Are Merrell Moab waterproof boots good for wide feet?

Absolutely! Merrell Moab waterproof boots are excellent for wide feet, featuring a generous toe box and available in wide-width options for both men and women. The roomier fit reduces pressure points and hot spots that narrow-footed hikers often experience with European-style boots. I've recommended them to numerous hiking partners with wide feet who previously struggled to find comfortable hiking footwear. The wide versions provide additional room without compromising support or waterproof integrity, making them one of the best mainstream options for hikers requiring extra width.
