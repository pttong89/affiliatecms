The outsole determines your connection to the terrain and significantly impacts hiking confidence. Sizing and Fitting Your Perfect Walking Boots

Proper boot fitting represents the most critical factor in hiking comfort and safety, yet it's surprisingly often overlooked by even experienced hikers. During my years of testing women's walking boots go outdoors, I've learned that perfect fit requires more than simply matching your shoe size to a boot size. The three-dimensional shape of your foot, your hiking sock choice, and even the time of day you try on boots all influence the final fit.

Go Outdoors stores provide professional boot fitting services that I strongly recommend utilizing, especially for your first pair of serious hiking boots. Their trained staff use inclined ramps that simulate downhill walking, allowing you to assess heel slippage and toe pressure under realistic conditions. This service proved invaluable when I discovered that my usual size 7 in casual shoes translated to a size 7.5 in hiking boots due to the different last shapes used by outdoor footwear manufacturers.

The Afternoon Fitting Rule

Always try on walking boots in the afternoon when your feet have naturally swollen to their largest daily size. Morning fittings often result in boots that feel uncomfortably tight during actual hiking, when foot swelling combines with heat and exertion. I learned this lesson during a disastrous day hike in Snowdonia, where boots that felt perfect in the morning store fitting became painfully tight by afternoon, forcing an early retreat.

The Thumb Test and Beyond

The traditional thumb-width space between your longest toe and the boot's front provides a starting point, but proper fitting requires additional checks. Your heel should sit securely in the heel cup without lifting during the walking motion. The widest part of your foot should align with the widest part of the boot to prevent pressure points. When laced properly, you should feel secure support without any pinching or excessive pressure.

Care and Maintenance Tips for Your Walking Boots

Proper care and maintenance can double or triple the lifespan of your women's walking boots go outdoors while maintaining their performance characteristics throughout their service life. I've learned through experience that prevention is far more effective than restoration when it comes to boot maintenance. My oldest pair of Scarpa boots, now in their fourth year and approaching 800 miles, still performs like new thanks to consistent care routine.

Post-Hike Cleaning Routine

The most critical maintenance occurs immediately after each hike. Remove loose dirt and debris while it's still damp and easier to brush off. Rinse boots with cool water to remove mud and salt (particularly important after coastal hikes), but avoid submerging them completely. Remove insoles and laces to ensure thorough drying. This simple routine prevents dirt from becoming embedded in materials and reduces the risk of bacterial growth that can cause odors.

Drying and Storage

Never use direct heat to dry walking boots, as it can damage both synthetic materials and leather while compromising waterproof membranes. Instead, remove insoles and laces, stuff boots lightly with newspaper (changed every few hours), and allow them to air dry at room temperature. Proper drying prevents bacterial growth and material degradation that shortens boot lifespan. Men's walking boots Go Outdoors require identical drying care regardless of construction materials.

Waterproofing Maintenance

Waterproof treatments require regular refresh to maintain effectiveness. DWR (Durable Water Repellent) coatings on boot uppers should be reapplied when water no longer beads on the surface. I use Nikwax TX.Direct for synthetic materials and Nikwax Nubuck & Suede Proof for leather boots, applying treatments monthly during heavy use periods. Heat activation in a dryer on low heat helps restore DWR effectiveness after application.

Are waterproof walking boots necessary for UK hiking?

Given the UK's unpredictable weather, waterproof walking boots are highly recommended for most hiking conditions.
