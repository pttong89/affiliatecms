Trail Overview & What Makes It Special

The Ricketts Glen Falls Trail stands as Pennsylvania's most spectacular waterfall hike, featuring 21 named waterfalls cascading through ancient hemlock forests. Located within the 13,050-acre Ricketts Glen State Park, this trail system traverses the Glens Natural Area, designated as a National Natural Landmark in 1969.

Quick Stats

  • Distance: 7.2 miles (full loop)
  • Elevation Gain: 1,050 feet
  • Duration: 4-6 hours
  • Difficulty: Strenuous
  • Waterfalls: 21 named falls

What sets this trail apart isn't just the quantity of waterfalls, but their incredible diversity and the pristine old-growth forest setting. The highest waterfall, Ganoga Falls, plunges 94 feet into a crystal-clear pool, while others like the delicate 11-foot Cayuga Falls offer intimate viewing opportunities. The trail follows Kitchen Creek and its tributaries through two main gorges: Ganoga Glen and Glen Leigh.

The Laurel Falls Trail in Great Smoky Mountains is often compared to Ricketts Glen, but I can tell you from personal experience that Ricketts Glen offers far more waterfalls and significantly more challenging terrain. Unlike easier waterfall hikes such as the Dark Hollow Falls trail, Ricketts Glen demands serious preparation and respect for its rugged conditions.

Important Safety Note: This trail has a reputation for accidents and even fatalities. The rock steps can be extremely slippery, especially when wet, and there are significant drop-offs along the trail. Proper footwear and caution are absolutely essential.

Complete Waterfalls Guide

The 21 waterfalls along the Ricketts Glen Falls Trail were named by Colonel R.B. Ricketts, who owned the land in the late 1800s. Most bear Native American tribal names, while others honor family members and friends. Here's your complete guide to these natural wonders, organized by the sections you'll encounter them.

Ganoga Glen Waterfalls (10 Falls)

Starting from the Lake Rose trailhead and descending into Ganoga Glen, you'll encounter the most spectacular concentration of waterfalls on the entire trail. This section features the steepest terrain and most dramatic drops.

Featured Waterfalls:

  • Ganoga Falls (94 ft) - The crown jewel
  • Erie Falls (47 ft) - Wide cascade
  • Tuscarora Falls (47 ft) - Multi-tiered beauty
  • Mohican Falls (39 ft) - Photographer's favorite
  • Delaware Falls (37 ft) - Hidden gem

Glen Leigh Waterfalls (8 Falls)

After crossing the bridge at Waters Meet, you'll ascend through Glen Leigh, which offers a different character with its narrower gorge and more intimate waterfall settings. The climbing here is steady but manageable, with excellent stone staircases built into the trail.

The standout here is Ozone Falls at 60 feet, named after the Wilkes-Barre Ozone hiking club that used Colonel Ricketts' stone house as their base. Other notable falls include F.L. Ricketts Falls (38 ft), named after the Colonel's brother, and R.B. Ricketts Falls (36 ft), honoring the man himself.

Kitchen Creek Falls (3 Falls)

Between Waters Meet and the Route 118 parking area, you'll find three additional waterfalls that many hikers skip. Don't make this mistake! Murray Reynolds Falls (16 ft), Sheldon Reynolds Falls (36 ft), and Harrison Wright Falls (27 ft) are among the most photogenic on the entire trail.

Photography Tip: The best light for waterfall photography occurs during the "golden hours" just after sunrise and before sunset. Overcast days also provide excellent conditions by eliminating harsh shadows.

Similar to the diverse waterfalls found along the Trail of 10 Falls in Oregon, each waterfall at Ricketts Glen has its own personality and viewing opportunities. However, unlike the more accessible Grotto Falls Trail, the Ricketts Glen waterfalls require significant effort to reach, making the experience all the more rewarding.

Planning Your Visit

Best Time to Visit

After hiking this trail in all four seasons, I can tell you that each offers unique advantages. Late spring (April-May) provides the highest water flow and comfortable temperatures, while fall (September-October) offers spectacular foliage but potentially slippery conditions due to wet leaves.

Seasonal Considerations:

  • Spring: High water flow, mud, wildflowers
  • Summer: Crowded weekends, comfortable water temperature
  • Fall: Peak foliage, slippery leaves, fewer crowds
  • Winter: Trail closed except for experienced ice climbers

I strongly recommend visiting on weekdays during school sessions. Weekend crowds, especially in summer and fall, can turn this peaceful nature experience into a traffic jam of hikers. On a busy Saturday, you might encounter hundreds of other hikers, significantly impacting your enjoyment and safety.

Parking & Trailheads

There are two main starting points for the Ricketts Glen Falls Trail, each offering different advantages:

Lake Rose Trailhead (Recommended)

  • Shorter overall distance (4.5 miles)
  • Better parking availability
  • Restroom facilities available
  • Easier descent into the glens

Route 118 Trailhead

  • Full 7.2-mile experience
  • Access to Adams Falls (22nd waterfall)
  • More gradual start
  • Limited parking fills early

Trail Regulations & Fees

Unlike some waterfall destinations such as the Pfeiffer Falls Trail in California, Ricketts Glen State Park does not charge entrance fees for day use. However, the park has strict regulations designed to protect both visitors and the fragile ecosystem.

Allowed:

  • Dogs on leash (6 ft max)
  • Photography for personal use
  • Day hiking
  • Swimming in designated areas

Prohibited:

  • Camping along the trail
  • Rock climbing on waterfalls
  • Drone flying
  • Alcohol consumption

Essential Gear & Safety Tips

Having witnessed multiple injuries on this trail, I cannot overemphasize the importance of proper gear and preparation. The Ricketts Glen Falls Trail demands more respect and preparation than easier hikes like the Stewart Falls Trail in Utah or the Booth Falls Trail in Colorado.

Footwear - Your Most Critical Decision

The single most important piece of equipment for this hike is proper footwear. The park explicitly warns against sneakers, sandals, or street shoes, and I've personally seen numerous accidents caused by inadequate footwear.

My Top Recommendation: Merrell Moab 3 Mid Waterproof Hiking Boots - These have been my go-to boots for three years of Ricketts Glen hikes. The aggressive tread pattern provides excellent grip on wet rocks, and the waterproof membrane keeps your feet dry.

Never attempt this trail in running shoes or casual footwear. The rock steps are carved directly into the stone and become extremely slippery when wet, which they often are due to spray from the waterfalls.

Trail Conditions & Navigation

Understanding the Terrain

The Ricketts Glen Falls Trail presents some of the most challenging terrain in Pennsylvania's state park system. The trail surface varies dramatically, from well-maintained dirt paths to steep rock staircases carved directly into the bedrock. Understanding these conditions is crucial for safe navigation.

Trail Surface Breakdown:

  • 40% Natural rock steps - Carved into bedrock, often wet
  • 30% Forest floor - Dirt, roots, and natural obstacles
  • 20% Wooden bridges - Multiple creek crossings
  • 10% Maintained trail - Well-graded sections

Navigation Note: Trail blazes are inconsistent in some areas. Download offline maps before your visit, as GPS and cell service are unreliable throughout most of the trail.

Seasonal Trail Conditions

Spring Conditions

High water flow makes waterfalls spectacular but creates extremely slippery conditions. Mud is common, and some sections may have minor flooding. This is peak season for water volume but requires extra caution.

Fall Hazards

Wet leaves create incredibly slippery conditions on rock surfaces. While foliage is beautiful, the trail becomes significantly more dangerous. Microspikes or similar traction devices are recommended.

Emergency Preparedness

Given the remote nature of portions of this trail and the documented history of accidents, emergency preparedness is not optional. I always carry a comprehensive first aid kit and have used it multiple times to help other hikers.

Emergency Protocol: If you encounter a serious injury, do not attempt to move the person. Send one hiker for help while another stays with the injured person. The nearest help is typically at the Lake Rose parking area or Route 118 trailhead.

Frequently Asked Questions

How difficult is the Ricketts Glen Falls Trail for beginners?

The Ricketts Glen Falls Trail is rated as strenuous and is not recommended for beginners. The trail features steep rock steps, significant elevation changes, and potentially dangerous conditions when wet. New hikers should start with easier trails and gradually build up their experience and fitness level. If you're determined to visit Ricketts Glen as a beginner, consider the shorter 3.2-mile option starting from Lake Rose trailhead, and absolutely ensure you have proper footwear and hiking experience on easier terrain first.

What's the best time of year to visit Ricketts Glen Falls Trail?

The optimal time to visit is late spring (April-May) or early fall (September-October). Spring offers the highest water flow making waterfalls most spectacular, while fall provides beautiful foliage. Summer can be very crowded, especially on weekends, but offers comfortable temperatures and good trail conditions. Winter hiking is only permitted for experienced ice climbers with proper equipment and park ranger check-in. Avoid visiting immediately after heavy rains when conditions become extremely hazardous.

Can I bring my dog on the Ricketts Glen Falls Trail?

Yes, dogs are permitted on the Ricketts Glen Falls Trail but must be kept on a leash no longer than 6 feet at all times. However, consider your dog's fitness level and paw protection, as the rocky terrain can be tough on their feet. The steep rock steps and narrow bridges can be challenging for dogs, and you'll need to carry water for them as well. Many sections have significant drop-offs where an unleashed dog could be seriously injured. If your dog isn't experienced with challenging terrain, consider leaving them home for their safety.

How long does it take to complete the full Ricketts Glen Falls Trail?

Plan for 5-6 hours to complete the full 7.2-mile loop, including time for photography and rest breaks at the waterfalls. Fit, experienced hikers might complete it in 4 hours, while others may need up to 8 hours depending on conditions and group size. The shorter 4.5-mile option from Lake Rose typically takes 3-4 hours. These times assume good weather and trail conditions. Factor in extra time during peak seasons when crowds can slow progress, and always start early to avoid being caught on the trail after dark. Remember, the trail is more challenging than distance alone suggests due to the rocky terrain.

Are there any entrance fees for Ricketts Glen State Park?

No, there are no entrance fees for day use at Ricketts Glen State Park. Parking at all trailheads is free, though spaces fill up quickly during peak times, especially weekends in summer and fall. The park does charge fees for camping if you plan to stay overnight, and there are fees for certain special programs or guided tours. However, hiking the Falls Trail and accessing all waterfalls is completely free. Consider arriving early (before 8 AM) to secure parking, as the lots can fill completely on popular hiking days.

What should I do if I encounter wildlife on the Ricketts Glen Falls Trail?

Ricketts Glen is home to black bears, so proper food storage and awareness are essential. Store all food in bear-resistant containers or hang it properly if camping nearby. If you encounter a bear, do not run; instead, make yourself appear large, back away slowly, and make noise. The park is also home to venomous timber rattlesnakes, so watch where you step and sit. Other wildlife includes deer, wild turkeys, and various small mammals. Always maintain a respectful distance from all wildlife, never feed animals, and report any aggressive animal behavior to park rangers immediately. Carry bear spray if you're comfortable using it, though encounters are relatively rare on this busy trail.
