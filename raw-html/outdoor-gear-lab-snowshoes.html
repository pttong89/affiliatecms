Sizing and Selection Considerations

Choosing the right snowshoe size involves more than just matching your weight to a chart. Through extensive testing, I've learned that factors like pack weight, snow conditions, and terrain type all influence optimal sizing decisions. The general rule of 1 square inch per pound of total weight provides a starting point, but real-world applications require more nuanced thinking.

Consider your typical winter hiking scenarios carefully. If you primarily explore groomed winter trails with your family, models like the MSR Evo Trail provide excellent value and performance. However, if you're planning backcountry adventures that might include overnight camping with down jacket systems, investing in premium options becomes worthwhile.

What size snowshoes should I choose according to Outdoor Gear Lab sizing guidelines?

Outdoor Gear Lab recommends calculating total system weight (body weight plus pack and gear) as the primary sizing factor, then considering intended terrain and snow conditions. Their general guideline suggests approximately 1 square inch of snowshoe surface area per pound of total weight, though this varies based on snow density and terrain difficulty. Through extensive field testing, I've learned that recreational hikers often benefit from slightly larger sizes for improved flotation, while technical mountaineers may prefer smaller, more maneuverable options. Most manufacturers provide specific weight recommendations for each snowshoe size, and Outdoor Gear Lab testing validates these suggestions across real-world conditions. Consider factors like typical pack weight, snow conditions in your region, and whether you prioritize flotation or maneuverability when making sizing decisions.
