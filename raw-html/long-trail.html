What is the Long Trail?

The Long Trail stands as Vermont's crown jewel and America's pioneering long-distance hiking adventure. Stretching an impressive 273 miles from the Massachusetts border to the Canadian frontier, this historic footpath carved its way through the heart of the Green Mountains between 1910 and 1930, making it the oldest continuous hiking trail in the United States.

What makes this trail truly special isn't just its age—it's the raw, unfiltered Vermont wilderness experience it provides. Unlike more modern trails designed with switchbacks and gentle grades, the Long Trail follows the philosophy of its era: straight up the mountain and straight back down. This creates a uniquely challenging adventure that rewards hikers with authentic New England mountain terrain.

The trail's southern section shares 105 miles with the famous Appalachian Trail, giving you the best of both worlds. As you progress northward, you'll tackle Vermont's most iconic peaks, including Mount Mansfield (4,395 feet), Camel's Hump (4,083 feet), and Killington Peak (4,235 feet). The long path philosophy extends beyond just distance—it's about connecting with nature's raw power.

With over 70 backcountry campsites and shelters maintained by the dedicated volunteers of the Green Mountain Club, the Long Trail offers infrastructure that supports both novice backpackers and seasoned thru-hikers. The trail crosses six wilderness areas in the Green Mountain National Forest, providing an unparalleled wilderness experience that inspired the creation of the Appalachian Trail itself.

Planning Your Long Trail Adventure

After completing the Long Trail myself, I can't stress enough how crucial proper planning is for success. The average completion time ranges from 20-30 days, but I've seen experienced hikers finish in as little as 16 days, while others take their time over several weeks. Your personal fitness level, hiking experience, and goals will determine your ideal timeline.

Northbound vs Southbound: My Recommendation

I hiked northbound (NOBO) and highly recommend this direction for first-time Long Trail hikers. Starting at the Massachusetts border, you'll gradually build your trail legs on the more moderate southern terrain before tackling the brutal northern sections. The psychological benefit of ending at the Canadian border monument is incredibly rewarding—trust me, you'll want that triumphant finish photo.

Southbound (SOBO) hikers face the most challenging terrain immediately, which can be demoralizing if you're not in peak physical condition. However, if you're starting in late summer or early fall, SOBO allows you to avoid potential snow on the higher northern peaks. The choice depends on your fitness level and seasonal timing.

No Permits Required

One of the Long Trail's biggest advantages is its simplicity—no permits are required for individual hikers. You can literally decide to hike it tomorrow (weather permitting). This freedom extends to camping, with no fees charged at any of the 70+ shelters and tent sites along the route as of 2023. However, you must follow proper food storage protocols in the Green Mountain National Forest, using bear canisters, Ursacks, or proper bear hangs.

Resupply logistics are surprisingly manageable. Unlike remote western trails, the Long Trail crosses roads frequently, typically every 4-7 days. Towns like Manchester, Rutland, Waitsfield, and Waterbury offer full resupply options, often with hiker-friendly services including shuttles and mail drops. I found the Vermont hiking community incredibly welcoming—trail angels are common, and local businesses understand hikers' needs.

My Personal Long Trail Experience

Completing the Long Trail in September 2023 remains one of my most challenging and rewarding outdoor adventures. I chose late summer for optimal weather conditions, avoiding both the notorious mud season and the biting black flies of early summer. My 23-day journey taught me lessons that no amount of research could have prepared me for.

The first week lulled me into false confidence. Following the Appalachian Trail south from Massachusetts, the terrain felt familiar and manageable. I was averaging 15+ mile days and feeling strong. The "Green Tunnel" reputation became reality as I found myself hiking through dense forest for hours without views, navigating root-tangled, rock-strewn trails that seemed designed to test your ankles at every step.

The Vermont Mud Challenge

Even in late summer, Vermont earned its "Vermud" nickname. The northern sections, especially around Mount Mansfield and Camel's Hump, challenged me with slippery, root-covered climbs that required hands-on scrambling. I learned to embrace the mud rather than fight it. My long-lasting walking boots proved essential—waterproof boots are a must, despite what ultralight enthusiasts might tell you.

The trail's design philosophy of "straight up and straight down" became apparent around Mount Abraham and Mount Ellen. These aren't technical rock climbs, but they're relentless steep ascents followed by knee-jarring descents. My long walking boots for men with excellent ankle support saved me from injury multiple times.

The psychological challenge surprised me most. Days of hiking through dense forest with minimal views tested my mental resolve. The payoff came at moments like sunrise from Mount Mansfield or the panoramic views from Jay Peak near the Canadian border. These experiences reminded me why the Long Trail, despite its challenges, remains one of America's most rewarding hiking adventures.

Seasonal Hiking Guide

Vermont's seasons dramatically transform the Long Trail experience, each offering unique advantages and challenges. Having hiked sections during different times of year, I've learned to match my expectations and gear choices to seasonal realities. Your timing decision will significantly impact your success and enjoyment.

Late Spring/Early Summer (May-June)

May through early June brings Vermont's infamous "mud season," officially discouraged by the Green Mountain Club to protect trail integrity. However, by mid-June, conditions improve dramatically. This period offers incredible wildflower displays and flowing water sources, but you'll face legendary black fly swarms that can drive even experienced hikers to distraction. I recommend a head net and long sleeves regardless of temperature.

The days are long with nearly 15 hours of daylight, allowing for extended hiking if bugs become unbearable during dawn and dusk peak feeding times. Stream crossings can be challenging due to spring runoff, so waterproof long outdoor boots become even more critical during this season.

Peak Summer (July-August)

Summer brings the busiest trail conditions but also the most predictable weather. Temperatures on ridgelines remain comfortable even when valleys swelter, and the infamous Vermont mud reaches its most manageable state. However, water sources can become unreliable during drought conditions, requiring careful planning and potentially longer carries between reliable springs.

Summer hiking allows you to enjoy Vermont's pristine mountain ponds and swimming holes. Little Rock Pond, Stratton Pond, and the Winooski River offer refreshing breaks that make the challenging climbs worthwhile. The long daylight hours provide safety margins for unexpected delays or navigational challenges.

Fall (September-October)

September through early October represents the Long Trail's sweet spot—my personal favorite hiking season. Bug populations crash after the first frost, temperatures cool to ideal hiking levels, and Vermont's legendary fall foliage transforms the Green Mountains into a photographer's paradise. Crowds thin significantly compared to summer peak season.

However, fall brings shorter days and the potential for early snow, especially on higher peaks after mid-October. I've experienced snow flurries on Mount Mansfield in late September, so proper layering becomes essential. The need for quality walking boots long increases as morning frost makes rocks and roots treacherously slippery. Despite these challenges, fall hiking on the Long Trail offers the most rewarding experience for prepared hikers.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to complete the Long Trail?

Based on my experience and conversations with fellow hikers, most people complete the Long Trail in 20-30 days. Experienced backpackers often finish in 16-23 days, while those taking their time or dealing with weather delays might take up to 35 days. Your daily mileage will largely depend on shelter locations, as they provide the best camping spots every 8-10 miles. I completed it in 23 days, which felt like a good balance of challenge and enjoyment. Remember, the northern sections are significantly more technical and slower than the southern AT-shared portions.

Do I need permits to hike the Long Trail?

No permits are required for individual hikers on the Long Trail, which is one of its greatest advantages. You can decide to hike it spontaneously without the months-long permit lottery systems required for other major trails. Additionally, there are no camping fees at any of the 70+ shelters and tent sites as of 2023. However, you must follow bear-proof food storage requirements in the Green Mountain National Forest using bear canisters, Ursacks, or proper bear hangs. Groups may need permits from the Forest Service or state parks department depending on size and specific locations.

What's the best time of year to hike the Long Trail?

September through early October offers the ideal Long Trail experience in my opinion. You'll avoid the notorious black flies of early summer, the mud of spring, and the crowds of peak summer while enjoying spectacular fall foliage. Late June through August provides the most predictable weather and longest daylight hours, but expect more hikers and bugs. Avoid April through May due to official mud season closures designed to protect trail integrity. Winter hiking is possible but requires mountaineering experience and appropriate gear for potentially dangerous conditions.

How difficult is the Long Trail compared to other thru-hikes?

The Long Trail punches well above its weight class in terms of difficulty. While only 273 miles compared to the AT's 2,100+ miles, Vermont's mountains are steep, rocky, and follow old-school trail design philosophy of going straight up and down without modern switchbacks. The northern sections feature hand-over-hand scrambling and technical terrain that rivals anything on longer trails. Many hikers use the Long Trail as preparation for the AT, PCT, or CDT. The persistent moisture and muddy conditions add another challenge layer that western hikers often find surprising. It's an excellent proving ground for longer adventures.

What gear is essential for the Long Trail?

Waterproof hiking boots are absolutely essential—don't attempt the Long Trail in trail runners unless you enjoy soaked feet and twisted ankles on technical terrain. Quality rain gear is mandatory, as Vermont's weather is notoriously unpredictable. Bear-proof food storage is required by law in the Green Mountain National Forest. A reliable tent or tarp is necessary despite shelter availability, as they can be full or closed for maintenance. Trekking poles provide crucial stability on the steep, slippery terrain. Finally, bring extra clothing layers as mountain weather changes rapidly, especially on the higher peaks above 4,000 feet.

How do resupply logistics work on the Long Trail?

Resupply on the Long Trail is remarkably convenient compared to western trails. The trail crosses roads frequently, typically every 4-7 days, providing access to towns like Manchester, Rutland, Waitsfield, and Waterbury. These communities offer full grocery stores, restaurants, and often hiker-specific services including mail drops and shuttles. Many trail angels provide free or low-cost rides to town. I found Vermont's hiking community incredibly welcoming and helpful. You can easily resupply without pre-planning mail drops, though sending packages ahead to popular stops like the Long Trail House can save time. The Green Mountain Club visitor center in Waterbury is an excellent resource for current trail information and local contacts.

Conclusion

The Long Trail represents more than just America's oldest long-distance hiking trail—it's a rite of passage that challenges hikers physically, mentally, and spiritually. My 23 days traversing Vermont's Green Mountains taught me lessons about perseverance, self-reliance, and the raw beauty of New England wilderness that continue to influence my outdoor adventures years later.

While the trail's 273 miles might seem manageable compared to longer thru-hikes, don't underestimate Vermont's unique challenges. The steep, technical terrain following early 20th-century trail design philosophy will test your limits. The persistent moisture and mud that earned Vermont its "Vermud" nickname will challenge your gear choices. The northern sections' hand-over-hand scrambling will demand every ounce of your physical fitness.

Yet these challenges create the Long Trail's magic. Every muddy mile, every steep ascent, every weather-beaten shelter contributes to an authentic wilderness experience that modern, over-engineered trails often lack. The trail strips away pretense and connects you directly with the mountains' raw power. The sense of accomplishment standing at the Canadian border monument, knowing you walked every step from Massachusetts, is unmatched.

For aspiring thru-hikers, the Long Trail offers the perfect introduction to long-distance hiking. Its manageable length allows you to experience the full emotional arc of a thru-hike—from initial excitement through mid-hike struggles to triumphant completion—without committing months of your life. The frequent resupply opportunities and excellent shelter system provide safety nets while you develop your trail skills.

Most importantly, the Long Trail connects you with hiking history. Walking the same paths that inspired the Appalachian Trail, staying in shelters built by generations of volunteers, and joining the ranks of End-to-Enders creates a profound sense of continuity with America's hiking heritage. You're not just completing a hike—you're participating in a tradition that spans over a century.

Whether you're seeking your first thru-hike experience, training for longer adventures, or simply wanting to explore Vermont's wild beauty, the Long Trail awaits. Prepare thoroughly, respect the mountains, and embrace the challenge. Those 273 miles will reward you with memories, confidence, and stories that last a lifetime. The Long Trail isn't just America's oldest footpath—it's your pathway to discovering what you're truly capable of accomplishing.
