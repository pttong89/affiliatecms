Trail Overview and History

The Elk River Trail represents one of West Virginia's most ambitious and successful rails-to-trails conversion projects. Stretching an impressive 73.3 miles from Clendenin in Kanawha County to Gassaway in Braxton County, this former Norfolk Southern railroad corridor has been transformed into a multi-use recreational pathway that celebrates both natural beauty and industrial heritage.

Currently, approximately 52 miles of the trail are open to the public, with ongoing construction work to complete the remaining sections. The trail maintains an incredibly gentle 0.5% grade, making it accessible to adventurers of all ages and abilities. During my numerous visits, I've witnessed families with young children, serious cyclists training for events, and contemplative solo hikers all sharing this remarkable resource harmoniously.

What sets the Elk River Trail apart from other rail trails is its intimate relationship with the Elk River itself. The trail runs parallel to the river for its entire length, creating opportunities for fishing, kayaking, and wildlife observation that are simply unmatched in the region. The combination of land and water recreation makes this destination particularly appealing for those seeking diverse outdoor experiences.

Trail Statistics at a Glance

  • Total Length: 73.3 miles (52 miles currently open)
  • Surface: Packed gravel with paved sections
  • Grade: 0.5% (extremely gentle)
  • Counties Served: Braxton, Clay, Kanawha
  • Activities: Hiking, biking, horseback riding, fishing
  • Accessibility: Suitable for all skill levels

Trail Sections and Access Points

Understanding the various sections of the Elk River Trail is crucial for planning your adventure. The trail is divided into several distinct segments, each offering unique characteristics and access opportunities. Based on my extensive exploration, I'll share insights about the most accessible and rewarding sections.

Northern Section: Gassaway to Frametown

The northern terminus begins near the intersection of Route 4 bridge and River Road in southern Gassaway. This 45.4-mile section represents the longest continuous stretch currently available. The trail passes through dense woodlands and offers frequent glimpses of the Elk River through breaks in the forest canopy. Key trailheads include Coastal Lumber, Frametown, Strange Creek, Duck, Ivydale, Dundon, and Hartland.

During my spring visits, this section showcases spectacular wildflower displays and active wildlife. I've consistently spotted deer, various bird species, and even caught glimpses of river otters playing in the calm waters. The Frametown area is particularly noteworthy for its excellent facilities and river access opportunities.

Southern Section: Queen Shoals to Clendenin

The southern section features a completed 4.5-mile stretch from Queen Shoals Trailhead to just south of Clendenin, with an additional one-mile paved section extending north from Clendenin. This area provides some of the most convenient access points and represents an ideal introduction to the Elk River Trail for first-time visitors.

I particularly recommend starting newcomers in this section due to its excellent maintenance, clear signage, and proximity to amenities. The paved portion near Clendenin is perfect for families with young children or those using wheelchairs or mobility devices.

Buffalo Creek Extension

The 18-mile Buffalo Creek rails-with-trail section from Dundon to Widen deserves special mention. This unique segment runs parallel to active railroad tracks and offers a different experience from the main trail. In 2024, this section became home to Rail Explorers' innovative rail-bike tours, featuring pedal-powered vehicles that provide a novel way to experience the landscape.

Access Point Recommendations

For your first visit, I recommend these strategic starting points:

  • Clendenin Trailhead: Best for beginners and families
  • Frametown: Excellent facilities and river access
  • Ivydale: Features bike repair stations
  • Duck: Combines trail and water activities

Activities and What to Expect

The diversity of activities available along the Elk River Trail truly sets it apart from other recreational trails. During my many visits throughout different seasons, I've experienced firsthand how this trail system caters to virtually every outdoor interest and fitness level.

Hiking and Walking

The trail's gentle grade and well-maintained surface make it ideal for hiking and walking. I've found that the packed gravel surface provides excellent traction in most weather conditions, though I always recommend appropriate river shoes for optimal comfort and safety. The relatively flat terrain allows hikers to focus on the scenery rather than technical navigation challenges.

During my autumn visits, the trail transforms into a spectacular corridor of fall foliage. The dense tree coverage creates a natural tunnel effect that frames views of the river while providing welcome shade during warmer months. Exercise stations positioned along certain sections add an element of fitness training for those seeking more intensive workouts.

Cycling Adventures

Cycling represents one of the most popular activities on the Elk River Trail. The smooth surface and minimal elevation changes make it perfect for road bikes, hybrid bikes, and mountain bikes alike. Class 1 and Class 3 e-bikes are also permitted, opening the trail to riders who might otherwise find long distances challenging.

Based on my cycling experiences, I recommend planning for 15-20 miles as a comfortable day ride for most cyclists. The multiple trailheads allow for flexible trip planning, whether you prefer out-and-back routes or one-way rides with shuttle arrangements. Several local outfitters provide shuttle services, making longer rides more accessible.

Water Activities and Fishing

The Elk River Trail's parallel relationship with the Elk River creates exceptional opportunities for water-based recreation. Multiple access points allow trail users to easily transition between land and water activities. The river supports excellent fishing for smallmouth bass, rock bass, and various panfish species.

Kayaking and canoeing are particularly rewarding along this section of the Elk River. The gentle current and numerous put-in/take-out points make it suitable for paddlers of varying skill levels. During my kayaking trips, I've observed great blue herons, kingfishers, and occasionally bald eagles hunting along the riverbanks.

Wildlife Observation

The trail corridor supports remarkably diverse wildlife populations. Regular visitors report sightings of white-tailed deer, black bears, bobcats, foxes, and various small mammals. Bird enthusiasts will particularly appreciate the variety of species, including several owl species, wild turkeys, and numerous songbirds.

Wildlife Viewing Tips

  • Early morning and late afternoon offer the best wildlife viewing opportunities
  • Move quietly and avoid sudden movements
  • Bring binoculars for distant observations
  • Respect wildlife by maintaining safe distances
  • Consider visiting during weekdays for fewer crowds

Navigation and Safety

While the Elk River Trail is well-marked and straightforward to follow, carrying basic navigation tools and safety equipment provides peace of mind and ensures you're prepared for unexpected situations.

Safety Essentials

  • First aid kit appropriate for your group size
  • Emergency whistle for signaling
  • Headlamp or flashlight with extra batteries
  • Multi-tool or knife for various needs
  • Weather-appropriate clothing layers
  • Sunscreen and insect repellent

For cyclists planning longer rides, I highly recommend quality hiking shoes for potential walking sections and a comprehensive repair kit. The trail's remote sections can be far from assistance, making self-sufficiency important.

My Personal Experience

My relationship with the Elk River Trail began three years ago during an unseasonably warm March weekend. A local ranger had recommended it as an excellent introduction to West Virginia's rail trail system, suggesting I start with the Clendenin section to get a feel for the terrain and conditions. That first visit exceeded every expectation and sparked a deep appreciation that has brought me back more than a dozen times since.

First Impressions and Learning Curve

Walking from the Clendenin trailhead on that first morning, I was immediately struck by the trail's tranquil atmosphere. The gentle sound of the Elk River flowing parallel to the path created a natural soundtrack that seemed to wash away the stress of daily life. What surprised me most was how quickly the trail felt remote and wild, despite being easily accessible from major roads.

During that initial 8-mile out-and-back hike, I made several rookie mistakes that taught me valuable lessons. I wore regular sneakers instead of proper trail shoes, which became uncomfortably wet when I encountered several muddy patches. I also underestimated how much water I'd need, particularly given the deceptively easy terrain that encouraged longer walks than anticipated.

Seasonal Discoveries

Each season has revealed different aspects of the Elk River Trail's character. Spring visits showcase incredible wildflower displays, particularly trilliums and bloodroot blooming in the forest understory. The increased bird activity during migration periods has led to some of my most memorable wildlife encounters, including a close encounter with a great blue heron fishing in a quiet river pool.

Summer adventures require more careful planning due to heat and humidity, but early morning starts reward visitors with cooler temperatures and peak wildlife activity. I've learned to appreciate the dense forest canopy's cooling effect and the way morning mist rises from the river surface, creating almost mystical conditions for photography.

Fall visits to the trail represent pure magic. The hardwood forests explode in brilliant oranges, reds, and yellows that reflect in the calm river waters. October hikes have become an annual tradition, and I always recommend this timing to visitors seeking the most visually spectacular Elk River Trail experience.

Lessons Learned and Pro Tips

Through trial and error, I've developed strategies that consistently improve trail experiences. Weather in West Virginia can change rapidly, so layered clothing systems prove invaluable. I've learned to pack rain gear even during seemingly perfect weather forecasts, and this preparation has saved multiple trips from disappointment.

The trail's multiple access points allow for creative trip planning. My favorite approach involves arranging shuttles between trailheads, enabling one-way journeys that cover more ground without backtracking. Local outdoor shops sometimes provide shuttle services, or groups can arrange car swaps to facilitate longer adventures.

Hard-Earned Insights

  • Start early during popular seasons to avoid crowds and enjoy cooler temperatures
  • Bring more water than you think you'll need - the easy terrain encourages longer adventures
  • Pack a basic first aid kit; help can be far away on remote sections
  • Consider combining trail and river activities for a more diverse experience
  • Respect private property boundaries and stay on designated trail areas
  • Check trail conditions after severe weather before planning trips

Frequently Asked Questions

How long is the Elk River Trail?

The Elk River Trail spans 73.3 miles from Clendenin to Gassaway in West Virginia, though currently about 52 miles are open to the public. The trail follows the former railroad bed along the beautiful Elk River, providing a relatively flat and accessible route for hikers, cyclists, and equestrians. The remaining sections are under construction, with completion expected in the coming years. This impressive length makes it one of the longest rail trails in West Virginia, offering opportunities for everything from short family walks to multi-day bikepacking adventures.

What difficulty level is the Elk River Trail?

The Elk River Trail is considered easy to moderate difficulty. With only a 0.5% grade following the old railroad bed, it's accessible for most fitness levels. The trail surface is primarily packed gravel with some paved sections, making it suitable for families, casual hikers, and cyclists of varying abilities. The gentle grade means you can focus on enjoying the scenery rather than navigating challenging terrain. However, the length of the trail allows visitors to customize their difficulty level by choosing shorter or longer distances based on their fitness and time constraints.

What activities are allowed on the Elk River Trail?

The Elk River Trail welcomes walkers, hikers, runners, cyclists, and horseback riders. Class 1 and Class 3 e-bikes are also permitted, expanding access for those who might need electric assistance. The trail provides excellent access to fishing and kayaking opportunities along the Elk River, with multiple water access points throughout its length. Exercise stations along certain sections offer additional fitness opportunities. The multi-use designation and excellent maintenance make it perfect for group outings where different participants prefer different activities, allowing everyone to enjoy the trail their own way.

Are there camping options along the Elk River Trail?

While the main Elk River Trail doesn't have designated camping areas, there are several lodging options nearby including cabins and inns. For camping enthusiasts, I recommend checking local state parks and private campgrounds in the surrounding counties of Braxton, Clay, and Kanawha. Some visitors choose to combine trail activities with stays at nearby accommodations, allowing for multi-day exploration without carrying camping gear. Always verify current camping regulations and private property boundaries before your visit, as these can change seasonally or due to land management decisions.

What's the best time of year to visit the Elk River Trail?

Spring through fall offers the best conditions for exploring the Elk River Trail. I particularly love visiting in late spring (May-June) when wildflowers bloom and wildlife is most active, or early fall (September-October) for stunning autumn foliage. Summer can be warm and humid, making early morning starts advisable, but the forest canopy provides welcome shade. Winter visits are possible but may present muddy or icy conditions, particularly in shaded areas. Weather in West Virginia can change quickly, so always check current conditions and dress in layers regardless of the season you choose for your adventure.
