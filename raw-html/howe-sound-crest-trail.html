Trail Overview & Essential Stats

Howe Sound Crest Trail Quick Facts

Distance: 29-32.8 km (18-20.4 miles)
Type: Point-to-point
Difficulty: Very Difficult
Duration: 2-3 days typical
Elevation Gain: 2,500+ meters cumulative
Highest Point: 1,592m (Brunswick Mountain area)
Location: Cypress Provincial Park, BC
Best Season: July - October

The Howe Sound Crest Trail represents the pinnacle of Vancouver-area hiking challenges. This legendary route traverses the dramatic ridgeline of the North Shore Mountains, offering hikers an authentic wilderness experience surprisingly close to urban civilization. From the moment you leave the crowds behind at St. Marks Summit, you'll be immersed in some of British Columbia's most spectacular and demanding terrain.

What makes this trail truly exceptional is its proximity to Vancouver—you can literally drive from downtown to the trailhead in 30 minutes, yet find yourself in pristine backcountry that rivals any remote wilderness area. The trail connects iconic landmarks including The Lions (Vancouver's most recognizable peaks), multiple alpine lakes, and several technical summits that require scrambling skills.

Trail Runner Sarah M. - Completed 3 times

"The HSCT is absolutely brutal but incredibly rewarding. I've done it as both a 2-day backpack and single-day run. The views from James Peak and the Lions are worth every challenging step, but don't underestimate the technical sections—they're genuinely dangerous in poor conditions."

The trail's reputation for difficulty is well-earned. Search and rescue teams respond to numerous calls along the Howe Sound Crest Trail each year, primarily due to hikers underestimating the technical challenges or getting caught unprepared by changing weather conditions. However, with proper preparation, appropriate gear, and realistic expectations about your abilities, this trail offers one of Canada's most memorable hiking experiences.

Planning Your Howe Sound Crest Trail Adventure

Optimal Timing and Seasonal Considerations

Timing is absolutely critical for a successful Howe Sound Crest Trail experience. The narrow hiking window of July through October exists for good reasons—outside these months, the trail becomes exponentially more dangerous due to snow, ice, and severe avalanche risk. I learned this lesson firsthand during an early June attempt that ended in retreat due to waist-deep snow and unstable cornices along the exposed ridges.

Late summer (August-September) offers the most reliable conditions, with minimal snow, stable weather patterns, and the best water availability. However, this is also peak season, so expect to encounter more hikers, especially on the first day to St. Marks Summit. Early October can provide stunning fall colors but brings increased weather volatility and shorter daylight hours.

Critical Safety Note

The Howe Sound Crest Trail ranks as #2 in British Columbia for search and rescue calls. Never attempt this trail without extensive hiking experience, proper gear, and favorable weather forecasts. Always inform someone of your detailed itinerary and expected return time.

Transportation and Logistics

The point-to-point nature of the trail requires transportation planning. Most hikers either arrange a car shuttle (dropping one vehicle at Porteau Cove and driving to Cypress Mountain) or coordinate pickup/drop-off with friends or family. The drive from Cypress to Porteau Cove takes approximately 45 minutes via Highway 99.

Starting Points: South to north (Cypress Mountain to Porteau Cove) is the most popular direction, offering better elevation profiles and ending with a long downhill finish. However, some experienced hikers prefer north to south for the challenging climbs early when energy levels are highest.

Backpacker Mike D. - 2 completions

"I've done both directions. South to north is definitely easier overall, but starting from Porteau means you hit the technical Lions section while fresh. Choose based on your strengths—endurance vs. technical skills."

Fitness and Experience Requirements

The Howe Sound Crest Trail demands exceptional physical fitness and significant hiking experience. This isn't a trail for weekend warriors or those new to backpacking. I recommend having completed multiple 20+ km day hikes with heavy packs, experience with scrambling and exposure, and comfort navigating unmarked terrain before attempting the HSCT. Consider tackling sections individually first—the AllTrails platform offers detailed information about approaching individual peaks along the route.

Detailed Route Description

Segment 1: Cypress Mountain to St. Marks Summit (5.5 km)

The journey begins at the Cypress Mountain ski area parking lot, following well-maintained trails through mixed forest. This section serves as a warm-up, gaining approximately 500 meters of elevation over moderate terrain. The trail is clearly marked and heavily traveled, making navigation straightforward. St. Marks Summit provides your first spectacular views of Howe Sound and serves as the last "easy exit" point—many day hikers turn around here, and for good reason.

Segment 2: St. Marks to Unnecessary Mountain (4 km)

Here's where the real Howe Sound Crest Trail adventure begins. The aptly named Unnecessary Mountain requires a steep descent followed by an equally steep ascent—truly "unnecessary" elevation change that tests your mental fortitude early in the journey. The trail becomes less defined, marked primarily by orange flagging and cairns. Weather can change rapidly in this exposed section, and I've experienced everything from blazing sun to sudden snow squalls within minutes.

Segment 3: The Lions Traverse (3 km)

The crown jewel of the route, this section around The Lions (East and West Lion peaks) represents both the most spectacular and most dangerous portion of the trail. The path traverses an exposed ledge beneath West Lion—roughly one foot wide with hundreds of feet of exposure. Chains and fixed ropes assist in several locations, but these are for emergencies only, not regular handholds.

Technical Challenge Warning

The Lions traverse should never be attempted in wet, icy, or windy conditions. I've witnessed multiple near-accidents here, including a hiker who slipped and was only saved by their partner's quick reaction. Take your time, test every handhold, and don't hesitate to turn back if conditions deteriorate.

Segment 4: James and David Peaks (4 km)

These connected peaks feature the trail's most technical scrambling, including a knife-edge ridge on James Peak equipped with chains for the final 30 feet. The exposure is significant but manageable with proper technique. David Peak follows with steep scree slopes and loose rock—many hikers find this more challenging than the chain sections due to the unstable footing and route-finding difficulties.

Segment 5: Magnesia Meadows to Brunswick Lake (8 km)

After the technical challenges, this section offers relative relief with beautiful alpine meadows and the first reliable water source at Magnesia Meadows. The emergency shelter here provides basic wind protection but don't count on it being available or in good condition. The route to Brunswick Lake includes the trail's highest point and some of the most spectacular views in coastal British Columbia.

Segment 6: Brunswick Lake to Porteau Cove (10 km)

The final push includes Deeks Lake, multiple stream crossings, and a relentless descent through old-growth forest to the highway. While technically easier than earlier sections, the steep downhill with a heavy pack can be brutal on knees and ankles. The last 3.5 kilometers follow an old logging road—mentally challenging after the wild mountain terrain but mercifully straightforward navigation.

Camping & Safety Guide

Prime Camping Locations

The Howe Sound Crest Trail offers several designated camping areas, each with unique characteristics and challenges. All camping is wilderness-style with no facilities—you're responsible for water treatment, waste management, and Leave No Trace principles. Choose your camping spots based on weather forecasts, personal energy levels, and water availability.

Magnesia Meadows (15 km from start)

My preferred first-night camping location, Magnesia Meadows offers stunning sunset views, reliable water from the alpine tarn, and relative protection from wind. The emergency shelter provides basic weather protection but don't count on it being available or in good condition. Camping spots are limited, so arrive early during peak season or have backup plans ready.

Brunswick Lake (19 km from start)

Brunswick Lake provides excellent swimming opportunities and beautiful camping on the south shore. The emergency shelter here is generally in better condition than Magnesia, and multiple water sources ensure reliable supply. This location works well for those wanting longer first days or shorter second days.

Deeks Lake (22 km from start)

Less popular for overnight camping due to its proximity to the trail's end, Deeks Lake offers peaceful settings and good fishing. This location works for three-day itineraries or emergency camping situations. The area sees day hikers from the north, so expect less solitude than other camping spots.

Bear Country Precautions

The HSCT passes through prime bear habitat. Proper food storage is mandatory and potentially life-saving. I've encountered black bears on every completion, including one memorable close call at Magnesia Meadows.

  • Use bear canisters or Ursacks for all scented items
  • Cook and eat away from sleeping areas
  • Hang food at least 4 meters high and 3 meters from tree trunks
  • Keep campsites scrupulously clean
  • Make noise while hiking, especially in dense forest

Water Management and Purification

Water availability varies dramatically by season and recent weather. Late summer can present serious water scarcity issues, especially on the southern sections before Magnesia Meadows. I always carry water purification tablets as backup to my primary filter system, and recommend starting with maximum water capacity during dry periods.

Jenny R. - Experienced backpacker

"Ran out of water before Magnesia during a hot August trip. Lesson learned—carry more than you think you need and don't count on finding water where the map shows streams. Climate change is affecting traditional water sources."

Emergency Preparedness and Risk Management

The Howe Sound Crest Trail presents numerous objective hazards that require constant vigilance and conservative decision-making. Weather changes, rockfall, wildlife encounters, and navigation errors can quickly escalate from minor inconveniences to life-threatening situations. Always travel with detailed emergency plans and communication devices.

Common Emergency Scenarios

  • Weather deterioration: Sudden fog, rain, or temperature drops are common. Know your escape routes and be prepared to retreat.
  • Navigation errors: The route isn't always obvious, especially in poor visibility. GPS devices and detailed maps are essential.
  • Injury on technical terrain: Even minor injuries become serious when evacuation is difficult. First aid skills are crucial.
  • Equipment failure: Gear malfunctions can strand you in dangerous conditions. Carry backup systems and repair materials.

Evacuation Points and Bailout Routes

Several established trails provide emergency exits from the HSCT, though none are easy or quick. The Unnecessary Mountain trail descends steeply to Lions Bay (extremely challenging with a full pack). The Lions-Binkert trail offers another escape route but requires technical scrambling. The Brunswick Mountain trail provides the most reasonable emergency exit for the middle sections.

Never underestimate the seriousness of the Howe Sound Crest Trail. I've witnessed multiple close calls and been involved in one assisted evacuation during my time on the trail. Preparation, conservative decision-making, and respect for the mountains are your best defenses against becoming another search and rescue statistic. Frequently Asked Questions

How difficult is the Howe Sound Crest Trail?

The Howe Sound Crest Trail is considered very difficult, featuring 29-32.8 kilometers of challenging terrain with over 2,500 meters of elevation gain and loss. The trail includes technical scrambling sections, exposed ridges with chains and ropes, and requires advanced hiking experience. Weather conditions can change rapidly, and the trail demands excellent physical fitness and proper gear. I recommend having completed multiple 20+ km day hikes with significant elevation before attempting the HSCT.

How long does it take to complete the Howe Sound Crest Trail?

Most hikers complete the Howe Sound Crest Trail in 2-3 days with overnight camping. Experienced trail runners can finish in 8-12 hours in a single day, but this requires exceptional fitness and preparation. For safety and enjoyment, I strongly recommend planning for at least 2 days with an overnight at Magnesia Meadows or Brunswick Lake. Three-day itineraries allow more time to enjoy the scenery and reduce physical stress on technical sections.

When is the best time to hike the Howe Sound Crest Trail?

The optimal hiking season is July through October when snow has melted and weather conditions are most stable. Late summer (August-September) offers the best combination of clear weather, minimal snow, and reliable water sources. Avoid winter and spring due to extreme avalanche danger and challenging conditions that require mountaineering experience. Early season attempts often encounter deep snow and unstable cornices that make the exposed ridges extremely dangerous.

What permits are required for the Howe Sound Crest Trail?

No permits are required for hiking the Howe Sound Crest Trail. However, wilderness camping is self-regulated and follows Leave No Trace principles. You're responsible for proper food storage using bear canisters or Ursacks, and must camp in designated areas or established sites like Magnesia Meadows and Brunswick Lake. Always inform someone of your detailed itinerary and expected return time, as the trail has a high search and rescue call volume.

Can dogs hike the Howe Sound Crest Trail?

While technically allowed on leash, dogs are strongly discouraged on the Howe Sound Crest Trail. The route includes dangerous scrambling sections, chains, ropes, and exposed ledges that are impossible for dogs to navigate safely. Many sections require technical climbing skills, making this trail unsuitable for four-legged companions. I've never seen a successful dog completion of the full trail, and several rescue calls have involved dogs that became stranded on technical sections.
