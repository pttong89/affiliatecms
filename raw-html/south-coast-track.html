Getting There and Transport Options

After completing the south coast track myself, I can tell you that getting there is half the adventure. Most hikers, including myself, choose to fly into Melaleuca via Par Avion from Cambridge Airport near Hobart. This scenic 30-minute flight costs around $300 per person and provides breathtaking aerial views of Tasmania's southwest wilderness - it's an incredible way to preview what you're about to experience on foot.

The traditional route involves flying to Melaleuca and walking east to Cockle Creek, which is what I'd strongly recommend. This approach eliminates weather-dependent pickup worries at the end of your hike. I learned from speaking with rangers that attempting the reverse route (starting at Cockle Creek) leaves you vulnerable to flight cancellations at Melaleuca, potentially extending your trip by several days.

For budget-conscious adventurers, you can drive to Cockle Creek - it's about 2.5 hours south of Hobart via the scenic Huon Valley. The final stretch on unsealed roads is rough but manageable with a standard vehicle. During peak season, several bus companies including Tasmanian Wilderness Experiences operate between Hobart and Cockle Creek, though bookings are essential.

Pro tip from my experience: If you're combining the south coast track with the Port Davey Track, consider starting at Scotts Peak Dam. This creates an epic 160km+ wilderness journey that I've witnessed other hikers complete with incredible success. The Routeburn Track in New Zealand offers similar multi-day trekking experiences for those seeking comparable adventures.

Important Transport Tip

Book your Par Avion flight at least 2 months in advance, especially for December-March dates. I nearly missed my preferred dates due to high demand, and custom flights cost significantly more.

Trail Overview and Difficulty

The south coast track is legitimately one of Australia's most challenging multi-day hikes, and having completed it, I can confirm it's significantly harder than Tasmania's famous Overland Track. This 85-kilometer wilderness journey typically takes 6-8 days and traverses some of the most remote coastline on Earth. The track follows Tasmania's wild southern shore, crossing two major mountain ranges and countless muddy plains that will test every piece of gear you're carrying.

What makes this trail so demanding isn't just the distance - it's the constantly changing terrain and unpredictable weather. During my hike, I experienced everything from 35°C heat on the button grass plains to near-freezing conditions with 60km/h winds on the Ironbound Range summit. The track alternates between pristine beaches, dense rainforest, alpine terrain, and seemingly endless muddy sections that can be knee-deep even after dry weather.

The infamous Ironbound Range crossing is the trail's crux - a 900-meter ascent followed by a treacherous descent through slippery rainforest. I spent over 11 hours on this section alone, and it was easily the most physically demanding day of hiking I've ever experienced. The descent requires constant attention to foot placement, and I used my high-quality hiking boots extensively for grip on wet rocks and exposed roots.

River crossings add another layer of complexity. The New River boat crossing requires physically dragging dinghies to the water and rowing across a 200-meter lagoon - solo hikers must make three trips total. During my crossing, strong winds made this genuinely challenging, and I understand why some hikers wait for better conditions.

Difficulty Warning

This is NOT a beginner-friendly trail. You need extensive multi-day hiking experience, excellent fitness, and complete self-sufficiency skills. Emergency evacuation from remote sections can take days.

My Experience: Day-by-Day Journey

Solo hiking the South Coast Track - real footage from the trail

My seven-day journey on the south coast track began with the scenic flight to Melaleuca, where I collected my pre-arranged fuel supplies from Par Avion's depot. The excitement was palpable as I signed the trail register and took my first steps across the airstrip, immediately encountering the track's signature feature - water! Within 50 meters, I was ankle-deep in a stream crossing, setting the tone for days of wet feet ahead.

Days 1-2: Melaleuca to Louisa River
The initial sections through Point Eric offered a false sense of security with well-maintained boardwalks and relatively easy terrain. However, crossing Red Point Hills introduced me to the track's muddy reality. Even with recent boardwalk improvements by Parks Tasmania, I found myself navigating knee-deep mud regularly. The campsite at Louisa River provided stunning views of the approaching Ironbound Range, which looked absolutely intimidating from ground level.

Day 3: The Ironbound Range Challenge
This was unquestionably the hardest single day of hiking in my life. The 900-meter ascent took three hours in deteriorating weather, with temperatures dropping to 5°C and winds reaching 40km/h at the summit. The descent through rainforest was treacherous - I spent four hours covering just 6 kilometers, using trees and rocks for stability on the slippery terrain. My lightweight trail shoes proved essential for the technical sections.

Days 4-5: Coastal Wilderness
The long beach walk on Prion Beach was a welcome relief, offering firm sand and spectacular ocean views. However, the New River boat crossing tested my upper body strength - dragging the dinghy to water level and rowing across choppy conditions solo required three separate trips. Camping at Little Deadmans Bay, I encountered the track's notorious leech population, requiring constant vigilance when outside my tent.

Days 6-7: Final Push to Cockle Creek
The Granite Beach waterfall climb and South Cape Range crossing provided the journey's final challenges. Despite covering only 20 kilometers over two days, the constant mud, river crossings, and technical terrain meant 10+ hour hiking days. Reaching Cockle Creek felt like emerging from another world - the contrast between wilderness isolation and civilization was profound.

Personal Lesson Learned

Pack gaiters and expect to get muddy - really muddy. I sank waist-deep in mud once when I didn't test the depth first. The wilderness experience is unparalleled, but respect the trail's difficulty.

Essential Gear and Preparation

After completing the south coast track, I can definitively say that gear selection can make or break your experience. The combination of mud, river crossings, unpredictable weather, and complete self-sufficiency requirements demands carefully chosen equipment. Every gram matters when you're carrying 7+ days of food through challenging terrain.

The most critical lesson I learned was waterproofing everything. Despite using quality rain gear, the persistent moisture from mud, river crossings, and high humidity meant that gear protection was essential. I stored all electronics, spare clothes, and sleeping gear in waterproof stuff sacks within my pack - this redundancy saved my trip when my pack's rain cover failed during the Ironbound Range storm.

For footwear, I found that lightweight, quick-drying options worked better than traditional heavy boots. The constant water meant feet stayed wet regardless, so breathability and fast drying became more important than waterproofing. Gaiters were absolutely essential for keeping debris out and providing some protection from leeches and scrub vegetation.

Best Time to Visit and Weather

Timing your south coast track adventure is crucial for both safety and enjoyment. The official hiking season runs from November through March, but having experienced the track in early December, I can share insights about seasonal variations that could significantly impact your journey.

December-February represents the peak season with the warmest temperatures and longest daylight hours - up to 15+ hours in midsummer. However, this period also brings the highest temperatures (up to 35°C+) and the greatest crowds. During my December hike, I encountered scorching heat on exposed sections and had to consume up to 1 liter of water per hour during the Red Point Hills crossing.

November and March offer excellent compromise conditions with milder temperatures and fewer hikers, though you'll face higher precipitation risks. March particularly appeals to experienced hikers seeking solitude, as autumn colors begin appearing and wildlife becomes more active. Rangers informed me that March often provides the most stable weather patterns, though water crossings can be more challenging after winter runoff.

The shoulder seasons (late October and early April) are possible for very experienced hikers, but weather becomes increasingly unpredictable. Snow can fall at elevation even during these months, and river levels may be dangerously high. I witnessed evidence of recent snowfall on the Ironbound Range in early December, highlighting how quickly conditions can change.

Weather patterns in Tasmania's southwest are notoriously unpredictable due to the Southern Ocean's influence. During my hike, I experienced everything from blazing sunshine to near-freezing conditions with driving rain - sometimes within the same day. The exposed coastal sections offer no shelter from sudden weather changes, making quality rain gear absolutely essential.

Seasonal Weather Summary

  • November: Cool mornings (10-15°C), warm days (20-25°C), moderate crowds, higher rain chance
  • December-January: Warmest period (15-35°C), longest days, peak crowds, lower rainfall but intense heat
  • February: Still warm (15-30°C), decreasing crowds, excellent wildlife viewing
  • March: Mild temperatures (10-25°C), fewer hikers, autumn colors, variable weather

For those seeking the ultimate challenge, consider timing your hike with Tasmania's rare Aurora Australis displays during darker months. While this requires shoulder season hiking with increased risks, the potential reward of seeing the Southern Lights from this remote wilderness could create once-in-a-lifetime memories. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the South Coast Track take to complete?

The south coast track typically takes 6-8 days for most experienced hikers, though I recommend planning for 7-8 days to allow for weather delays and rest. The 85-kilometer distance might seem manageable, but the challenging terrain, river crossings, and muddy conditions significantly slow progress. During my hike, I averaged only 1-2 kilometers per hour on the most difficult sections, particularly the Ironbound Range descent and muddy plains between river crossings. Factor in time for photography, wildlife viewing, and simply absorbing the incredible wilderness experience - rushing this trail defeats its purpose.

What fitness level is required for the South Coast Track?

This trail demands exceptional fitness and extensive hiking experience - it's significantly more challenging than Tasmania's Overland Track. You need the ability to hike 8-12 hours daily while carrying a 20kg+ pack across technical terrain. My preparation included months of training with weighted packs, focusing on leg strength for the constant mud navigation and upper body conditioning for the boat crossings. Essential prerequisites include previous multi-day hiking experience, comfort with river crossings, and mental resilience for solo wilderness travel. If you're considering lighter footwear options, ensure they still provide adequate ankle support for the technical sections.

How challenging are the river crossings on the South Coast Track?

River crossings range from simple creek hops to the famous New River boat crossing, which genuinely tests your physical capabilities. The boat crossing requires dragging heavy dinghies to the water's edge and rowing 200 meters across a lagoon - solo hikers must make three separate trips. During my crossing, 30km/h winds made rowing extremely difficult and genuinely dangerous. Most smaller creek crossings have ropes, though I found these weren't always positioned optimally. Water levels can rise rapidly after rain, making timing crucial. Always check with rangers for current conditions and consider postponing crossings during high water or severe weather.

Where can you camp on the South Coast Track?

The track features designated camping areas approximately every 10-15 kilometers, ranging from basic clearings to more established sites with composting toilets. Major campsites include Point Eric, Louisa River, Little Deadmans Bay, Surprise Bay, Granite Beach, and South Cape Rivulet. Most sites offer reasonable wind protection and nearby water sources, though water quality varies significantly. Some locations like Little Deadmans Bay have notorious leech populations requiring constant vigilance. Wild camping is permitted in emergency situations, but finding suitable flat ground can be extremely challenging given the terrain. I recommend planning your itinerary around established campsites while maintaining flexibility for weather delays.

What are the main safety risks on the South Coast Track?

The primary risks include hypothermia from sudden weather changes, injuries from slips on wet rocks during river crossings, and getting trapped by high tides on coastal sections. The Ironbound Range descent is particularly dangerous when wet - the combination of steep terrain, slippery rocks, and exposed tree roots creates serious fall risks. Remote location means emergency evacuation can take days, making self-sufficiency crucial. Wildlife encounters are generally positive, though I did encounter aggressive quolls seeking food. Navigation can be challenging in poor visibility, and some creek crossings become dangerous during high water. Essential safety gear includes satellite communication devices, comprehensive first aid supplies, and emergency shelter options.
