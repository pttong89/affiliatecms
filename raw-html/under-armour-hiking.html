Understanding Under Armour's Hiking Technology

Under Armour has revolutionized the under armour hiking experience by incorporating advanced athletic technologies into their outdoor footwear. Their signature HOVR cushioning technology, originally developed for running shoes, provides exceptional energy return and impact absorption that's particularly beneficial during long hiking sessions. This foam compound offers 30% more energy return compared to traditional EVA foam, reducing fatigue on extended trails.

The Charged Cushioning system represents another breakthrough in hiking comfort. This dual-layer midsole technology combines responsive foam in the forefoot for propulsion with softer foam in the heel for impact absorption. After testing this system on various terrain types, I've found it particularly effective on rocky trails where foot strike varies significantly. The technology adapts to different surfaces, providing stability on loose scree and comfort on hard-packed earth.

Under Armour's approach to waterproofing differs from traditional hiking brands. Instead of relying solely on Gore-Tex or similar membranes, they've developed proprietary waterproof-breathable barriers that maintain the lightweight feel essential for their athletic-inspired designs. Their Storm technology repels water while allowing moisture vapor to escape, though I've noticed it performs better in moderate conditions rather than prolonged exposure to heavy rain.

The outsole technology deserves special mention, particularly in their partnership with Vibram. The Vibram LiteBase compound used in select Under Armour hiking boots provides exceptional durability while maintaining flexibility. This material excels on mixed terrain, offering reliable grip on both wet and dry surfaces. However, the aggressive lug pattern can pick up debris in muddy conditions, requiring frequent cleaning to maintain traction effectiveness.

Trail Running Shoes vs Hiking Boots: Making the Right Choice

The decision between trail running shoes and hiking boots in the under armour hiking lineup often comes down to personal hiking style and terrain demands. Trail running shoes like the Charged Maven series excel in fast-and-light hiking scenarios where agility and speed take precedence over maximum protection. These shoes offer superior breathability and flexibility, making them ideal for well-maintained trails and day hiking adventures where quick movement is desired.

Trail Running Shoes - Best For:

  • Day hiking and fast-paced adventures
  • Well-maintained trail systems
  • Warm weather hiking
  • Light pack weights under 25 pounds
  • Approach hikes and scrambling

Hiking Boots - Best For:

  • Multi-day backpacking trips
  • Rocky, technical terrain
  • Cold weather conditions
  • Heavy pack weights over 30 pounds
  • Unstable ankle concerns

The hiking shoes Under Armour category bridges this gap effectively, offering low-cut designs with enhanced protection and support compared to traditional trail runners. For hikers with strong ankles and experience on technical terrain, trail running shoes Under Armour offers can provide an excellent balance of performance and comfort.

My Personal Experience Testing Under Armour Hiking Gear

Real-World Testing Results

Over the past two hiking seasons, I've put Under Armour hiking gear through rigorous testing across diverse conditions including desert slot canyons in Utah, alpine environments in Colorado, and temperate rainforests in the Pacific Northwest.

My first extended test of under armour hiking footwear occurred during a five-day section hike on the Colorado Trail. Wearing the HOVR Dawn WP boots with a 35-pound pack, I covered 75 miles of varied terrain including stream crossings, scree fields, and alpine meadows. The boots performed admirably for the first three days, with excellent cushioning and waterproof protection. However, by day four, I noticed increased heel slippage as the boot broke in, requiring tighter lacing that eventually caused pressure points.

Desert testing in Arches National Park revealed different strengths and weaknesses. The Charged Maven trail runners excelled in the sandy, technical terrain of Devil's Garden, providing exceptional grip on slickrock and quick sand disposal through their open mesh design. The lightweight construction reduced foot fatigue during the 12-mile circuit, but I did experience some hot spots on the arch after six hours of hiking in 85-degree temperatures. The shoes dried quickly after unexpected rain showers, a significant advantage in desert climates where weather can change rapidly.

Winter testing presented the greatest challenges for under armour hiking boots. During a winter ascent of Mount Elbert, Colorado's highest peak, I used the Charged Valsetz waterproof boots with microspikes. The boots maintained excellent traction on packed snow and provided adequate warmth down to 15 degrees Fahrenheit. However, the synthetic materials didn't breathe as well as expected, leading to moisture buildup during the ascent. The waterproof membrane worked flawlessly against external moisture but trapped internal perspiration more than I'd prefer for winter conditions.

One unexpected discovery came during a ranger-led interpretive hike in Great Smoky Mountains National Park. The ranger, an experienced hiker with 20+ years of trail experience, noted that Under Armour's athletic background shows in their attention to fit and comfort details often overlooked by traditional hiking brands. Features like seamless toe construction and heel counter padding reflect running shoe technology adapted for hiking applications. This insight helped me appreciate why these boots feel immediately comfortable compared to stiffer, traditional hiking footwear.
