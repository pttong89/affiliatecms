First Impressions and Initial Testing

When I first unboxed the Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex, I was immediately struck by their sleek, understated design. Unlike the flashy colors typical of many hiking shoes, these came in a subtle gray-black colorway that looked equally at home on mountain trails and city streets. Weighing just 13.9 ounces per shoe in my size 10.5, they felt remarkably light compared to traditional hiking boots.

The Quicklace system, while convenient for quick adjustments, immediately raised questions about long-term durability. The plastic toggle felt somewhat flimsy, and I wondered how it would hold up to hundreds of miles of aggressive hiking. The tongue's elastic pocket for storing excess lacing was a nice touch, though it took some practice to tuck the laces properly without them sliding out.

First Wear Experience: The shoes required zero break-in time. I wore them on a 5-mile technical trail on day one without any hot spots or discomfort, which was impressive for a shoe with this level of structure.

The Advanced Chassis system (ADV-C) provided immediate stability that I could feel with each step. Unlike some lightweight hiking shoes that feel flimsy underfoot, the Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex delivered a reassuring sense of support that inspired confidence on uneven terrain. The heel cup was particularly impressive, locking my foot in place without feeling restrictive.

Performance Analysis: Traction, Comfort & Waterproofing

Traction: Contagrip Delivers on Mixed Terrain

The Contagrip MA outsole proved to be one of the standout features of the Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex. During my testing across various terrains—from loose scree in Colorado to muddy Pacific Northwest trails—the aggressive chevron lug pattern provided exceptional grip. The 4.5mm lug depth offered excellent bite on soft surfaces while maintaining enough contact area for confidence on rock slabs.

What impressed me most was the outsole's performance on wet rocks and roots. During a particularly challenging descent in Olympic National Park, where conditions were wet and slippery, the Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex maintained grip where other hikers in my group were sliding. The rubber compound strikes an excellent balance between stickiness and durability, though I did notice some wear on the toe and heel after 300+ miles.

Comfort and Fit: Narrow but Accommodating

The comfort level of these shoes exceeded my expectations, particularly given their technical focus. The OrthoLite insole provided adequate cushioning for day hikes up to 15 miles, though I found myself wanting more underfoot padding on longer backpacking trips. The shoe runs narrow, which worked well for my medium-width feet, but hikers with wider feet should definitely try before buying or consider the wide version.

The Active Support wings, integrated into the lacing system, created a secure midfoot lock-down that prevented any heel slippage during descents. However, the higher ankle cuff—a departure from traditional low-cut hiking shoes—occasionally rubbed against my ankle bone when wearing low-cut socks. Switching to crew-height hiking socks eliminated this issue entirely.

Waterproofing: Gore-Tex Performance

The Gore-Tex membrane in the Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex proved highly effective in keeping my feet dry during stream crossings, puddle splashing, and extended periods in wet grass. The waterproof height of approximately 3.25 inches provided adequate protection for typical hiking scenarios. However, like all waterproof shoes, breathability was compromised compared to non-waterproof alternatives.

During summer hikes in warm conditions, I noticed increased moisture retention inside the shoe, particularly during high-exertion climbs. The synthetic upper materials, while durable, don't breathe as well as mesh alternatives. For summer hiking in dry conditions, you might consider the non-Gore-Tex version for better ventilation, though I found the X Ultra 4 Mid Gore-Tex hiking boots offered better ankle protection for rocky terrain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex shoes true to size?

Yes, the Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex runs true to size for most hikers. However, the shoe has a narrow fit that may feel snug for those with wide feet. I recommend trying them on with hiking socks or considering the wide version if available. The toe box provides adequate room for toe movement during descents, and the 11mm heel-to-toe drop accommodates most foot shapes. For hikers coming from brands like Merrell or Keen, which typically run wider, you might initially notice the narrower European fit.

How does the durability compare to other hiking shoes?

The Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex offers moderate durability typical of lightweight hiking shoes. Expect 400-600 miles of optimal performance before significant wear becomes apparent. This falls between ultralight trail runners (200-400 miles) and traditional leather hiking boots (800-1200 miles). The trade-off for the 13.9-ounce weight is reduced longevity compared to heavier alternatives. Quality control issues have been reported, but proper care and rotation with other footwear can extend lifespan. The X Ultra 4 Mid Gore-Tex variant offers slightly better durability with ankle protection.

Can these shoes handle winter hiking conditions?

The Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex is not ideal for winter hiking conditions. While the Gore-Tex membrane provides waterproofing, the shoe lacks insulation for cold temperatures and the outsole compound becomes significantly stiffer in freezing conditions, reducing traction. The relatively narrow toe box also limits compatibility with thick wool socks or toe warmers. For winter hiking, consider insulated boots or the mid-height version for better ankle protection. However, they work well for shoulder season hiking and early winter conditions above freezing. Microspikes can be used but the narrow fit may feel restrictive with traction devices attached.

What's the difference between men's and women's versions?

The women's Salomon X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex features a narrower heel and slightly different last shape to accommodate women's foot anatomy. The colorway options differ, with women's versions often featuring more vibrant color combinations. Performance characteristics, materials, and technologies remain identical between versions. Women with wider feet might find the men's version more comfortable, while men with narrow feet could consider women's sizing for a more precise fit. The women's X Ultra 4 Mid Gore-Tex hiking boots offer the same anatomical considerations in the mid-height version.

How do they compare to the X Ultra 3 model?

The X Ultra 4 represents a significant improvement over the X Ultra 3 in several key areas. The newer model is 20-40 grams lighter per pair while maintaining similar support levels. The updated Advanced Chassis (ADV-C) provides better balance between flexibility and stability. The Active Support wings are new to the 4th generation, offering improved midfoot lockdown. Outsole durability has been enhanced, though lug wear patterns remain similar. The heel cup design provides better lockdown than the X Ultra 3. If you're choosing between models, the X Ultra 4 offers better overall performance, though the X Ultra 3 Mid Gore-Tex may still be available at discounted prices.

Is the Quicklace system reliable for long hikes?

The Quicklace system offers convenience but has reliability concerns based on user feedback and my experience. While the system works well for quick adjustments and eliminates pressure points from traditional lacing, the plastic toggle mechanism can fail unexpectedly. I carry backup laces as a precaution during longer hikes. The system tends to loosen gradually during extended hiking, requiring periodic re-tightening. The elastic pocket for storing excess lacing works well when properly used. For day hikes, the system is generally reliable, but for multi-day backpacking trips, consider carrying traditional laces as backup. Some hikers prefer models with standard lacing, which explains the popularity of alternatives like the X Ultra 4 Gore-Tex walking shoes with conventional lacing systems.
