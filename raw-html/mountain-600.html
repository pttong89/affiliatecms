Detailed Performance Analysis

After extensive field testing and comparing data from independent lab tests, I can provide specific insights into how the mountain 600 performs across key metrics that matter to serious hikers.

Cushioning and Comfort

The RunRepeat lab testing revealed a shock absorption reading of 99 SA, which places the Mountain 600 above the average hiking boot. In practical terms, this translates to noticeable impact protection during long descents. During my 15-mile descent from Camp Muir on Mount Rainier, I experienced significantly less heel soreness compared to my previous boots.

The 36.8mm heel stack height provides ample cushioning without feeling disconnected from the trail. The 13.8mm drop feels natural for hikers accustomed to traditional footwear, though those coming from minimal shoes might need time to adjust.

Traction and Stability

The 4mm lug depth proves adequate for most hiking scenarios, though I've found it occasionally lacking on technical rock faces. The Vibram Megagrip compound excels on wet surfaces – something I've tested extensively during Washington's notoriously rainy hiking season.

The torsional rigidity rating of 5 out of 5 means these boots resist unwanted foot movement on uneven terrain. This translates to increased confidence when carrying heavy packs or navigating challenging approaches. The women's mountain 600 version maintains identical stability characteristics with gender-specific fit modifications.

Durability Assessment

The full-grain leather upper has proven remarkably resistant to abrasion. After two years of regular use, including bushwhacking through dense Pacific Northwest undergrowth, the leather shows typical wear patterns but no structural damage. The toe cap extension provides excellent protection against rock strikes.

One area of concern is the heel padding, which scored only 2 out of 5 in durability testing. While this hasn't affected my boots' performance, it's something to monitor during extended use. The Vibram outsole, despite slightly below-average thickness at 2.1mm, has shown impressive longevity in my experience.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Danner Mountain 600 boots true to size?

Based on my experience and extensive user feedback, the Mountain 600 fits true to size for most hikers. However, Danner officially recommends sizing down a half size for optimal fit. I personally ordered my regular size (10.5) and found the fit perfect with room for thick hiking socks. The boots accommodate average to slightly wide feet well, but those with particularly narrow or wide feet should consider trying them on first. The mountain 600 also comes in wide and extra-wide options for better accommodation. Remember that proper fit is crucial for hiking performance and comfort, so consider your typical sock thickness and any foot swelling during long hikes when choosing your size.

How long do Danner Mountain 600 boots last?

With proper care, expect 400-600 miles from the original outsole, though this varies significantly based on terrain and hiking style. My boots have over 500 miles and show minimal sole wear. The leather upper typically outlasts the sole by several years with regular conditioning. Danner offers resoling services for $100-140, and full rebuilds for $280, making these boots a long-term investment. The women's Mountain 600 shows similar longevity patterns. Professional guides report getting 2-3 years of heavy use before needing resoling. Proper storage away from heat and regular leather conditioning significantly extends lifespan. The recraftable design means these boots can potentially last decades with proper maintenance and periodic rebuilding.

Can you hike long distances in Mountain 600 boots?

Absolutely. The Mountain 600 excels at long-distance hiking thanks to its Vibram SPE midsole technology and excellent cushioning system. I've completed multiple 15+ mile days and a 50-mile section of the Wonderland Trail in these boots with minimal foot fatigue. The key is the balance between support and comfort that prevents the usual heel soreness associated with long days. Many thru-hikers and section hikers choose the mountain 600 for segments requiring durability and foot protection. However, weight-conscious ultralight hikers might prefer lighter alternatives for maximum distance hiking. The boots handle varied terrain exceptionally well, from rocky approaches to soft forest trails. Proper sock selection and foot care remain important for any long-distance hiking, but the Mountain 600's comfort platform provides an excellent foundation.

Are Mountain 600 boots good for wide feet?

The standard Mountain 600 accommodates average to slightly wide feet comfortably, measuring 94.3mm at the widest point according to lab testing. Danner also offers wide and extra-wide versions for those needing additional room. The toe box width of 72.3mm provides adequate space for most foot shapes, though the toe box height is below average at 22.9mm. This might cause issues for hikers with high-volume feet or those prone to toe problems on descents. I recommend trying them on if you have particularly wide feet, as the leather will stretch slightly with use but won't dramatically change the boot's dimensions. The women's hiking boots feature gender-specific sizing that often works better for narrower feet. Many users report excellent comfort even with wider feet, but individual fit varies significantly.

What's the difference between Mountain 600 and Mountain 600 Leaf?

The primary difference lies in construction philosophy and environmental impact. The Mountain 600 Leaf represents Danner's first recraftable Mountain 600 design, specifically engineered for multiple resoling and rebuilding cycles. Both boots share identical performance characteristics, waterproofing, and comfort features. The Leaf version uses more sustainable materials where possible and comes with enhanced documentation for the recrafting process. From a hiking performance standpoint, there's no difference – both feature the same Vibram SPE midsole and Megagrip outsole technology. The Leaf typically costs slightly more ($20-30) but offers better long-term value through the recrafting program. Environmental consciousness often drives the choice, as the Leaf design significantly reduces waste through multiple lifecycles. Both versions of the mountain 600 maintain Danner's reputation for quality and durability, so the choice depends on your environmental priorities and long-term usage plans.

Do Mountain 600 boots need waterproofing treatment?

The Mountain 600 comes with Danner Dry waterproofing and requires minimal additional treatment initially. However, the leather upper benefits from regular conditioning to maintain water resistance and prevent cracking. I treat my boots every 30-40 hiking days with a quality leather conditioner, focusing on seam areas and high-flex zones. The waterproof membrane remains effective regardless of leather treatment, but conditioned leather sheds water better and lasts longer. Avoid over-treating, as excessive conditioning can make leather too soft and reduce support. Nikwax, Obenauf's, or similar products work well – avoid petroleum-based treatments that can damage the leather over time. In extremely wet conditions, I occasionally apply DWR treatment to the exterior, though it's not necessary for normal use. The Mountain 600's waterproofing system is robust enough for most hiking scenarios with minimal maintenance.
