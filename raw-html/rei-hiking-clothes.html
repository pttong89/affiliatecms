Understanding the Layering System

The layering system is the foundation of effective outdoor clothing. I learned this principle during a challenging backpacking trip in the North Cascades, where temperatures swung from 75°F during the day to 35°F at night. Without proper layering, I would have been either overheating or freezing throughout the journey.

Base Layer

Your next-to-skin layer manages moisture and regulates temperature. Choose merino wool for natural odor resistance or synthetic materials for quick-drying performance.

Best REI Options:
  • • Merino 185 Long-Sleeve Base Layer
  • • Active Pursuits T-Shirt
  • • Sahara Shade Hoodie

Mid Layer

Provides insulation and traps warm air. Fleece and down pieces are ideal for this layer, offering warmth without excessive bulk.

Best REI Options:
  • • Trailmade Fleece Jacket
  • • 650 Down Jacket
  • • Trailmade Insulated Hoodie

Outer Layer

Protects against wind, rain, and external elements. Look for waterproof-breathable fabrics that keep you dry while allowing moisture to escape.

Best REI Options:
  • • Rainier Rain Jacket
  • • Trailmade Rain Jacket
  • • Rainier Long Line Rain Jacket

Seasonal Hiking Clothes Guide

One of the biggest lessons I've learned over the years is that there's no such thing as bad weather, only inappropriate clothing. Each season brings unique challenges that require specific gear adaptations. Here's how I modify my rei hiking clothes setup throughout the year.

Spring Hiking (March-May)

Spring hiking means unpredictable weather. During a memorable April hike in the Columbia River Gorge, I experienced everything from snow flurries to blazing sunshine in a single day.

Essential Spring Layers:

  • • Base layer: Merino wool long-sleeve
  • • Mid layer: Lightweight fleece
  • • Outer layer: Waterproof rain jacket
  • • Bottom: Quick-dry pants with rain pants backup
  • • Accessories: Beanie and lightweight gloves

Summer Hiking (June-August)

Summer hiking is all about sun protection and cooling. My worst hiking experience involved severe sunburn during a Zion National Park hike because I underestimated UV exposure at high altitude.

Essential Summer Gear:

  • • Base layer: UPF 50+ long-sleeve hoodie
  • • Bottom: Lightweight hiking pants or shorts
  • • Hat: Wide-brimmed sun hat
  • • Emergency: Lightweight rain jacket
  • • Cooling: Moisture-wicking bandana

Fall Hiking (September-November)

Fall offers some of the best hiking weather, but temperature swings can be dramatic. During an October backpacking trip in the Cascades, morning temperatures were 28°F while afternoon highs reached 68°F.

Essential Fall Layers:

  • • Base layer: Midweight merino wool
  • • Mid layer: Insulated vest or jacket
  • • Outer layer: Wind-resistant softshell
  • • Bottom: Thermal underwear + hiking pants
  • • Accessories: Warm hat and gloves

Winter Hiking (December-February)

Winter hiking demands respect and proper preparation. A close call during a Mount Hood winter climb taught me never to compromise on insulation and weather protection.

Essential Winter System:

  • • Base layer: Heavyweight thermal system
  • • Mid layer: Down or synthetic insulation
  • • Outer layer: Waterproof hardshell jacket
  • • Bottom: Thermal base + softshell pants
  • • Extremities: Insulated gloves, warm hat, gaiters

Critical Safety Note

Weather conditions can change rapidly in the mountains. Always carry extra layers regardless of the forecast. During a "perfect weather" summer day on Mount Whitney, unexpected afternoon thunderstorms dropped temperatures 40 degrees and brought hail. My extra fleece layer literally saved me from hypothermia.

The key to seasonal success is adaptability. I've learned to check not just the weather forecast, but also elevation profiles, regional climate patterns, and historical weather data for my hiking destinations. This preparation has allowed me to stay comfortable and safe across thousands of miles and countless adventures.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes REI hiking clothes better than regular athletic wear?

REI hiking clothes are specifically designed for outdoor environments and extended wear. Unlike regular athletic wear, they feature UV protection, abrasion-resistant materials, and specialized fits for pack-wearing. The fabric treatments repel water and resist odors better than typical gym clothes. After testing both extensively, I've found that REI clothes maintain their performance characteristics longer and provide better protection against the elements. The thoughtful design details like reinforced stress points and strategic ventilation make a significant difference during long hiking days.

How do REI hiking clothes fit compared to regular clothing sizes?

REI clothes generally run true to size, but with a slightly relaxed fit designed for layering and movement. I typically wear the same size in REI as I do in regular brands, but I recommend checking their detailed size charts since fit varies by product line. The Sahara line tends to be more generous, while the Trailmade line is more fitted. REI's generous return policy makes it easy to exchange sizes, and their customer service team can provide specific fit guidance based on your measurements and intended use.

Are REI brand clothes worth the price compared to budget alternatives?

In my experience, REI clothes offer excellent value for their price point, sitting between budget outdoor wear and premium brands like Patagonia or Arc'teryx. The quality is consistently high, and the lifetime satisfaction guarantee provides peace of mind. While you can find cheaper alternatives, I've found that REI clothes last longer and perform better in challenging conditions. For serious hikers who are on the trail regularly, the durability and performance justify the cost. However, casual day hikers might find budget alternatives sufficient for their needs.

What's the best way to care for and maintain REI hiking clothes?

Proper care significantly extends the life of hiking clothes. I wash REI garments in cold water with technical fabric detergent (like Nikwax or Grangers) and avoid fabric softeners which can damage moisture-wicking properties. Air drying is preferred, but low heat tumble drying is usually safe. For DWR-treated items like rain jackets, periodic treatment renewal with spray-on DWR restores water repellency. I've found that following care instructions religiously has kept some pieces performing like new after 5+ years of regular use.

Can REI hiking clothes work for other outdoor activities besides hiking?

Absolutely! REI hiking clothes are incredibly versatile for various outdoor activities. I regularly use my hiking pants for camping, fishing, and even casual wear. The moisture-wicking properties make them excellent for cycling and climbing, while the sun protection is valuable for any outdoor sport. The durable construction handles bushwhacking, rock scrambling, and other rough activities well. Many pieces transition nicely from trail to town, making them excellent travel clothes for outdoor-focused trips.

How often should I replace my REI hiking clothes?

The replacement timeline depends on usage frequency and care. My most-used REI base layers last 2-3 years with weekly use, while my rain jacket is still going strong after 6 years. Signs it's time to replace include loss of moisture-wicking properties, persistent odors that won't wash out, fabric thinning, or failure of technical features like DWR coating that can't be renewed. I typically replace items when they stop performing their intended function rather than based on appearance alone. REI's satisfaction guarantee means you can return items that fail prematurely.
