Why Choose a Tankless Water Heater for RV

When I first started researching tankless water heater for RV options, I was skeptical about replacing my reliable 6-gallon traditional tank. However, after experiencing the benefits firsthand during a month-long boondocking adventure in Utah's Zion National Park, I became a true believer in on-demand water heating technology.

The primary advantage of an RV tankless water heater is unlimited hot water supply. Traditional tank heaters limit you to 6-16 gallons before requiring a 20-30 minute recovery time. With a tankless system, you can enjoy continuous hot showers, perfect for families or couples who prefer back-to-back showers. During my stay at Glacier National Park, this feature proved invaluable when hosting visiting family members in our 32-foot travel trailer.

Energy efficiency represents another compelling reason to upgrade to a tankless water heater for RV. These units only consume propane when hot water flows, eliminating standby heat loss that plagues traditional tank systems. My propane consumption decreased by approximately 30% after switching to a Girard tankless unit, extending my boondocking capabilities significantly. This efficiency boost translates to fewer propane refills and lower operating costs over time.

Space optimization makes tankless systems particularly attractive for smaller RVs and campervans. Most campervan water heater installations benefit from the compact footprint of tankless units, which occupy roughly half the space of traditional tanks. This freed-up space can accommodate additional storage, larger fresh water tanks, or other essential equipment. For detailed comparisons, check out our comprehensive guide on camper water heaters.

Weight reduction is another often-overlooked benefit. A traditional 6-gallon water heater with water weighs approximately 70 pounds, while most tankless units weigh under 25 pounds. This weight savings improves fuel economy and allows for additional cargo capacity â€“ crucial considerations for any serious RVer planning extended adventures.

Pros and Cons of RV Tankless Water Heaters

Understanding the complete picture of tankless water heater for RV ownership requires honest evaluation of both advantages and limitations. After three years of real-world experience across diverse camping conditions, I've compiled this comprehensive analysis.

Advantages

  • Unlimited Hot Water: Continuous supply eliminates waiting between showers
  • Energy Efficiency: 30% reduction in propane consumption vs. tank systems
  • Space Savings: 50% smaller footprint enables storage optimization
  • Weight Reduction: 45 pounds lighter than traditional 6-gallon systems
  • Freeze Protection: Advanced systems prevent winter damage automatically
  • Longer Lifespan: 15-20 year expectancy vs. 8-10 for tank systems

Disadvantages

  • Higher Initial Cost: $400-600 vs. $200-300 for tank systems
  • Propane Only: Cannot utilize campground electricity for heating
  • Water Waste: 10-15 seconds delay before hot water delivery
  • Complex Installation: Requires electrical modifications and professional service
  • Flow Rate Requirements: Minimum 0.5 GPM needed for activation
  • Winterization Complexity: Requires antifreeze circulation vs. simple draining

The decision to upgrade depends heavily on your camping style and priorities. Full-time RVers and boondockers benefit most from the efficiency gains and unlimited hot water supply. Weekend warriors using campgrounds with electrical hookups might prefer the simplicity and dual-fuel capability of traditional 6-gallon RV water heater systems.

Temperature consistency represents a crucial consideration often overlooked in marketing materials. While tankless systems provide endless hot water, achieving stable temperatures requires proper water flow rates and system calibration. Low-flow faucets or partially closed valves can cause temperature fluctuations that frustrate users accustomed to tank system stability.

For those considering electric water heater for RV alternatives, note that electric tankless options require 220V power and draw 75+ amps â€“ exceeding most RV electrical system capabilities. This limitation makes propane tankless systems the only viable on-demand option for RV applications.

Installation and Maintenance Guide

Installing a tankless water heater for RV requires careful planning and often professional expertise. While some experienced DIYers can handle the installation, the complexity of electrical integration and propane system modifications makes professional installation advisable for most RVers.

Pre-installation preparation begins with measuring the existing water heater compartment and verifying compatibility with your chosen tankless unit. Most retrofit installations require removing the old tank system and potentially modifying the external access door. Document existing plumbing connections with photographs before beginning any work â€“ this reference proves invaluable during reassembly.

Safety Warning

Tankless water heater installation involves propane system modifications and electrical work. Improper installation can result in gas leaks, electrical fires, or carbon monoxide exposure. Always use certified RV technicians for installation unless you possess extensive experience with RV propane and electrical systems.

The installation process typically requires four to six hours for professional completion. Key steps include removing the existing water heater, installing the tankless unit mounting bracket, connecting water lines using flexible stainless steel connectors, running 12V power from the battery compartment, connecting the propane supply line, and installing the external control panel.

Electrical requirements vary by manufacturer but typically include a dedicated 12V circuit with 15-20 amp capacity. The power connection supplies ignition control, circulation pump operation, and freeze protection systems. Some units also require 120V AC power for enhanced features, complicating the electrical integration process.

Maintenance schedules depend primarily on water quality and usage frequency. Hard water areas require descaling every 3-6 months using white vinegar or commercial descaling solutions. The process involves circulating the cleaning solution through the heat exchanger for 30-45 minutes, followed by thorough flushing with fresh water.

Winterization procedures differ significantly from traditional tank systems. Rather than simple drainage, tankless systems require antifreeze circulation throughout the entire heat exchanger assembly. This process typically requires 2-3 gallons of RV antifreeze and specialized winterization procedures outlined in the manufacturer's manual.

Annual professional inspections should include combustion chamber cleaning, burner adjustment, electrical connection verification, and propane system leak testing. These preventive measures ensure safe operation and maximize system lifespan. For comprehensive maintenance guidance, review our RV water heater replacement resources.

FAQ About RV Tankless Water Heaters

Can I install a tankless water heater myself?

While experienced DIYers with RV modification experience can potentially handle installation, I strongly recommend professional installation for most RVers. The process involves propane system modifications, 12V electrical integration, and precise calibration for optimal performance. Improper installation can create safety hazards including gas leaks and electrical fires. Professional installation typically costs $200-400 but ensures proper operation and maintains warranty coverage. Most RV service centers offer installation services with parts and labor warranties.

How much propane does a tankless water heater use?

Propane consumption varies by usage patterns and unit size, but most RV tankless systems consume 0.5-1.0 gallons per hour during operation. Unlike traditional tank systems that maintain pilot lights, tankless units only consume propane when hot water flows. During my testing, daily consumption averaged 0.2-0.3 gallons for typical two-person usage including showers, dishwashing, and hand washing. This represents approximately 30% reduction compared to traditional tank systems that maintain standby temperatures 24/7. High-demand periods like simultaneous shower and dishwashing can double consumption temporarily.

What size tankless water heater do I need for my RV?

Sizing depends on simultaneous usage requirements and your RV's hot water demand patterns. For RVs under 25 feet with 1-2 occupants, units rated 40,000-50,000 BTU provide adequate performance for sequential usage. Larger RVs (25-40 feet) with 2-4 occupants should consider 55,000-65,000 BTU systems for simultaneous shower and kitchen usage. Flow rate capacity is equally important â€“ ensure your selected unit provides minimum 2.0 GPM for comfortable shower pressure. Consider your typical usage patterns: boondockers prioritizing efficiency might choose smaller units, while those preferring luxury features should select higher-capacity systems.

Do tankless water heaters work in cold weather?

Modern RV tankless systems include freeze protection features and perform well in cold weather with proper preparation. During winter camping in sub-freezing temperatures, the freeze protection system circulates antifreeze solution through the heat exchanger preventing damage. However, extremely cold inlet water (below 40Â°F) can reduce heating capacity and extend hot water delivery time. I've successfully operated my Girard system in temperatures as low as -10Â°F with no issues. Key considerations include ensuring adequate propane pressure in cold weather, maintaining battery voltage for electrical systems, and following manufacturer winterization procedures when temperatures drop below freezing.

Can I use campground electricity with a tankless water heater?

Most RV tankless water heaters operate exclusively on propane for heating, though they require 12V DC power for ignition and control systems. Unlike traditional electric water heater for RV options that offer dual-fuel capability, tankless systems cannot utilize campground 120V electricity for water heating. This limitation means continued propane consumption even when connected to electrical hookups. However, the 12V control system can operate from your RV's converter while connected to shore power, reducing battery drain. Some high-end units offer 120V AC operation but require 220V service and draw excessive amperage exceeding typical RV electrical system capabilities.

How long do RV tankless water heaters last?

Quality RV tankless water heaters typically last 15-20 years with proper maintenance, significantly longer than traditional tank systems averaging 8-10 years. The absence of storage tanks eliminates corrosion issues that limit tank system lifespan. However, longevity depends heavily on water quality, maintenance frequency, and usage patterns. Hard water areas require more frequent descaling to prevent mineral buildup in heat exchangers. Annual professional maintenance, regular descaling, and proper winterization procedures maximize system lifespan. My three-year-old Girard unit shows no signs of degradation despite full-time usage across diverse water conditions. Quality brands offer 3-5 year warranties covering major components and heat exchangers.
